01. THE MECHANICS

The Habit Loop

According to Charles Duhigg, every habit is driven by a 3-step loop. Analyze the components below. Can you map one of your own daily habits (good or bad) onto this structure?

1. The Cue

The trigger that initiates the behavior. It can be a specific time, a location, an emotional state, or preceding action. (e.g., Feeling stressed before a major presentation).

2. The Routine

The actual behavior or action you perform in response to the cue. This is the habit itself. (e.g., Mindlessly scrolling social media).

3. The Reward

The benefit you gain from the behavior. This satisfies the craving and tells the basal ganglia to remember this loop for the future. (e.g., A temporary dopamine hit and distraction).

Your Turn:

Identify a bad habit. What is the actual cue? To break the habit, you must keep the cue and the reward, but swap the routine. Discuss with your partner.

adjective

(Of a habit or belief) firmly fixed or established; difficult to change. "A deeply ingrained morning routine."

To cause an event, reaction, or habit to start functioning. "Stress usually triggers his smoking habit."
adjective

The process where small, consistent actions multiply and produce massive results over a long period.
verb
To override
To use your conscious willpower to stop an automatic process or impulse. "He had to actively override the urge to check his phone."
noun / verb
Plateau
A state of little or no change following a period of activity or progress. "After a month of studying, she hit a plateau."
adjective
Detrimental
Tending to cause harm. "Lack of sleep is highly detrimental to building new neural pathways."
noun
Relapse
A deterioration in behavior after a temporary improvement; falling back into bad habits.
03. PSYCHOLOGICAL BRIEF

The Anatomy of a Habit

When a person attempts to change their life trajectory, learn a language, or adopt a healthier lifestyle, they often struggle with their own neurobiology. Habits are housed deep within the basal ganglia, a primitive part of the brain that stores automatic routines to save cognitive energy.

Because these routines are deeply ingrained, mere motivation is rarely enough to change them. An expert's precise focus during complex deep work is a result of compounding habits built over thousands of hours of repetition, where conscious effort has been fully automated.

Understanding neuroplasticity gives us hope: the brain is not static. By identifying the cues that trigger a detrimental routine, individuals can consciously override the default pathway and forge new connections.

06. COMPREHENSION

Fact vs. Fiction

1. The brain's reward-based learning process works exactly the same way for eating food as it does for smoking.
TRUE. Brewer states that trigger, behavior, and reward (the loop) apply universally to basic survival (food) and destructive habits (smoking).
2. The prefrontal cortex is the oldest and most reliable part of the brain when we are stressed.
FALSE. It is the youngest part of the brain and the *first* to go offline when we are stressed, leaving the older basal ganglia in charge.
3. Mindfulness training is twice as effective as standard therapy for quitting smoking.
TRUE. He mentions a study where mindfulness training was twice as good as gold standard therapy for quitting smoking.
07. SYSTEM RULES

The Future Continuous

To talk about habit formation goals, we must look into the future. The Future Continuous describes an action that will be in progress at a specific time in the future, or expected future routines.

Future Continuous

will be + V-ing (e.g., will be sleeping, will be studying)
"Don't call me at 6 AM tomorrow. I will be sleeping." (Action in progress).
"This time next month, I will be waking up without an alarm." (Expected routine).
08. SYSTEM RULES

The Future Perfect

We use this to say that an action will be completed before a specific time or another action in the future. It is almost always used with time expressions like "by..." or "before...".

Future Perfect

will have + V3 (e.g., will have finished, will have built)
"By December, I will have automated my morning routine."
"By the time we arrive, the lecture will have started."

10. PHONETICS

The Flow of Fluency

Native speakers rarely pronounce every syllable of complex future tenses. In the Future Perfect, the auxiliary verbs contract heavily.

will have + V3 → /wɪləv/ + V3

Listen and repeat: "By tomorrow, I /wɪləv/ finished the project prep."
