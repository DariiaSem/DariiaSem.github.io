Task 3: Vocabulary

Words of the Chase

Click to expand: Open each box below to reveal the definitions of these advanced words related to hunting and hysteria.

Fugitive (noun)
A person who has escaped from a place or is hiding to avoid arrest or persecution by the authorities.
Track down (phrasal verb)
To locate someone or something after a long and thorough search.
Persuade (verb)
To convince someone to do or believe something through reasoning, argument, or pressure.
Foe (noun)
An enemy or an opponent in a conflict.
Endeavor (noun/verb)
An attempt to achieve a difficult goal, or a dangerous and serious project.
Hysteria (noun)
Exaggerated or uncontrollable emotion or excitement, especially among a large group of people.

Task 5: Reading

The Hunters of Salem

Read this short historical text about the events of 1692.

In the cold winter of 1692, the small town of Salem, Massachusetts descended into absolute hysteria. It began when a group of young girls claimed that invisible spirits were attacking them. The doctors could not explain it, so they blamed witchcraft.

Quickly, the town magistrates acted like ruthless bounty hunters. They began to track down any women (and men) who were considered unusual or who had many foes in the village. If you were accused, the only way to survive was to confess and persuade the court that you were forced to do magic by someone else.

Many innocent people became fugitives, running into the freezing forests to escape the hunters. Surviving the winter outside was a dangerous endeavor, but staying in Salem was a death sentence.

Task 7: Grammar Theory

Narrative Tenses: The Scene vs The Action

When we tell a story, we use different past tenses to separate the "background picture" from the "main actions".

Tense Usage in Storytelling Example
Past Continuous
(was/were + V-ing)		 The Background / The Interrupted Action. Describes what was happening around a certain time, setting the scene. The wind was howling, and the girls were screaming...
Past Simple
(V-ed / Irregular)		 The Main Event / The Interruption. Describes the sequence of events moving forward in time. ...when suddenly, the magistrate kicked the door open and arrested her.

Task 11: Advanced Grammar

The "Past of the Past"

To explain why something happened, or to jump backwards in our timeline, we use Perfect tenses.

Past Perfect Simple

Describes completed actions which took place before the main past action.

had + V3
The fugitive had escaped...
EARLIER PAST
Past Simple
...before the hunters arrived.
MAIN PAST

Past Perfect Continuous

Describes longer continuous actions that happened before the main past action, often explaining a visible condition.

had been + V-ing
She had been hiding for a week...
ONGOING PAST
Past Simple
...so she looked terrible when they found her.
MAIN PAST

Task 13: Pronunciation

Suspenseful Intonation

Based on Teaching American English Pronunciation, when telling a story, native speakers use pitch to build suspense and separate background events from sudden actions.

When an action is ongoing (Continuous), keep your voice steady and slightly raised (↗).
When the sudden interruption happens (Simple), drop your pitch and stress the verb heavily (↘).

Practice: Listen & Repeat:

"We were searching the forest (↗)... when suddenly she APPEARED (↘)!"

"I quickly realized (↗)... that he had already ESCAPED (↘)."

Task 14: Speaking

The Confrontation in the Woods

Act out this scene set in 1692. A Hunter has finally caught up with an accused Fugitive in the snowy forest. Focus on using correct narrative tenses!

The Hunter (You):
"It's over! I know you had skipped town before the judges even signed the papers."
↓
The Accused (Teacher):
"You're making a mistake! While I was hiding in the cabin, someone else stole the horse!"
↓
The Hunter (You):
[Prompt: Accuse them using Past Perfect Continuous. E.g., "Liar! You had been planning this escape for weeks!"]
↓
The Accused (Teacher):
"I promise, I had never seen that horse before yesterday. If you turn me in, I will hang."
↓
The Hunter (You):
[Prompt: Finish the scene. State what you did using a Past Simple sequence (e.g., "I heard enough, I grabbed her, and we left.")]
