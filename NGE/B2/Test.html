Task 4 · Reading

The Wiseman Luck Experiment

Read the short article about a psychological study on luck. Pay attention to how the subjects behave.

Can you study luck scientifically?

Dr. Richard Wiseman, a psychologist, decided to test if luck is pure chance or if it's related to human psychology. He gathered 400 volunteers who considered themselves either exceptionally lucky or exceptionally unlucky.

In one experiment, he gave both groups a newspaper and asked them to count the photographs inside. The "unlucky" people took about two minutes to count the photographs. The "lucky" people took only seconds. Why?

Because on the second page, written in massive text, was a message: "Stop counting. There are 43 photographs in this newspaper." The unlucky people completely missed it because they were too focused on the specific task of counting. The lucky people, however, were more relaxed and open to their surroundings, allowing them to notice opportunities.

Wiseman concluded that lucky people generate their own good fortune. They are skilled at creating and noticing chance opportunities, making lucky decisions by listening to their intuition, and adopting a resilient attitude that transforms bad luck into good.

Task 6 · Grammar Theory

First vs. Second Conditional

When we talk about luck, we often imagine different situations. Let's look at how to form conditions.

Real / Possible Future
First Conditional
Unreal / Imaginary Present or Future
Second Conditional
Conditional Structure Usage & Example
First Conditional If + Present Simple,
... will + verb		 Used for real, possible situations in the future.

"If I find a lucky penny, I will keep it." (It's possible I will find one today).
Second Conditional If + Past Simple,
... would + verb		 Used for unreal, imaginary, or highly unlikely situations in the present/future.

"If I won the lottery, I would buy an island." (I probably won't win).

Note: In the Second Conditional, we often use "were" instead of "was" for all subjects (e.g., If I were you, I wouldn't walk under that ladder.)

Task 11 · Pronunciation

Fast Speech & Contractions

In the Second Conditional, native speakers use heavy contractions. "Would" becomes 'd, and "would you" merges together.

1. I would → I'd (/aɪd/)

"If I had the money, I'd buy it."

Practice: I'd go / We'd see / They'd know / She'd win

2. Would you → "Wouldja" (/ˈwʊdʒə/)

"What would you do?" sounds like "What wouldja do?"

🗣️ Read this dialogue aloud rapidly with your partner:

A: What wouldja do if a black cat crossed your path?
B: I'd probably ignore it. What wouldja do?
A: I'd turn around and walk the other way!
