01. THE MECHANICS

The Fraud Triangle

In criminology, investigators look for three elements before naming a suspect. Analyze the components below. Discuss a famous white-collar crime and map it to this structure.

1. Motive (Pressure)

The reason why a person commits the crime. Usually financial pressure, revenge, or psychological compulsion.

2. Opportunity

The ability to execute the crime without being caught immediately. Often created by weak security systems or blind trust.

3. Rationalization

The psychological justification. "They owe me this money," or "I'm just borrowing it temporarily."

03. CASE FILE

The Gardner Museum Heist

In the early hours of March 18, 1990, the Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum in Boston was infiltrated by two men disguised as police officers. Within 81 minutes, thirteen priceless works of art had been stolen, including masterpieces by Rembrandt and Vermeer.

The security guards were handcuffed and placed in the basement. Astonishingly, the museum's alarms were bypassed entirely, suggesting the perpetrators possessed insider knowledge. Despite a massive FBI investigation, no arrests have ever been made.

It is widely believed that the paintings were moved overseas, but they have never been recovered. The empty frames remain hanging on the walls to this day, waiting for the art to be returned.

Why Passive Voice? Notice how the text focuses on the actions and the victims/objects (the art, the alarms) rather than the perpetrators, because their identities are unknown.

06. COMPREHENSION

Fact vs. Fiction

1. Psychopathic killers generally possess a highly overactive orbital cortex.
FALSE. He explicitly states that the orbital cortex (which controls moral behavior and impulsivity) is "turned off" or under-active in these individuals.
2. The MAOA gene (the "warrior gene") is exclusively passed down from the mother.
TRUE. It is on the X chromosome, and since males only get one X chromosome (from their mother), it affects men more prominently.
3. Genetics guarantee that a person will become a violent criminal, regardless of their childhood.
FALSE. He emphasizes that violent behavior is triggered by a combination of genetics AND severe childhood trauma (epigenetics).
07. SYSTEM RULES

The Passive Voice: All Forms

The passive voice is ubiquitous in news, crime reporting, and academic writing. We use it when the *action* is more important than the *actor*.

Core Passive Forms

to be + Past Participle (V3)
  • Present Simple: Evidence is collected daily.
  • Past Simple: The suspect was interrogated yesterday.
  • Present Continuous: The crime scene is being examined right now.
  • Present Perfect: The stolen funds have been recovered.
  • Past Perfect: The safe had been tampered with before police arrived.
  • Modals: The truth must be discovered.
08. SYSTEM RULES

Advanced Passive Constructs

Impersonal Passive (Reporting)

Used to report rumors, beliefs, or unverified claims without assigning a specific source.

It is said/believed/thought that...
"It is believed that the perpetrator fled the country."
Subject + is said/believed to + infinitive
"The perpetrator is believed to have fled the country."

Causative Passive

Used when we arrange for someone else to do something for us.

have / get + object + V3
"The CEO had the financial records altered." (He didn't do it himself; he ordered it).
"We need to get the security cameras checked."

10. PHONETICS

The Flow of Fluency

Native speakers rarely pronounce auxiliary verbs clearly in passive voice. They are reduced to "weak forms."

has been → /həz bɪn/
are being → /ər biːɪŋ/

13. INTERROGATION

The Detective & The Suspect

The Detective

You are interrogating a suspect regarding corporate fraud. Use Passive Voice to present facts coldly. ("The accounts were altered. Money has been moved.")

The Suspect

You deny everything. Use Active Voice to shift the blame to others, or Causative Passive to explain innocence. ("My accountant did it! I had my taxes done by a professional!")
