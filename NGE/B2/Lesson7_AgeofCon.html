Task 2 — Warm-Up: The Case Files

Briefing: Four Great Deceptions

Read these four brief case files. Which deception do you find most ingenious? Most unethical? Discuss with your partner before we go deeper.

Case File 001 // ~1200 BCE Mythological
The Trojan Horse

After ten years of failed siege, the Greeks built a giant hollow wooden horse and pretended to sail away, leaving it as an "offering." The Trojans wheeled it inside their walls. That night, Greek soldiers hidden inside emerged and opened the gates. Troy fell in hours. The strategy: weaponise your enemy's pride and curiosity against them.

Case File 002 // 1943 WWII Intelligence
Operation Mincemeat

British intelligence planted forged military documents on the corpse of a homeless man, dressed as a Royal Marines officer, and floated him off the coast of Spain. The documents deceived the Nazis into believing the Allied invasion would target Greece, not Sicily. It worked perfectly — and is considered one of the most audacious intelligence operations in history.

Case File 003 // 1944 WWII Deception
Operation Fortitude

To conceal the D-Day landings at Normandy, the Allies created an entirely fictional army group — "FUSAG" — commanded by General Patton, complete with fake radio traffic, inflatable tanks, and fabricated troop movements. Hitler held his best divisions back, waiting for the "real" invasion at Calais. By the time he realised, it was too late.

Case File 004 // 1960s–80s Cold War
Soviet Active Measures

The KGB ran sophisticated "active measures" (активные мероприятия) — planting fabricated news stories in foreign newspapers, forging US government documents, and funding front organisations to fracture Western alliances. Historians now estimate that the KGB employed more staff in disinformation than in actual espionage. The blueprint for modern information warfare.

🗣️ Discussion: What makes a deception "work"? What do all four operations have in common psychologically? Which would have been harder to execute, and why?
Task 3 — Vocabulary: Decrypted

Eight Words. Study Them Carefully.

These C1-level words appear throughout the lesson. Each entry is your cipher key — word, part of speech, definition, and a field example from the case files.

01
audacious
adj
Showing a willingness to take surprisingly bold risks; daringly original or daring in conception.
"Floating a corpse off enemy coastline was, by any measure, an audacious gamble."
02
fabricate
verb
To invent or construct (information or evidence) in order to deceive; to manufacture something false.
"They fabricated an entire army — fake tanks, fake radio signals, fake orders."
03
plausible
adj
Seeming reasonable or probable; appearing to be true or genuine, even if it may not be.
"A good con relies on appearing plausible — close enough to reality to be believed."
04
disinformation
noun
False information deliberately and often covertly spread in order to influence public opinion or obscure the truth. Distinct from misinformation, which may be accidental.
"The KGB's disinformation campaigns planted seeds of doubt that lasted for decades."
05
subterfuge
noun
Deceit used in order to achieve a goal; a trick or stratagem used to avoid or escape a problem, or to conceal one's true intentions.
"The entire operation depended on subterfuge — every detail had to be meticulously false."
06
credulous
adj
Having too great a readiness to believe things; overly willing to accept information without sufficient critical scrutiny. (Antonym: sceptical)
"A credulous enemy is a deceiver's greatest asset."
07
manipulate
verb
To control or influence (a person or situation) cleverly, unfairly, or unscrupulously; to handle or manage with skill.
"The operation was designed to manipulate the enemy's own confirmation bias against them."
08
conceal
verb
To keep something hidden or secret; to prevent (something) from being known or noticed by others.
"Every successful deception must conceal not just the truth, but the act of concealing itself."

Tasks 5 & 6 — Reading & Comprehension

The Operation Mincemeat Dispatch

Read this feature article about one of history's most extraordinary intelligence operations. Pay close attention to how verbs are followed — you'll need this for the grammar section.

"The Man Who Never Was": Britain's Most Audacious Wartime Deception

Adapted for B2/C1 Language Study — Original Events: April 1943

In the spring of 1943, two British intelligence officers — Ewen Montagu and Charles Cholmondeley — proposed doing something that sounded utterly impossible: convincing Adolf Hitler to defend the wrong country. Their plan, eventually known as Operation Mincemeat, involved fabricating an entire human identity and using a corpse to deliver forged military documents directly to the Nazi high command.

They began by managing to obtain the body of Glyndwr Michael, a Welsh vagrant who had died of accidental poisoning. They spent weeks constructing a false identity for him — Major William Martin of the Royal Marines — complete with love letters, receipts, a photograph of a fictional fiancée, and theatre ticket stubs. The goal was to make the Germans stop questioning the documents and start accepting them as genuine intelligence.

On April 30th, 1943, the submarine HMS Seraph surfaced off the coast of Huelva, Spain, and released the body. A Spanish fisherman discovered it. The local pro-Nazi intelligence network managed to photograph the documents before pretending to return them to the British, unaware that the British had secretly wanted them to do exactly that.

It worked beyond anyone's expectations. Hitler personally refused to reconsider his defensive positioning after seeing the documents. He ordered his generals to stop preparing for a Sicilian landing and to reinforce Greece and Sardinia instead. When Allied forces landed in Sicily on July 10th, the German defences were completely unprepared. Historians estimate that the deception saved tens of thousands of lives.

What makes Operation Mincemeat so remarkable is not just its audacity, but its precision. The team understood that avoiding detection required attending to every microscopic detail. They even remembered to include a bus ticket and a key to a non-existent locker in the dead man's pocket. Succeeding at this level of deception meant ceasing to think like intelligence officers and starting to think like the enemy.

Task 7 — Grammar Theory

Gerunds vs Infinitives: The Full Dossier

At B2/C1 level, choosing between a gerund (-ing) and an infinitive (to + verb) is about understanding the meaning and intention behind the main verb. This is not guesswork — it follows logical patterns.

Pattern What it signals Key Verbs Example from the text
Verb + -ing An ongoing activity, a general habit, or something completed and reflected upon. enjoy, avoid, deny, suggest, consider, imagine, involve, finish, practise, admit, risk, miss, can't help, recall, resent "The plan involved fabricating an entire identity."
Verb + to + inf. A future-oriented intention, plan, desire, or attempted action. want, decide, plan, hope, manage, refuse, agree, promise, expect, fail, arrange, afford, tend, choose, offer "Hitler refused to reconsider his positioning."
Verb + obj + to + inf. When you cause or want someone else to do something. want, expect, ask, tell, order, allow, persuade, convince, force, encourage, remind, warn, invite, get "He ordered his generals to reinforce Greece."
The Critical Exceptions
Verb With -ing (Gerund) With to + inf. Meaning Change?
stop He stopped questioning the documents.
(He no longer questioned them.)		 He stopped to question the documents.
(He paused in order to question them.)		 YES — completely different meaning
remember She remembered hiding the documents.
(She recalls a past action.)		 She remembered to hide the documents.
(She didn't forget — she did it.)		 YES — past recall vs. future duty
try They tried forging the signature.
(They experimented with it — as one option.)		 They tried to forge the signature.
(They made an effort, but may have failed.)		 YES — experiment vs. attempted effort
regret I regret joining the operation.
(I'm sorry about a past decision.)		 I regret to inform you the mission failed.
(Formal: sorry to say this now.)		 YES — past regret vs. formal present action
need The plan needs adjusting.
(Passive meaning — the plan needs to be adjusted.)		 We need to adjust the plan.
(Active — we must do it.)		 YES — passive vs. active meaning
🧠 The Rule of Thumb: If the action feels completed, habitual, or enjoyable in itself → use -ing. If the action feels intended, planned, or future-oriented → use to + infinitive. When in doubt, ask: "Is this looking backwards or forwards in time?"

Task 12 — Pronunciation Intel

Weak Forms: The Hidden Sound of "To"

Building on our work with elision (Lesson 6) and catenation (Lesson 4), today we examine weak forms — how function words like "to," "of," and "for" are drastically reduced in natural fast speech.

"to" — the infinitive marker
Strong: /tuː/ — Weak (natural): /tə/
In fast speech, "to" almost always reduces to a brief /tə/ (schwa). Only stressed when used as a destination or for contrast: "I want to go" → "I wanna go". Notice how "want to" becomes "wanna" in casual speech entirely.
Gerund vs Infinitive in Speech
Notice the rhythm difference
Compare these two:
"He stopped QUES-tion-ing" (3 beats, stress on QUE)
"He stopped tə QUES-tion" (pause + 2 beats)
The gerund flows; the infinitive creates a slight pause before it — use this to distinguish them when listening.
Reduction: "supposed to"
/səˈpəʊzd tuː/ → /səˈpəʊstə/
The -d and the t- of "to" merge and the vowel disappears: "He was supposed to report back" → "He was sposeta report back."
Reduction: "going to"
/ˈɡəʊɪŋ tuː/ → /ˈɡənə/
"Going to" (for future intention with gerund/infinitive structures) almost universally becomes "gonna" in spoken English. "I'm going to try forging it" → "I'm gonna try forging it."
