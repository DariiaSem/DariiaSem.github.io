Task 3 — Vocabulary: The Evidence Board

Case Files: 8 Words to Master

Study these C1-level words. Each one appears as a tagged evidence card — learn the word, its pronunciation, and a usage example. You will need them for the reading and writing tasks ahead.

Noun macabre
/məˈkɑːbrə/
Disturbing and horrifying due to its connection with death or injury; grotesque.
"Visitors were drawn to the macabre displays of human ash at the memorial."
Noun voyeurism
/ˈvɔɪ.ər.ɪ.z(ə)m/
The practice of deriving pleasure from observing the private suffering or misfortune of others.
"Critics argue that dark tourism is little more than glorified voyeurism."
Adjective morbid
/ˈmɔː.bɪd/
Having an excessive interest in disturbing and unpleasant subjects, especially death.
"Her morbid fascination with Victorian funeral rites became an academic specialty."
Noun atrocity
/əˈtrɒs.ɪ.ti/
An extremely wicked or cruel act, typically involving physical violence or injury on a massive scale.
"The museum was built to ensure the world never forgets the atrocities committed there."
Noun commodification
/kəˌmɒd.ɪ.fɪˈkeɪ.ʃən/
The process of treating something as a commodity — something to be bought, sold, or profited from.
"The commodification of tragedy raises profound ethical questions."
Verb grapple
/ˈɡræp.əl/
To struggle or deal with (a difficult problem or challenge); to try to overcome or understand something complex.
"Society continues to grapple with the ethics of filming disasters for social media."
Noun catharsis
/kəˈθɑː.sɪs/
The process of releasing strong or repressed emotions through a powerful experience; emotional purification.
"For many survivors, revisiting the site provided an unexpected catharsis."
Adjective solemn
/ˈsɒl.əm/
Formal and dignified; not cheerful or smiling; characterized by deep sincerity and serious intent.
"The atmosphere at the memorial was deeply solemn; nobody spoke above a whisper."

Tasks 5 & 6 — Reading & Comprehension

The Thanatourism Debate

Read this adapted academic article about dark tourism (also called "thanatourism"). Notice how the author uses hedging language and sophisticated vocabulary.

In the summer of 2019, a queue of over two thousand people stretched along the perimeter fence of the Chernobyl Exclusion Zone. They had come not in spite of the catastrophe that had occurred there, but precisely because of it. This is the paradox at the heart of dark tourism — a multi-billion-dollar industry that scholars have only recently begun to examine with the seriousness it deserves.

"We are drawn to death because it is the one thing we cannot understand from the inside."

The academic who coined the term "thanatourism" — the Greek thanatos meaning death — argued that the impulse is neither new nor inherently pathological. Romans flocked to gladiatorial arenas; Victorian Londoners purchased tickets to public executions. The morbid curiosity embedded in human nature appears to predate mass tourism by several millennia.

What has changed, however, is the scale and the commodification of these experiences. A ticket to tour Auschwitz-Birkenau costs around 80 Polish złoty. Submarine expeditions to the Titanic wreck were marketed at $250,000 per passenger before the ill-fated Titan submersible disaster of 2023. Critics argue that this transformation of atrocity into a product to be purchased is deeply voyeuristic — a form of disaster entertainment that risks trivializing genuine human suffering.

Defenders, however, argue the opposite. If people had not visited Hiroshima, would the world so viscerally grapple with the moral weight of nuclear weapons? The solemn atmosphere that descends upon visitors to war memorials and genocide museums can produce a genuine catharsis — a reckoning with history that no documentary or textbook can fully replicate. "If dark tourism did not exist," argued one scholar, "we would probably need to invent it."

The question, ultimately, may be less about whether people visit these sites and more about how they behave when they do. Selfies in front of gas chambers and souvenir mugs printed with images of nuclear shadows represent a macabre failure of empathy. But a quiet hour of reflection at a memorial wall may represent something altogether more profound — humanity's attempt to look directly at its own capacity for destruction without flinching.

Task 8 — Grammar Theory

The Mixed Conditional

You know the 2nd Conditional (present unreal) and the 3rd Conditional (past regrets). The Mixed Conditional combines them — it links a past condition to a present result, or a present condition to a past result.

PAST CONDITION
(if clause)
"If they hadn't built that memorial..."
NOW ← Crosses time →
The result affects the PRESENT
PRESENT RESULT
(main clause)
"...we wouldn't understand the atrocities today."
Type Structure Meaning Example
Mixed Type A
Past → Present		 If + Past Perfect, would/could + infinitive If something had (or hadn't) happened in the past, the present situation would be different. "If Chernobyl hadn't been opened to tourists, it would still be completely unknown to most people."
Mixed Type B
Present → Past		 If + Past Simple, would have + Past Participle If someone's current character/situation were different, a past event would have been different. "If she weren't so curious about history, she wouldn't have visited Auschwitz in the first place."
⚠️ Key Difference: In Mixed Type A, the past event is imaginary — it creates a different present. In Mixed Type B, the present state is imaginary — it retroactively changes a past outcome. Look for time adverbs like "now," "still," "today" (Type A) vs "back then," "at the time," "yesterday" (Type B).
Task 9 — Grammar: Full Conditionals Revision

The Conditional Ladder

Let's map all four conditionals together so you can see exactly when to use each one. Study the table and then discuss the examples with your teacher.

Type If-Clause Main Clause Real or Unreal?
Zero If + Present Simple Present Simple Always true / general facts. "If you visit Pompeii, you see the casts."
1st If + Present Simple will + Infinitive Real future possibility. "If you go to Chernobyl, you will need a guide."
2nd If + Past Simple would + Infinitive Unreal present/future. "If I had the money, I would visit Hiroshima."
3rd If + Past Perfect would have + V3 Unreal past. "If she had booked earlier, she would have got a place."
Mixed A If + Past Perfect would + Infinitive Past condition → Present result. "If they hadn't built the museum, we wouldn't know today."
Mixed B If + Past Simple would have + V3 Present state → Past result. "If she weren't so brave, she wouldn't have entered."

Task 13 — Pronunciation

Elision: The Art of Disappearing Sounds

Following our study of Catenation in Lesson 4, today we explore Elision — when native speakers drop certain sounds entirely in fast, natural speech. This is especially common in conditional structures.

would have
Written: /wʊd hæv/
Spoken fast: /wʊdəv/ → "wouldah"
The 'h' in 'have' is swallowed and the vowel reduces to a schwa. You'll hear: "She wouldah come if she'd known."
could have
Written: /kʊd hæv/
Spoken fast: /kʊdəv/ → "couldah"
Identical pattern. Never written "could of" — though many native speakers spell it that way incorrectly!
if I had
Written: /ɪf aɪ hæd/
Spoken fast: /ɪf aɪd/ → "if I'd"
The 'h' and the 'a' of 'had' are both elided. In fast speech: "If I'd gone there, I wouldn't be here now."
wouldn't have
Written: /ˈwʊdnt hæv/
Spoken fast: /ˈwʊdnəv/ → "wouldn't of"
The 't' in 'wouldn't' is often glottalized (a small pause/stop in the throat) before elision of the 'h'.
