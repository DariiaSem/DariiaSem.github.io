03. EDITORIAL REVIEW

The Anatomy of a Conspiracy

The Sociological Review // Issue 42
Control in a Chaotic World
The human brain is, fundamentally, a pattern-recognition machine. We are biologically hardwired to seek order in chaos. When confronted with monumental, random tragedies—a sudden assassination, a global pandemic, or an economic crash—accepting that these events lack a grand design triggers severe cognitive dissonance.

It is often more psychologically comforting to believe that a clandestine group of elites is actively colluding to control the world than to accept that the world is inherently uncontrollable. Conspiracy theories act as an emotional smokescreen against the terrifying randomness of existence.

In the digital age, these narratives proliferate instantly within algorithmic echo chambers. Once a theory takes root, confirmation bias ensures that believers only seek out information that makes the conspiracy seem plausible, dismissing all contradictory evidence as part of the cover-up itself.

06. SYSTEM RULES

Defining vs. Non-Defining

Relative clauses give more information about a noun. The presence or absence of commas completely changes the meaning of the sentence.

1. Defining Relative Clauses

No Commas

These clauses give essential information. Without them, we don't know who or what is being talked about. You can use "that" instead of who/which.

"The scientist who leaked the documents vanished."
(Tells us *which specific* scientist vanished. There are many scientists.)

2. Non-Defining Relative Clauses

Requires Commas

These give extra, non-essential information. If you remove the clause, the sentence still makes perfect sense. You cannot use "that".

"Dr. Smith, who leaked the documents, vanished."
(We already know who Dr. Smith is. The clause just adds a fun fact.)
07. SYSTEM RULES

Prepositions in Relative Clauses

In C1 academic and formal English, prepositions should not dangle at the end of a relative clause.

Preposition + Whom / Which

Move the preposition before the relative pronoun. You must use whom (people) or which (things). Never use who or that.

Informal (B2) Formal (C1)
The forum that they post their theories on is illegal. The forum on which they post their theories is illegal.
The whistleblower who the journalist spoke to disappeared. The whistleblower to whom the journalist spoke disappeared.
08. SYSTEM RULES

Advanced Structures

Quantifiers + Relative

Used to talk about a portion of a group.

some of / many of / none of + whom/which

"There were hundreds of leaked files, most of which were heavily redacted."

Full-Clause "Which"

Sometimes "which" refers to the entire previous action/situation, not just one noun. It needs a comma.

"They denied all the allegations, which only fueled the conspiracy further."
09. SYSTEM RULES

Reduced Relative Clauses

To make academic writing concise, we often drop the relative pronoun and the verb "to be", replacing the clause with a participle phrase.

Active Voice → Present Participle (-ing) Passive Voice → Past Participle (-ed/V3)
The theories which are circulating online...
↓
The theories circulating online...		 The evidence which was found at the scene...
↓
The evidence found at the scene...
