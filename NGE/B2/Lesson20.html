02. C1 LEXICON

Words of the Unknown

These advanced terms are crucial for discussing myths, psychology, and internet culture. Hover over the redacted blocks to reveal their meanings.

Apocryphal
adjective
Of doubtful authenticity, although widely circulated as being true or factual.
"The story about the alligators in the sewers is entirely apocryphal."
Credulity
noun
A tendency to be too ready to believe that something is real or true.
"Scammers rely on the credulity of people on the internet."
Ubiquitous
adjective
Present, appearing, or found everywhere at the same time.
"The Slenderman myth became ubiquitous across forums in 2011."
To debunk
verb
To expose the falseness or hollowness of a myth, idea, or belief.
"Snopes was created specifically to debunk internet folklore."
Macabre
adjective
Disturbing and horrifying because of involvement with or depiction of death and injury.
"Urban legends often feature macabre details to make them memorable."
To propagate
verb
To spread and promote an idea, theory, or piece of information widely.
"Social media algorithms propagate fear-based myths faster than facts."
03. EVIDENCE FILE

From Campfires to Creepypastas

Read the editorial. Observe the highlighted phrases—these are advanced quantifiers we use to express precise volumes or amounts.

The Digital Folklore Archive // Vol. 4
The Mutation of Myth
For centuries, human beings required a great deal of time and physical proximity to propagate a myth. A cautionary tale about walking alone in the woods would take generations to spread across a continent. Today, that timeline has been reduced to a fraction of what it once was.

In the modern era, scores of terrifying stories—now known as "creepypastas"—are generated daily on forums like Reddit. The vast majority of these stories are forgotten instantly. However, a handful of them strike a visceral nerve, tapping into our collective anxieties. Slenderman, for instance, started as a single Photoshop contest entry. Within months, there was scarcely any corner of the internet that hadn't encountered him.

Interestingly, despite having access to limitless information, modern society shows a negligible amount of skepticism when faced with a compelling narrative. We possess masses of data, yet very little immunity to a well-crafted, macabre lie. It seems that no matter the technology, our credulity remains beautifully, terrifyingly intact.
04. SYSTEM RULES

Advanced Quantifiers

In C1 English, we move beyond "a lot of" and "some". We use precise quantifiers that collocate strictly with Countable (C) or Uncountable (U) nouns, or both (B).

I. Expressing Large Amounts

Quantifier Rule (C / U / B) Example & Nuance
A great deal of Uncountable only "It takes a great deal of courage to debunk a myth." (Formal)
Scores of Countable (Plural) "Scores of teenagers believed the story." (Means 'large numbers of')
Masses of / Loads of Both "We found masses of evidence/files." (Informal, overwhelming amount)
The vast majority of Both "The vast majority of the internet / people ignored it." (Almost all)

II. Expressing Small or Zero Amounts

Quantifier Rule (C / U / B) Example & Nuance
A negligible amount of Uncountable only "There is a negligible amount of truth in it." (So small it doesn't matter)
A handful of Countable (Plural) "Only a handful of people saw the creature." (A very small number)
Scarcely any / Hardly any Both "There is scarcely any doubt / witnesses." (Almost none. *Never use with double negative*)
⚠️ The Classic Trap: Few vs. A Few / Little vs. A Little
"A few / A little" = Positive meaning (Some). "I have a few clues." (That's good!)
"Few / Little" = Negative meaning (Not enough). "I have few clues." (That's bad!)

Correct. Studies show that narratives evoking high-arousal emotions (like fear/disgust) are shared significantly faster.
2. The internet has caused a negligible amount of change to the actual core structure of folklore.
False. The medium has changed the structure—digital myths often rely on crowd-sourced worldbuilding rather than a single linear narrative.

Not Given. The video discusses human psychology and credulity generally, but provides no specific demographic data on teenagers' media literacy.
