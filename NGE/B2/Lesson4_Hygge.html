Task 3: Reading

The Hygge Manifesto

Read through the core pillars of the Danish Hygge philosophy. Which of these resonates the most with you?

🕯️ 1. Atmosphere & Presence

Atmosphere: Turn down the harsh lighting. The right ambiance, preferably from a fireplace or candles, is crucial for setting a serene mood.


Presence: Be here now. Turn off the phones. It's about being entirely present in the current moment without digital distractions.

🍰 2. Pleasure & Comfort

Pleasure: Allow yourself to be indulgent. Coffee, chocolate, cookies, cakes, and rich comfort foods are highly encouraged.


Comfort: Get comfy. Take a break. It's about relaxation, wearing soft clothing, and letting go of strict formalities.

🤝 3. Truce & Togetherness

Truce: No drama. Let's discuss politics and controversial topics another day. Establish a temporary peace to enjoy the company.


Togetherness: Build relationships and narratives. Nurture equality by sharing tasks, like cooking the meal together.

Task 8: Phonology Theory

Connected Speech: Catenation

Native English speakers rarely pronounce words individually. They link them together to create a smooth, flowing rhythm. The most common type of linking is Catenation (linking a final consonant to an initial vowel).

The Rule Written Form How it Sounds (Linked)
Consonant + Vowel

When a word ends in a consonant sound, and the next word begins with a vowel sound, the consonant "jumps" over to the vowel.		 An apple a napple
Trip over tri pover
Clean up clea nup
This afternoon thi safternoon
Task 9: Advanced Phonology

Connected Speech: Intrusion

What happens when a word ends in a vowel, and the next word ALSO begins with a vowel? We don't pause! Instead, we intrude (add) a tiny extra sound: /w/, /j/ (y), or /r/.

The Rule (Vowel + Vowel) Written Form How it Sounds (Intrusion)
Adding a /w/ sound
Used after lips are rounded (e.g., words ending in o, u, ow)		 Go out
You are		 go /w/ out
you /w/ are
Adding a /j/ (y) sound
Used after lips are wide (e.g., words ending in i, e, ay)		 I ate
The apple		 I /j/ ate
the /j/ apple
Adding an /r/ sound
Used in British English when ending in "a" or "aw"		 Law and order law /r/ and order
