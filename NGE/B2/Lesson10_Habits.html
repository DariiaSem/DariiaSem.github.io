01. THE MECHANICS

The Habit Loop

According to Charles Duhigg, every habit is driven by a 3-step loop. Analyze the components below. Can you map one of your own daily habits (good or bad) onto this structure?

1. The Cue

The trigger that initiates the behavior. It can be a specific time, a location, an emotional state, or preceding action. (e.g., Feeling stressed at 3 PM).

2. The Routine

The actual behavior or action you perform in response to the cue. This is the habit itself. (e.g., Going to the kitchen and eating chocolate).

3. The Reward

The benefit you gain from the behavior. This satisfies the craving and tells your brain to remember this loop for the future. (e.g., A temporary sugar rush and distraction).

Your Turn:

Identify a bad habit. What is the actual cue? To break the habit, you must keep the cue and the reward, but swap the routine. Discuss with your partner.

The Language of Rewiring

noun
Neuroplasticity
The brain's ability to reorganize itself by forming new neural connections throughout life, allowing us to learn or break habits.
adjective
Ingrained
(Of a habit or belief) firmly fixed or established; difficult to change. "A deeply ingrained morning routine."
verb
To trigger
To cause an event, reaction, or habit to start functioning. "Stress usually triggers his smoking habit."
adjective
Compounding
The process where small, consistent actions multiply and produce massive results over a long period.
verb
To override
To use your conscious willpower to stop an automatic process or impulse. "He had to actively override the urge to check his phone."
noun / verb
Plateau
A state of little or no change following a period of activity or progress. "After a month of gym, she hit a plateau."
adjective
Detrimental
Tending to cause harm. "Lack of sleep is highly detrimental to building new neural pathways."
noun / verb
Relapse
A deterioration in someone's state of behavior after a temporary improvement; falling back into bad habits.

04. SYSTEM RULES

Projecting the Future Self

To talk about habit formation goals, we must look into the future. At C1 level, we use the Future Continuous and Future Perfect to express high-level time relationships.

Future Continuous

Action in progress
will be + V-ing (or: will be sleeping, will be working)

We use this to describe an action that will be in progress at a specific time in the future, or to talk about expected, normal future events.

"Don't call me at 6 AM tomorrow. I will be sleeping." (Action in progress).
"This time next month, I will be waking up without an alarm." (Expected routine).

Future Perfect

Action completed before a deadline
will have + V3 (or: will have finished, will have built)

We use this to say that an action will be completed before a specific time or another action in the future. It is almost always used with time expressions like "by..." or "before...".

Action Completes
Deadline (By 2027)
"By December, I will have automated my morning routine."
"By the time we arrive, the lecture will have started."
