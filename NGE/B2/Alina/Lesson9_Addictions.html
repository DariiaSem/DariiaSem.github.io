noun
Craving
A powerful, often uncontrollable desire for something — especially a substance or behaviour. In habit science, cravings are the neurological signal that initiates the habit loop.
👁
noun
Dopamine
A neurotransmitter (chemical messenger in the brain) associated with reward, motivation, and pleasure. It is the core biological driver of habit reinforcement and addictive behaviour.
👁
verb
To trigger
To cause or initiate a reaction, process, or behaviour. In habit loops, a trigger (also called a cue) is the environmental or emotional signal that sets the cycle in motion.
👁
noun
Relapse
The return to a previous negative habit or addictive behaviour after a period of successful abstinence or improvement. Common in smoking cessation, alcoholism, and other behavioural change programmes.
👁
adj.
Compulsive
Resulting from or relating to an irresistible urge to behave in a certain way, even against one's own conscious wishes. Compulsive behaviours feel driven and are difficult to stop voluntarily.
👁
noun
Abstinence
The deliberate and sustained practice of restraining oneself from a harmful substance or behaviour, such as tobacco, alcohol, or sugar. Often prescribed as part of a clinical treatment plan.
👁
noun
Reinforcement
The process by which a behaviour is strengthened or made more likely to recur. Positive reinforcement adds a reward; negative reinforcement removes an unpleasant stimulus — both increase the behaviour.
👁
noun
Mindfulness
The practice of deliberately focusing full, non-judgemental attention on the present moment — including one's thoughts, feelings, and sensations. Research shows it is more effective than willpower for breaking addictive habits.

Tasks 5 & 6: Reading & Comprehension

The Habit Loop & the Dental Chair

Read these two passages — a neuroscience overview and a clinical case study. Identify how the vocabulary from Task 3 appears in context.

🔬 The Neuroscience of Habit

According to MIT neuroscientist Ann Graybiel, every habit operates as a three-part loop: a trigger (or cue) that initiates the behaviour, a routine (the behaviour itself), and a reward that satisfies a craving and tells the brain the loop is worth remembering.


The reward releases dopamine, which etches the loop deeper into the brain's basal ganglia — a primitive structure that operates below conscious awareness. This is why habits feel automatic: over time, the brain chunks the entire sequence into a single mental unit and runs it without deliberate thought.


Breaking a habit, then, requires targeting the craving — not the routine. As psychiatrist Judson Brewer's research demonstrates, the most effective intervention is not willpower (suppressing the routine) but mindfulness: paying curious attention to the craving itself until the brain learns it is not actually satisfying.

🦷 Clinical Case: The Compulsive Grinder

Maria, 38, presented with severe bruxism (teeth grinding) that had caused significant enamel wear and fractured two molars over five years. She was referred for a behavioural assessment after her third night guard in two years had been destroyed.


The assessment revealed a clear habit loop: the trigger was workplace stress; the routine was jaw clenching and grinding during sleep; the perceived reward was tension release. Her grinding had become entirely compulsive — she was unaware it was happening until waking with jaw pain.


The clinical team recommended a dual approach: a new mouthguard for immediate protection, and a course of mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) therapy. After eight weeks, Maria reported a significant reduction in tension and a near-complete cessation of grinding — without a single relapse. The key was addressing the trigger, not suppressing the routine.

Task 8: Grammar Theory

Modal Verbs: Ability, Possibility & Obligation

Modal verbs are the backbone of professional clinical language — how we advise patients, describe possibilities, and state requirements. We use them constantly without always noticing. Let's bring them into conscious awareness.

Function Modal Verbs Clinical / Habit Context Example
Ability
Present / general		 can / be able to With mindfulness training, patients can interrupt the habit loop before it completes. / She is able to manage her cravings using breathing techniques.
Ability
Past		 could / was able to After therapy, he was able to stop grinding his teeth at night. (= succeeded on a specific occasion) / She could always sense the craving coming. (= general past ability)
Possibility
Present / future		 may / might / could The patient might relapse if the trigger isn't addressed. / Bruxism could be causing the jaw pain. / This habit may take months to rewire.
Obligation / Necessity
(strong — external rule)		 must / have to Patients must wear the night guard every evening. / You have to complete the full course of treatment.
Obligation / Necessity
(weaker — advice)		 should / ought to You should try replacing the reward with a healthy alternative. / Dentists ought to screen for bruxism at every routine check-up.
No obligation don't have to / needn't You don't have to quit sugar entirely — just reduce frequency. / You needn't replace all habits at once.
Prohibition must not / cannot You must not use a hard-bristle toothbrush — it accelerates enamel wear. / You cannot resume smoking after the extraction — it will cause dry socket.
⚠️ C1 Distinction — could vs. was able to (past ability):
Could = general past ability (she could always feel the craving) or polite requests
Was able to = specific achievement in a particular instance (she was able to resist the craving yesterday)
❌ She could resist the craving yesterday.     ✅ She was able to resist the craving yesterday.

Task 12: Video Comprehension — Interactive

True, False, or Not Given?

Based on Dr. Brewer's TED Talk, decide on each statement. Click for instant feedback, then report his claims using Reported Speech: "Brewer argued that..." / "He stated that..."

1. Dr. Brewer claims that our brains' habit-forming mechanism is one of the most evolutionarily conserved learning processes known to science.
✓ TRUE. Brewer opens by saying this reward-based learning is "one of the most evolutionarily conserved learning processes currently known in science, conserved back to the most basic nervous systems."
2. Brewer's research found that mindfulness training was twice as effective as standard gold-standard therapy for helping people quit smoking.
✓ FALSE. He states it was five times as effective as the current gold-standard treatment for smoking cessation — not twice.
3. According to the talk, the habit loop Brewer describes consists of: trigger → craving → reward.
✓ FALSE. Brewer describes it as trigger → behaviour → reward (not "craving" as the middle element — he uses "craving" as what drives the trigger, but the loop itself is trigger → behaviour → reward).
4. Dr. Brewer uses personal anecdotes about his own mindfulness practice to introduce the difficulty of paying attention.
✓ TRUE. He opens by describing his own experience of learning to meditate — sweating through T-shirts in winter, taking naps, finding it exhausting — to illustrate why paying attention is so hard.
5. Brewer argues that patients should be given willpower-based reward systems by their doctors to overcome addictions.
✓ FALSE. This is the opposite of Brewer's argument. He explicitly challenges the willpower approach and argues that curiosity and mindfulness — not reward systems — are the key to sustainable habit change.
6. The talk mentions a specific app or digital programme that Brewer developed to deliver mindfulness training at scale.
✓ TRUE. Brewer mentions developing app-based and online mindfulness training programmes to deliver tools in context — "right when and where cravings arise" — at scale, beyond the clinical setting.

Task 13: Pronunciation Focus

Weak Forms of Modal Verbs in Fast Speech

Based on Avery & Ehrlich's Teaching American English Pronunciation, modal verbs are almost always unstressed and reduced in fast, natural speech. This is one of the most important patterns for sounding fluent and natural — especially in consultations.

can → /kən/

Stressed: CAN (when denying or contrasting)
"Yes, I CAN do that!" /kæn/
Reduced (normal speech):
"You /kən/ use a straw." "I /kən/ see the issue."

should → /ʃəd/

Full form never used in fast speech
"You /ʃəd/ try a night guard."
Contracted with subject:
"You should've /ˈʃʊdəv/ come sooner."

must → /məst/ or /məs/

Full /mʌst/ only for strong emphasis
"You MUST stop immediately!" /mʌst/
Reduced in normal clinical advice:
"You /məs(t)/ wear the guard nightly."
✍️ Pair practice — read these clinical phrases aloud with reduced modal forms:

1. "You should /ʃəd/ try to identify what triggers the craving."
2. "She can /kən/ manage the habit, but it might /maɪt/ take several months."
3. "Patients must /məst/ not smoke after an extraction — it could /kəd/ cause serious complications."
4. "You don't have to quit everything at once — you ought to /ˈɔːtə/ focus on one habit first."
