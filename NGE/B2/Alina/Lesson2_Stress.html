Task 6: Advanced Grammar Theory

Present Simple vs. Continuous

At the C1 level, we use these tenses for very specific, advanced nuances beyond just "routines vs. happening now."

Advanced Usage Tense Clinical/Professional Example
Performative Verbs: When the verb itself performs the action (promise, advise, apologize, suggest). Present Simple I suggest we use a composite here.
(Not: I am suggesting)
Dramatic Narrative: Telling a story or summarizing events to make them feel immediate. Present Simple The patient walks in and says...
Annoying Habits: Repeated actions that cause frustration, usually with always or constantly. Present Continuous He is always missing his appointments!
Changing Situations: A trend or state that is developing or shifting over time. Present Continuous Clinic wait times are increasing this year.
The staff is getting burnt out.

Task 8: Advanced Grammar Theory

Present Perfect: Simple vs. Continuous

Both tenses connect the past to the present, but they highlight entirely different aspects of the action.

PAST
X
X
X
NOW
Tense Focus / Rules Clinical Example
Present Perfect Simple
(have/has + V3)
  • Focuses on the completed result or quantity. (How much? How many?)
  • Recent completed actions (with just/already/yet).
  • Permanent situations with stative verbs.
 I have prepped three teeth for crowns this morning.
I have just finished the extraction.
Present Perfect Continuous
(have/has been + V-ing)
  • Focuses on the ongoing duration or process. (How long?)
  • Actions that just stopped explaining a visible present side-effect.
 I have been holding this curing light for 60 seconds.
My hands hurt because I have been scaling teeth all day.
Stative Verb Rule: Even if you want to emphasize duration (How long), you cannot use the continuous form with stative verbs (know, need, want, believe).
✅ I have known this patient for years.
❌ I have been knowing this patient...

Task 10: Pronunciation Theory

The 3 Sounds of "-s / -es"

Based on Peter Avery & Susan Ehrlich's Teaching American English Pronunciation, the "s" ending for plural nouns and 3rd-person Present Simple verbs has three distinct sounds.

Sound 1: /s/

If the word ends in a voiceless sound (p, t, k, f).

Dentists
Extracts

Sound 2: /z/

If the word ends in a voiced sound or a vowel.

Crowns
Cures

Sound 3: /ɪz/

Adds an extra syllable! Used after 'sibilants' (s, z, sh, ch, j, x).

Brushes
Bridges
