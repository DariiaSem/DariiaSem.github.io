Causing or relating to inflammation — the body's immune response to harm. An inflammatory diet (high in sugar and processed food) is linked to gum disease and systemic illness.

👁
noun
Demineralisation
The process by which acids dissolve the mineral content (calcium and phosphate) of tooth enamel, making it weaker and more vulnerable to cavities.
👁
adj.
Holistic
Considering the whole person — physical, mental, and social wellbeing — rather than just the specific symptoms or condition being treated.
👁

In healthcare: to advise, guide, or give structured professional advice to a patient about their health, behaviour, or treatment options.
👁
noun
Cariogenic
Promoting the development of tooth decay (caries). A cariogenic diet is one high in fermentable sugars that feed cavity-causing bacteria in the mouth.

Tasks 5 & 6: Reading & Comprehension

What Your Mouth Says About Your Diet

Read these two extracts — one from a patient interview, one from a clinical nutrition guide. Notice how clinicians ask and tell their patients things — you will need this for Grammar Tasks 8–10.

🗣 Patient Interview: Maria, 34

The hygienist sat down and asked me what I usually ate for breakfast. I told her I had a large glass of orange juice every morning. She frowned and asked me if I knew how acidic that was on tooth enamel. I said I didn't, honestly.


Then she told me to stop drinking juice directly and to use a straw instead. She also asked whether I rinsed my mouth with water after eating fruit. I shook my head. She asked me not to brush my teeth immediately after eating acidic food, because the enamel is temporarily softened.


Finally, she asked what dairy products I ate regularly. When I mentioned cheese, she smiled and said cheese was actually great for neutralising mouth acid. She told me to keep eating it. I left feeling like my entire morning routine had been exposed as a health crime.

📋 Clinical Nutrition Guide (Extract)

Advising patients on diet is one of the most impactful — and most overlooked — aspects of preventive dentistry. Research consistently shows that patient compliance improves significantly when advice is personalised rather than generic.


Key principles for the dentist to communicate:


1. Frequency matters more than quantity. Sipping a cariogenic drink over an hour is far more damaging than drinking it quickly. Advise patients to limit the frequency of acid or sugar exposure, not just the amount.


2. Remineralisation window. After acid exposure, there is a 20–30 minute window where enamel is vulnerable. Counsel patients to wait before brushing, and to use fluoride products to accelerate remineralisation.


3. Anti-inflammatory diets. A diet rich in leafy greens, omega-3 fatty acids, and fermented foods supports a healthy oral microbiome and significantly reduces the risk of periodontal (gum) disease.

Task 8: Grammar Theory

Reported Questions

When a dentist or hygienist asks a patient a question, and we report it later, we must make three simultaneous changes: backshift the tense, change pronouns/time expressions, and — crucially — reverse the word order back to normal statement order (no more inversion!).

The Two Types of Reported Question

⚡ YES/NO Questions → use if or whether

DIRECT

"Do you floss daily?"

→
REPORTED

She asked if he flossed daily. (no auxiliary, normal order)

DIRECT

"Have you eaten today?"

→
REPORTED

He asked whether she had eaten that day.

🔍 WH- Questions → keep the WH- word, drop inversion

DIRECT

"What do you eat for breakfast?"

→
REPORTED

She asked what he ate for breakfast. (no "do"!)

DIRECT

"How long have you had this pain?"

→
REPORTED

The dentist asked how long she had had that pain.

🚨 The Most Common Mistake: Students often keep the inverted word order in reported questions.
❌ She asked what did he eat.     ✅ She asked what he ate.
The auxiliary verb (do/does/did/have/has) disappears completely in reported questions. The subject comes before the verb — just like a normal statement.

Reporting Verbs for Questions

Reporting Verb Pattern Clinical Example
ask ask + (object) + if/wh-word + clause She asked him what he had eaten that morning.
enquire / wonder enquire + if/wh-word + clause The patient enquired whether the procedure was painful.
want to know want to know + if/wh-word + clause The dentist wanted to know how often she brushed.
Task 9: Grammar Theory

Reported Commands & Advice

When a dentist tells a patient to do something — or not to do it — we report it with a special structure: verb + object + (not) to + infinitive. The imperative completely disappears.

Direct Command / Advice Reported Form Reporting Verb Used
"Stop drinking fizzy drinks." She told him to stop drinking fizzy drinks. tell (+ object + to)
"Don't brush immediately after eating." She told him not to brush immediately after eating. tell (+ object + not to)
"Try to eat more fermented foods." She advised him to try to eat more fermented foods. advise (+ object + to)
"Please come back in three months." She asked him to come back in three months. ask (+ object + to)
"You should use fluoride toothpaste." She recommended that he use fluoride toothpaste. (subjunctive!) recommend / suggest (+ that + subj.)
🎯 C1 Note — recommend/suggest: Unlike tell and advise, the verbs recommend and suggest do NOT take a direct object + to-infinitive.
❌ She recommended him to eat less sugar.
✅ She recommended (that) he eat less sugar. (subjunctive — no backshift needed)
✅ She recommended eating less sugar. (gerund)

Task 13: Pronunciation Focus

Intonation in Clinical Questions

Based on Teaching American English Pronunciation (Avery & Ehrlich), question intonation conveys attitude and clinical trust. In consultations, getting this right makes patients feel safe and heard.

↗ Rising Intonation (Yes/No Questions)

Your voice rises at the end — it signals you expect an answer.
"Do you drink fizzy drinks? ↗"
Feels open and non-judgmental — critical for sensitive dietary questions.
"Have you been flossing? ↗"

↘ Falling Intonation (WH- Questions)

Your voice falls at the end — signals authority and confidence.
"What do you usually eat for lunch? ↘"
Falling tone sounds professional, not interrogative or aggressive.
"How long have you had this sensitivity? ↘"

↗↘ Tag Questions (Building Rapport)

Rising tag = genuine question. Falling tag = seeking confirmation.
"You've reduced sugar, haven't you? ↘" (expecting yes)
Use tag questions to gently confront patients about habits.
"You haven't been rinsing after meals, have you? ↗"
