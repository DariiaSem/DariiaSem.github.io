01 // The Scenario

The Single-Use Dilemma

Medicine relies heavily on single-use plastics to prevent cross-contamination. But what happens to the thousands of gloves, suction tips, and barriers a clinic uses every month? Read the quote below and discuss.

"We try to save patients, but we forget to save the environment they live in. The healthcare sector is responsible for nearly 5% of global greenhouse gas emissions. If healthcare were a country, it would be the fifth-largest climate polluter on the planet."

— Adapted from Health Care Without Harm (HCWH) Global Report.

💬 Discussion: Think about your clinic. If you stop to consider the waste produced in just one implant surgery, what items take up the most space in the bin? Can cross-contamination protocols coexist with environmental sustainability?
02 // Lexical Focus

C1 Adjectives: The Environment

Forget basic nouns like "pollution" or "recycling". To sound like a fluent C1 speaker in daily conversation or business, you need powerful adjectives. Hover to unlock.

🍂
adjective
Biodegradable
Capable of being naturally decomposed by bacteria, avoiding pollution. "We replaced plastic cups with biodegradable bamboo ones."
🗑️
adjective
Disposable
Intended to be used once, or until no longer useful, and then thrown away. "The sheer volume of disposable tools in dentistry is terrifying."
⚠️
adjective
Hazardous
Risky or dangerous to human health or the environment. "Used needles must be placed in a hazardous waste bin immediately."
🚰
adjective
Resource-intensive
Requiring a huge amount of water, electricity, or raw materials to produce or run. "Running an autoclave 10 times a day is incredibly resource-intensive."
⚖️
adjective
Negligible
So small or unimportant that it is not worth considering. "Switching off the lights has a negligible impact compared to reducing plastic waste."
🌲
adjective
Pristine
In its original condition; completely clean and unspoiled. "Microplastics are now being found even in the most pristine environments on Earth."
🤥
adjective
Deceptive
Giving an appearance or impression different from the true one (often used for 'greenwashing'). "Their marketing is highly deceptive; the product isn't actually eco-friendly."
♻️
adjective
Sustainable
Able to be maintained at a certain rate or level without depleting natural resources. "We need a viable, sustainable alternative to amalgam."

04 // Grammar Core

Verbs that Change Meaning

As you saw in the simulator, some verbs completely change their meaning depending on whether they are followed by a gerund (-ing) or an infinitive (to do). This is a classic C1 grammar trap.

Verb + to Infinitive (Forward / Interruption) + Gerund (-ing) (Backward / The Action itself)
STOP stop to do
To interrupt what you are doing in order to do something else.
"We stopped to check the patient's airway." 		stop doing
To cease an action entirely.
"We stopped buying disposable plastics last year."
REMEMBER remember to do
To not forget to perform a necessary future task.
"Please remember to sort the hazardous waste." 		remember doing
To have a memory of a past event.
"I remember ordering the biodegradable cups."
FORGET forget to do
To fail to do a necessary task.
"He forgot to turn off the water." 		forget doing
To lose the memory of having done something.
"I'll never forget visiting that pristine forest."

The Eco-Audit

Complete these sentences from a clinic's environmental audit by typing the correct form (infinitive with 'to' OR gerund '-ing') of the verb in brackets.

  • [reduce] The clinic manager is trying the amount of disposable plastic, but it is extremely difficult.
  • [buy] I completely forgot the new biodegradable cups, so we have none left today.
  • [use] If patients complain about the paper cups leaking, try two cups together to make them stronger.
  • [dispose] Staff must stop of normal waste in the hazardous bins—it costs us extra money.
  • [announce] We regret that our transition to sustainable procurement has been delayed.

  • 1. The clinic generates 50kg of hazardous waste per week. Its carbon footprint is massive.

  • 2. We rely on strict cross-contamination protocols. They are absolutely essential for patient safety.
