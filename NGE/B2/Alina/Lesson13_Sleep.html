03 // Lexical Focus

The Language of Rest & Ruin

Hover over the cards to unlock C1/C2 terminology related to sleep science and its dental implications. You will need these for the video and the debate.

🦷
noun (medical)
Bruxism
The involuntary or habitual grinding of the teeth, typically during sleep. Highly correlated with stress and sleep apnea.
🧠
noun phrase
Circadian rhythm
The natural, internal 24-hour clock that regulates the sleep-wake cycle in the brain. Light exposure heavily influences it.
🔄
verb (advanced)
To consolidate
To make something physically stronger or more solid. In sleep science: the process where the brain solidifies short-term memories into long-term storage during deep sleep.
📉
noun phrase
Cognitive decline
A gradual decrease in mental faculties such as memory, thinking, and focus. Chronic sleep deprivation accelerates this process.
🌬️
noun
Airway
The passage by which air enters and leaves the lungs. Dentists are often the first to notice airway obstruction issues.
🔋
adj
Restorative
Having the ability to restore health, strength, or well-being. Deep NREM sleep is highly restorative for physical tissue (and gums).
🪫
adj
Sluggish
Moving or reacting slower than normal; lacking energy or alertness. A common symptom after a night of fragmented sleep.
⚖️
noun
Deprivation
The damaging lack of a material benefit considered to be a necessity. Sleep deprivation alters hormones, increases hunger, and spikes cortisol.

06 // Grammar Core

Relative Clauses: Defining vs. Non-Defining

Relative clauses add information about a noun. At the C1 level, the distinction between Defining (essential) and Non-Defining (extra) is crucial for precision in clinical notes, emails, and professional communication.

// DEFINING (Essential)

Provides vital information. Without it, we don't know which person/thing you mean. NO commas.

"The patient who has severe bruxism needs a night guard."

(Which patient? Only the one with bruxism.)

  • Can use that instead of who/which.
  • Can omit the pronoun if it's the object. (The device [that] we ordered arrived.)

// NON-DEFINING (Extra)

Provides bonus information. We already know who/what you mean. Requires commas.

"Mr. Davis, who has severe bruxism, needs a night guard."

(We already know it's Mr. Davis. The bruxism is extra info.)

  • Cannot use "that".
  • Cannot omit the pronoun.

Advanced Pronouns & Prepositions

Pronoun Usage / Clinical Example
Whose Possession.
"The patient whose airway is obstructed will feel sluggish."
Preposition + Whom/Which Formal writing (C1). Preposition moves to the front.
"This is the sleep study on which we based the diagnosis." (Instead of: ...which we based the diagnosis on.)
Which (whole clause) Refers back to the entire previous idea, not just a noun.
"He refused to wear his CPAP machine, which led to cognitive decline."

The Synthesizer

Combine the two sentences into one using a relative clause. Decide if it needs commas (Non-Defining) or no commas (Defining). Watch out for formal prepositions!

  • 1. The new mandibular device is highly effective. It was designed to keep the airway open.

11 // Clinical Simulation

The Consultation

A patient comes in complaining of intense jaw pain, sluggishness, and morning headaches. As the dental professional, you suspect bruxism linked to a compromised airway. Role-play the interaction using your new vocabulary and relative clauses.

// The Dental Assistant / Dentist:

Explain the connection between their jaw pain and sleep. Use a Defining Relative Clause: "Patients who grind their teeth at night often..." Suggest a custom night guard. Explain the importance of restorative sleep.

⬇
// The Patient:

Express skepticism. You think it's just stress from work. Use a Non-Defining Relative Clause to describe your job: "My job, which requires 60 hours a week, is..." Ask how a piece of plastic in your mouth can fix your sluggishness.

⬇
// The Dental Professional:

Defend the treatment using advanced C1 vocabulary (airway, consolidate, circadian rhythm). Use Formal Preposition + Which: "The airway obstruction, to which your body responds by grinding the teeth, prevents..."
