02 // Lexical Focus

The Language of Influence

To persuade without manipulating, you need precise language. Hover over the cards to unlock C1 vocabulary essential for advanced patient management.

🤝
noun
Rapport
A close and harmonious relationship in which people understand each other's feelings or ideas and communicate well. "Building rapport before picking up the drill is essential."
😰
adj.
Apprehensive
Anxious or fearful that something bad or unpleasant will happen. "Dental assistants are experts at calming apprehensive patients."
🛡️
verb
To mitigate
To make something less severe, harmful, or painful. "We use topical gel to mitigate the discomfort of the injection."
🗣️
verb
To elicit
To draw out a response, answer, or fact from someone in reaction to one's own actions or questions. "The dentist asked open-ended questions to elicit the patient's true concerns."
🩺
adj.
Paternalistic
Relating to the restriction of the freedom and responsibilities of subordinates/patients in their supposed best interest. "Modern medicine has moved away from a paternalistic 'doctor knows best' model."
⚖️
noun
Autonomy
The right or condition of self-government; a patient's right to make their own medical decisions. "Informed consent is the foundation of patient autonomy."
✔️
verb
To validate
To recognize or affirm the validity or worth of a person's emotions or opinions. "Even if the fear is irrational, you must validate the patient's feelings first."
⚠️
noun
Coercion
The practice of persuading someone to do something by using force or threats. "Warning a patient of risks is ethical; using fear-mongering borders on coercion."

Ethos, Pathos, or Logos?

Based on the TED-Ed video, decide if the following statements are True, False, or Not Given. Discuss with your partner how these ancient Greek concepts map directly onto modern dental vocabulary (like rapport and validation).

1. Ethos relies entirely on presenting cold, hard statistics to the audience.
✓ FALSE. Ethos is about establishing credibility and character. Using statistics is an example of Logos. In dentistry, Ethos is your white coat, your diploma, and your professional demeanor.
2. The video suggests that using Pathos involves evoking an emotional response, such as pity or anger, from the audience.
✓ TRUE. Pathos targets the audience's emotions. In the clinic, validating a patient's fear or showing empathy is a highly effective use of Pathos to build rapport.
3. Aristotle believed that a persuasive argument only needs one of the three rhetorical appeals to be successful.
✓ FALSE. The video explicitly states that a truly persuasive argument effectively combines all three: establishing trust (Ethos), proving the point (Logos), and connecting emotionally (Pathos).
4. If a dentist uses too much Logos without Pathos, the patient may feel that the dentist is acting in a paternalistic manner.
✓ NOT GIVEN. While this statement is absolutely true in real clinical practice, the video does not specifically discuss dentists, "paternalism," or clinical communication breakdowns.

06 // Advanced Grammar

Elevating Your Relative Clauses

At the C1 level, we move beyond basic who/which/that. To sound truly professional in academic papers, referral letters, and formal patient communication, you need to master prepositions, possessives, and full-clause references.

1. Prepositions + Whom / Which

In formal English, we do not end a sentence with a preposition. We move the preposition before the relative pronoun. We must use whom (for people) or which (for things) — never that or who.

Informal (B2): The specialist who I referred the patient to is excellent.

Formal (C1): The specialist to whom I referred the patient is excellent.

Informal (B2): The research paper that we based the treatment on...

Formal (C1): The research paper on which we based the treatment...

2. Whose (Possession)

Used to show ownership. It replaces his/her/their/its. It can be used for people, organizations, and inanimate objects.

"We must respect the autonomy of the patient whose treatment plan was altered."

(Common error: Do not confuse 'whose' with 'who is' / 'who's'!)

3. Full-Clause "Which"

Sometimes, which doesn't refer to a single noun, but to the entire previous action or situation. It always comes after a comma.

"The assistant validated the patient's fear, which helped build immediate rapport."

('which' refers to the whole act of validating the fear, not just 'the fear' itself.)

08 // Clinical Simulation

The Persuasion Protocol

Return to the scenario from Task 1. The patient is refusing the X-ray for an abscess due to internet radiation fears. You must persuade them to proceed using Ethos, Pathos, and Logos—while seamlessly weaving in advanced relative clauses.

Step 1: Pathos & Validation

Goal: Establish rapport and mitigate their fear.
Requirement: Use a full-clause 'which'.
"I completely understand your hesitation about radiation, which shows you are actively engaged in your health..."

⬇
Step 2: Ethos & Authority

Goal: Gently establish your clinical expertise without being paternalistic.
Requirement: Use preposition + whom/which.
"The safety protocols on which we rely here in the clinic are designed to..."

⬇
Step 3: Logos & Autonomy

Goal: Explain the logic of the abscess risk and offer the final choice.
Requirement: Use whose.
"Patients whose abscesses go untreated face a much higher risk of... However, the final decision is yours."
