04 comprehension.quiz --interactive

True, False, or Not Given?

Watch the talk again if needed, then test what you retained. Click your answer for instant feedback. After each question, use a deductive modal to explain your reasoning to your partner: "This must mean that..." / "It can't be coincidence that..."

1. Adam Alter states that Steve Jobs described the iPad as "extraordinary" and "the best browsing experience you've ever had" at its launch.
// TRUE. Alter quotes Jobs almost verbatim from the 2010 iPad launch. He uses this to set up the irony of Jobs then telling a journalist his own children weren't allowed to use it.
2. According to Alter, the amount of time adults spend in front of screens has doubled since 2007, when the first iPhone was introduced.
// FALSE. Alter says screen time has increased dramatically since 2007 — but he shows it has more than tripled, not merely doubled. The visual in the talk shows free time being almost entirely consumed.
3. Alter's research found that apps focused on relaxation, exercise, and education generally make users feel better, while social media and news apps make users less happy.
// TRUE. He presents data showing exactly this divide — "good" apps vs. apps that make people miserable. The catch: people spend THREE TIMES longer on the apps that make them unhappy.
4. Alter argues that a "stopping cue" is a signal that tells us it is time to stop an activity, and he claims these cues have almost entirely disappeared from modern digital media.
// TRUE. This is one of Alter's central arguments. He contrasts newspapers (which had an end) with infinite scrolling feeds that are specifically engineered to have NO natural stopping point.
5. Alter recommends that people delete all social media apps from their phones and replace them with books and exercise apps.
// NOT GIVEN. Alter does not prescribe deleting apps. His solution is more nuanced — creating "stopping cue" moments (like a phone-free dinner table) and being intentional about screen time. He doesn't make specific app recommendations.
6. Alter mentions a Dutch design firm where desks were designed to physically rise to the ceiling at the end of the workday, forcing employees to disconnect.
// TRUE. He describes this Dutch company as a positive example of businesses creating external "stopping cues" so that workers are physically prevented from continuing to work — a structural solution rather than a willpower-based one.
05 vocab.unlock --context

Words You've Just Heard

These eight words all appeared in Alter's talk or the clinic notice (Task 2). You've already encountered them in context — now hover to lock in their precise C1 definitions. Then find the sentence in the article or talk where each was used.

👁
noun
Stopping cue
A signal — environmental or structural — that naturally indicates an activity has ended and it is time to move on. Alter argues these have been engineered out of modern digital media.
👁
noun
Dogfooding
A business practice of using one's own products to demonstrate confidence in them. Alter uses it to highlight the hypocrisy of tech executives who sell addictive devices but ban them at home.
👁
noun
Screen time
The total amount of time spent using devices with screens — smartphones, tablets, computers. Alter shows this has expanded to consume almost all of adults' discretionary free time since 2007.
👁
adj.
Discretionary
Available for personal use or choice; not committed to any fixed obligation. Alter's "white space" — the sliver of time left after work, sleep, and survival — is our discretionary time.
👁
verb
To be harvested
In the context of data and attention: to be systematically collected and exploited by a third party — usually tech companies — without the individual fully consenting or understanding the process. Passive form used deliberately.
👁
noun
Compliance
Here: the extent to which a policy is followed. Used in the clinic notice — "compliance with the new digital wellness policy." Same word as Lesson 8 (patient dietary compliance), different context.
👁
noun
Behavioural addiction
An addiction to an activity or behaviour (not a substance) — scrolling, gaming, online shopping — driven by immediate reward and compulsive return despite negative consequences. Alter's central concept.
👁
adj.
Designated
Officially assigned or set aside for a specific purpose. From the clinic notice: "designated break times." Commonly used in workplace policies and clinical protocols.

06 grammar.theory --passive_voice

Now We Name It — The Passive Voice

You've already spotted it in Task 2. Now let's formalise the rules. The passive voice shifts the focus from who does something to what happens — essential in clinical writing, formal notices, and academic English.

// PASSIVE FORMULA

Subject + to be (conjugated) + past participle (V3) + [by + agent — optional]

→ The phone was confiscated [by the dentist].

Tense Passive Structure Clinic / Tech Example
Present Simple is / are + V3 Patient data is stored securely on encrypted servers.
Present Continuous is / are being + V3 New booking software is being installed this week.
Present Perfect has / have been + V3 The digital wellness policy has been approved by management.
Past Simple was / were + V3 The patient's phone was confiscated during surgery.
Past Continuous was / were being + V3 Complaints were being filed every week before the new policy.
Past Perfect had been + V3 The new scanner had been ordered three months before it arrived.
Modal + Passive modal + be + V3 All devices must be stored during patient contact hours.

When do we USE the passive?

Reason to Use Passive Example
The agent (doer) is unknown Three complaints were filed last week. (We don't know who filed them)
The agent is obvious/unimportant The patient was referred to a specialist. (obviously, by the dentist)
Formal, objective, scientific/clinical writing A digital detox policy has been implemented across 12 clinics.
To emphasise the action/result over the doer Our attention is being harvested by tech companies. (Alter's framing)

10 pronunciation.passive_stress

Where Does Stress Fall in Passive Phrases?

Based on Avery & Ehrlich's Teaching American English Pronunciation: in passive constructions, the main stress falls on the past participle (V3) — not on the auxiliary verb "be." This is the opposite of how many students instinctively say it.

Present Passive — stress on V3

Written: "It is STORED on the server."
Spoken: "It is STORED /stɔːrd/ on the server."
Not: "It IS stored..." (unless contrasting tenses)
"Data IS stored, not WAS stored."

Past Passive — was/were unstressed

Written: "The phone was CONFISCATED."
Spoken: "/wəz/ kən-FIS-keɪ-tɪd" (was = schwa)
Natural fast speech: "was" reduces to /wəz/
"It /wəz/ DONE before I arrived."

Perfect Passive — "been" reduces

Written: "It has been APPROVED."
Spoken: "It /həz bɪn/ ə-PROOVD"
"has been" → /həzbɪn/ — very fast, unstressed
"The policy /həzbɪn/ CHANGED."

Four Corners Debate

Each corner represents a radical position. You must defend the one you're assigned — whether you personally agree or not. Use today's vocabulary, passive voice, and deductive modals throughout.

// CORNER A — BAN IT

"Phones must be banned from all clinical spaces entirely. Patient data is compromised every time a personal device is used near medical systems. No exceptions."

// CORNER B — TECH IS NEUTRAL

"Technology itself is neutral. The same phones that are being blamed for distraction are used to save lives via emergency protocols. The problem isn't screens — it's poor self-regulation."

// CORNER C — SYSTEMIC PROBLEM

"These addictive patterns were deliberately engineered by tech companies. Individual willpower can't be expected to fix a system-level problem. Regulation must be imposed on the industry."

// CORNER D — DETOX IS PRIVILEGE

"Digital detox is only practiced by the wealthy. For nurses, dentists, and front-line workers, being reachable is required at all hours. The detox conversation has been dominated by people who can afford to disconnect."

📋 Debate rules: Each person speaks for 90 seconds defending their corner. Others may challenge. Use passive voice at least twice. Use a deductive modal at least once. Report what your opponent said using Reported Speech in your rebuttal.
