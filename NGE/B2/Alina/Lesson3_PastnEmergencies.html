Task 4: Reading

The Night Shift Narrative

Read this dramatic story about a dental emergency. Pay close attention to how the author uses different past tenses to build the timeline of events.

It was 8:00 PM on a Friday, and I was cleaning the sterilization room when the clinic phone suddenly rang. A panicked mother said her son had fallen off his skateboard and knocked his tooth out. I told her to put the tooth in milk and drive over immediately.

When they arrived, the boy was crying loudly. His mouth was bleeding because he had bitten his lip during the fall. Dr. Evans examined him and realized the tooth had avulsed cleanly. Because the mother had mitigated the damage by acting so fast, Dr. Evans was able to re-implant the tooth successfully. After they left, we were exhausted; we had been working non-stop for 12 hours.

Task 6: Grammar Theory

Past Simple vs. Past Continuous

We use these two tenses together to show how a longer background action was interrupted by a shorter, sudden event.

X
Past Continuous
I was cleaning...
Past Simple
...when the phone rang.
NOW
Tense Structure Usage in Storytelling
Past Continuous was/were + V-ing Sets the background scene. Describes a longer, continuous action that was in progress.
Past Simple V-ed / Irregular The main events of the story. Short, sudden actions that interrupt the background.

Task 8: Grammar Theory

The Past Perfects

When we are already talking about the past (Past Simple), and we need to talk about something that happened even earlier, we use the Past Perfect. It is "the past of the past".

Past Perfect
The boy had fallen...
Past Simple
...before they arrived.
NOW
Tense Structure Usage Emphasis
Past Perfect Simple had + V3 An action that was completely finished before another past action. (Emphasizes the result).
Past Perfect Continuous had been + V-ing An action that was ongoing up until another past action. (Emphasizes the duration/cause of a visible past state).

Task 10: Pronunciation

Consonant-Vowel Linking

Based on Peter Avery & Susan Ehrlich's Teaching American English Pronunciation, fluent English speakers do not pause between words. When a word ends in a consonant sound (like the past tense -ed) and the next word begins with a vowel, they link together perfectly.

The "Pull-Over" Rule

When linking, imagine pulling the final consonant of the first word over to become the first letter of the second word.

Written: I pulled it out.
Spoken: I pull ‿ di ‿ tout.

Listen & Repeat:

Listen to your teacher read these clinical phrases. Focus heavily on smoothly linking the consonant to the vowel without stopping.

1. He chipped_a tooth.
(chip-pa)

2. We cleaned_it_all.
(clean-di-tall)

3. She worked_on_an emergency.
(work-to-nan)

Task 13: Speaking

The Post-Crisis Debrief Role-Play

Act out a scenario between a Senior Dentist and a Junior Assistant after a very stressful emergency avulsion case.

Dentist: Initiate the debrief. Ask the assistant how they felt during the emergency. Use Past Cont: "How did you feel while we were mitigating the bleeding?"
↓
Assistant: Admit you panicked a bit. Use Past Perfect: "I was scared because I had never seen an avulsion before."
↓
Dentist: Reassure them. "You did great. While I was prepping the tooth, you were preparing the composite perfectly."
↓
Assistant: Ask for advice. "What should I do next time so I don't panic?"
↓
Dentist: Give practical advice based on the video (e.g., control your breathing, remember it is a safe environment).
