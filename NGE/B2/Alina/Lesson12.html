Legal & Forensic Vocabulary

These C1 legal terms are essential when interacting with law enforcement or regulatory bodies. Hover to reveal their definitions.

👁
noun
Subpoena
A writ ordering a person to attend a court or produce documents. Pronounced: /səˈpiː.nə/. "The clinic received a subpoena for the patient's x-rays."
👁
noun/verb
Breach
An act of breaking a law, agreement, or code of conduct. "Sharing the file without a warrant is a breach of confidentiality."
👁
adj.
Forensic
Relating to the application of scientific methods to investigate crime. "Forensic dentistry (odontology) uses bite marks to identify suspects."
👁
verb (passive)
To be indicted
To be formally accused of or charged with a serious crime. Pronounced: /ɪnˈdaɪ.tɪd/. "The doctor was indicted for insurance fraud."
👁
noun
Testimony
A formal written or spoken statement given in a court of law. "The expert testimony provided by the dentist secured the conviction."
👁
verb (passive)
To be summoned
To be officially ordered to appear before a judge or magistrate. "You have been summoned to appear as a witness next Tuesday."
👁
noun
Malpractice
Improper, illegal, or negligent professional behavior. "Failing to report suspected abuse can be considered medical malpractice."
👁
noun
Liability
The state of being legally responsible for something. "To avoid liability, the clinic requested a formal warrant before handing over the data."

06 grammar.theory --advanced_passive

The Advanced Passive Voice

We covered simple passives in Lesson 11. Now let's tackle Continuous, Perfect, and Modal Perfect passives. These are crucial for discussing ongoing investigations, past legal errors, and hypothetical forensic scenarios.

// MODAL PERFECT PASSIVE FORMULA

Subject + modal + have been + past participle (V3)

→ The data should have been protected.

Tense / Structure Form Legal / Clinic Example
Present Continuous Passive
Action happening right now		 is/are being + V3 The suspect is being investigated by the police.
Past Continuous Passive
Action happening at a specific time in the past		 was/were being + V3 The files were being copied when the manager walked in.
Past Perfect Passive
Action completed before another action in the past		 had been + V3 The records had been deleted before the subpoena arrived.
Modal Perfect Passive
Regrets, past deductions, missed opportunities		 modal + have been + V3 The breach should have been reported immediately.
The suspect could have been identified earlier.
Future Perfect Passive
Action will be completed before a future time		 will have been + V3 By tomorrow, the testimony will have been recorded.
⚠️ Watch out: The biggest mistake C1 students make is dropping the "been" in Modal Perfect Passives.
❌ "The files should have protected." (Active meaning: The files themselves should have protected something).
✅ "The files should have been protected." (Passive meaning: Someone should have protected the files).

Active → Advanced Passive

Transform these active sentences into advanced passives. Pay close attention to tenses and modals.

ACTIVE

The detectives are interrogating the dentist right now.

→
PASSIVE (Continuous)

The dentist is being interrogated [by the detectives].

ACTIVE

They were reviewing the security footage when the power went out.

→
PASSIVE (Past Continuous)

The security footage when the power went out.

ACTIVE

Someone had accessed the forensic data before the trial.

→
PASSIVE (Past Perfect)

The forensic data before the trial.

ACTIVE

The clinic should have reported the malpractice sooner.

→
PASSIVE (Modal Perfect)

The malpractice sooner.

ACTIVE

The jury might have misunderstood the expert testimony.

→
PASSIVE (Modal Perfect)

The expert testimony [by the jury].

10 pronunciation.modal_perfect

The "Schwa" in Modal Perfects

In fast, natural spoken English, the auxiliary words in modal perfect passives ("have been") reduce drastically. Mastering this makes your English sound much more fluent and less robotic.

Should have been

Written: "It should have been reported."
Spoken: "It /ʃʊdəvbɪn/ re-POR-ted."

Could have been

Written: "He could have been indicted."
Spoken: "He /kʊdəvbɪn/ in-DIE-ted."

Must have been

Written: "Data must have been stolen."
Spoken: "Data /mʌstəvbɪn/ STO-len."
🎤 Rapid-fire drill: One partner reads an active regret/deduction. The other instantly produces the passive with reduced pronunciation.

Active: "They should have protected the data." → Passive: "The data /ʃʊdəvbɪn/ protected."
Active: "Someone must have deleted the file." → [you say the passive]
Active: "They could have saved the evidence." → [passive]
12 debate.launch --ethics

Four Corners Debate

Where do you draw the line? Defend the corner you are assigned, using advanced passives and legal vocabulary.

// CORNER A — ABSOLUTE SECRECY

"Patient confidentiality must never be breached, regardless of the crime. If patients fear they will be indicted, they won't seek medical help. Doctors are healers, not police."

// CORNER B — MANDATORY REPORTING

"Any suspicion of violent crime should be reported immediately. If a doctor stays silent and the criminal hurts someone else, that injury could have been prevented. The doctor shares the liability."

// CORNER C — LAW OVER ETHICS

"The rule is simple: wait for a subpoena. Patient files are being protected by law until a judge says otherwise. We shouldn't play hero; we just follow legal procedure."

// CORNER D — PUBLIC INTEREST ONLY

"Confidentiality should only be broken if there is a direct, ongoing threat to public safety. Past crimes (like a theft that has already been committed) are not the clinic's business."
