Task 1: Grammar Recap

Modal Verbs — Ability, Possibility & Obligation

Last lesson we covered the core Modal Verbs. Before we build on them with deduction, let's make sure the foundations are solid with these clinical finance scenarios.

  • (Specific past achievement): After restructuring the clinic's payment plan, the manager (be able to / could) reduce outstanding patient debt by 40% in three months. Which modal is correct here, and why?
  • (Strong prohibition): A dental receptionist (must not / don't have to) share patient billing information with third parties without written consent — it violates GDPR and professional ethics.
  • (No obligation vs. prohibition — choose the right one): Staff (must not / don't have to) wear the clinic uniform on non-patient days, but it is strongly encouraged.
  • (Advice): If you are struggling to make ends meet, you consider speaking to a financial advisor before taking out a large bank loan for new equipment.
  • (Possibility — uncertain): The new 3D scanner pay for itself within two years if patient demand for implants continues at the current rate.
🎯 Quick discussion: In your clinic (or any business you know), give a real example of something staff must do, something they should do but don't have to, and something they must not do under any circumstances.

Task 3: Advanced Vocabulary

Hover to Unlock

Eight C1 words spanning personal finance psychology, clinical accounting, and business management. Hover to reveal. Then try to use four of them in one sentence about running a dental practice.

👁
noun
Liquidity
The availability of cash or assets that can be quickly converted to cash. A business with good liquidity can meet its short-term financial obligations without crisis.
👁
noun
Overhead
The ongoing, fixed operating costs of running a business that do not depend on volume of activity — rent, utilities, salaries, and insurance. Every clinic has unavoidable overheads.
👁
noun
Write-off
An amount of bad debt or expense that is officially removed from accounts because it is considered irrecoverable. Clinics sometimes write off unpaid patient bills.
👁
adj.
Solvent
Able to pay one's debts and financial obligations; financially stable. The opposite of insolvent. A solvent clinic has more assets than liabilities.
👁
noun
Pre-commitment
A behavioural economics strategy where you make a binding decision in advance to limit your future choices — for example, automatically transferring savings on payday before you can spend them.
👁
noun
Present bias
The psychological tendency to overvalue immediate rewards and undervalue future ones — leading people to spend today rather than saving for tomorrow, even when they logically know they shouldn't.
👁
noun
Pro-social spending
The act of spending money on other people — gifts, charity, treats for colleagues — rather than on oneself. Research shows it consistently produces greater happiness than self-directed spending.
👁
noun
Depreciation
The reduction in the value of an asset over time due to use, age, or obsolescence. Dental equipment like X-ray machines depreciates annually and must be accounted for in a clinic's budget.

Tasks 5 & 6: Reading & Comprehension

The Clinic That Almost Went Bankrupt

Read these two passages — a real-world clinic finance scenario and a personal finance psychology case. Notice the deductive language used to analyse unknown situations from available evidence.

🏥 Case Study: Clinic Aurora

Clinic Aurora had been running for seven years. On paper, it was busy — they were seeing 30 patients a day. But in March, the clinic's accountant noticed something alarming: the bank balance was critically low and they couldn't cover the next payroll cycle.


The investigation revealed the truth. The clinic's overhead had crept up 40% over two years — a new lease on a prestige address, a receptionist, and cleaning staff. Meanwhile, 12% of invoices were going unpaid beyond 90 days. This outstanding write-off figure must have been growing unnoticed for months.


The owner might have caught it earlier if she had reviewed monthly cash flow statements. "I knew we were busy," she later admitted, "but I assumed that being busy meant being profitable. That can't have been a bigger mistake." She had confused revenue with profit — a classic error in small clinic management.


Within 90 days, a new financial consultant restructured the practice: redundant costs were cut, payment terms were tightened, and a cash reserve could be rebuilt. Clinic Aurora survived — but it was close.

🧠 Psychology of the Clinic Owner

Behavioural economist Richard Thaler described a concept called "mental accounting" — the tendency to put money into separate psychological "buckets" and treat them differently, even when they are all the same money.


A dental practice owner might freely spend €5,000 on a new waiting room chair (from the "clinic improvements" budget) while simultaneously refusing to spend €200 on a team social event (from the "unnecessary expenses" bucket) — even though both come from the same bank account.


This must explain why so many small business owners fall into the same trap: they see money in one account and assume the clinic is healthy, without tracking the holistic picture. Liquidity — not total revenue — is what keeps a business alive.


The same present bias that stops individuals from saving for retirement also stops clinic owners from building a depreciation reserve for equipment replacement. "I'll deal with the scanner replacement next year" is the managerial equivalent of "I'll start saving on Monday."

Task 8: Grammar Theory

Modal Verbs: Deduction & Probability

When we don't have all the facts, we use modal verbs to reason from evidence — just like a detective (or a financial consultant). The modal you choose signals exactly how certain you are.

The Certainty Scale — Present Deduction

can't be ~0% certain I'm sure it's NOT true
might / could be ~30–50% It's possible but uncertain
should be ~70–80% I expect it's true
must be ~95% certain I'm almost sure it IS true
Time Frame Modal Structure Certainty Level Finance / Clinic Example
Present must be + noun/adj ~95% — almost certain it IS true Look at these invoices — there must be an accounting error somewhere.
can't be + noun/adj ~0% — almost certain it is NOT true This can't be the right balance — we had €40k in the account last week.
might / could be ~30–50% — possible but uncertain The shortfall might be due to the unpaid invoices from November.
should be ~70–80% — expected, based on logic If we fixed the write-offs, our cash flow should be positive by Q3.
Past
(+ have + V3)		 must have been ~95% — almost certain past event The financial problem must have been building for at least 18 months.
can't have been ~0% — almost certain past event did NOT happen She can't have checked the accounts properly — this is obvious.
might / could have been ~30–50% — possible past event The clinic might have survived if they'd had a financial reserve.
🚨 Don't confuse deduction with obligation!
"You must be the new accountant." → Deduction (I'm concluding this from evidence)
"You must submit the tax forms by Friday." → Obligation (external rule)
Context is everything. When there is no agent telling someone to do something, must is almost always deduction.

Task 12: Video Comprehension — Interactive

True, False, or Not Given?

Based on Norton's TED Talk, decide on each statement. Click for instant feedback. Then use deductive modals to reason about his claims: "This must mean that..." / "Norton's research can't have been..."

1. Norton argues that the popular idea "money can't buy happiness" is fundamentally correct.
✓ FALSE. Norton explicitly argues the opposite — he says this idea is "wrong," and that people just need to spend money differently. His central thesis is that money CAN buy happiness, if directed toward others.
2. In Norton's Vancouver experiment, people who spent money on others reported being happier than those who spent it on themselves.
✓ TRUE. This was his core experimental finding. People who spent money on others consistently reported higher happiness by the end of the day — regardless of whether they spent $5 or $20.
3. Norton found that the happiness effect of pro-social spending only worked when people gave large amounts — small amounts made no difference.
✓ FALSE. Norton's research showed that the amount did NOT matter — spending $5 on someone else produced similar happiness gains to spending $20. What mattered was the act of giving, not the size.
4. Norton's research extended beyond Canada and showed similar patterns in countries including Uganda.
✓ TRUE. Norton explicitly mentions Uganda as one of the countries where he replicated the experiment. He found the pro-social spending effect was consistent across very different economic contexts.
5. Norton argues that large lottery wins typically make people much happier in the long term because they can now buy everything they want.
✓ FALSE. Norton describes research showing the opposite — lottery winners often end up in debt and with worse friendships, because the money changes social relationships in negative ways.
6. The talk discusses how global Gallup data shows that people in almost every country who donate to charity report higher levels of happiness than those who do not.
✓ TRUE. Norton presents Gallup global data showing a consistent cross-cultural link between charitable giving and self-reported happiness. This was his evidence that the effect was universal, not just a Canadian or Western phenomenon.

Task 13: Pronunciation Focus

Contractions in Deductive Modals

Based on Avery & Ehrlich's Teaching American English Pronunciation, in fast natural speech, deductive modal phrases reduce and contract significantly. This is what separates fluent B2 from natural C1 English.

must have → /mʌstəv/ "mustov"

Written: "She must have known about the debt."
Spoken: "She /mʌstəv/ known about it."
The "have" reduces to a schwa — never fully pronounced.
"That /mʌstəv/ been a shock."

can't have → /kɑːntəv/ "cantov"

Written: "He can't have checked the accounts."
Spoken: "He /kɑːntəv/ checked them."
Americans often say "couldn't have" (/kʊdntəv/) instead.
"It /kʊdntəv/ been his fault."

might have → /maɪtəv/ "mightov"

Written: "The scanner might have caused the debt."
Spoken: "It /maɪtəv/ been the scanner."
Often written informally as "might've" — both are correct.
"They /maɪtəv/ known earlier."
✍️ Pair practice — read these aloud with natural contractions:

1. "She must have been /mʌstəv bin/ reviewing the accounts every month — the numbers are just too clean."
2. "That budget deficit can't have been /kɑːntəv bin/ a surprise to the accountant."
3. "The new payment system might have solved /maɪtəv sɒlvd/ the write-off problem if they'd introduced it earlier."
