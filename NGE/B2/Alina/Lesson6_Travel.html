Task 1: Grammar Recap

Mixed Conditionals Audit ✈️

Before we board today's lesson, let's check your memory on Mixed Conditionals from Lesson 6. Remember the two types:

Type A — Past Action → Present Result

Past Action (had done)
→
Present Result (would be)

"If she had studied abroad, she would speak better English now."

Type B — Present State → Past Result

Present State (were)
→
Past Result (would have done)

"If she weren't so afraid of flying, she would have accepted that job in Tokyo."

  • (Dental context): If the patient (follow) his dentist's advice years ago, his teeth (not/be) in such bad condition right now.
  • (General context): If I (not/be) so risk-averse, I (invest) all my savings in that tech company last year.
  • (Type B): If she (speak) Turkish, she (negotiate) a much better price at the Istanbul clinic last month.

The Dental Tourism Boom

🌍 Why Patients Travel

The global dental tourism market is now worth over $6 billion per year. Patients from the UK, USA, and Scandinavia are increasingly choosing to combine their vacation itineraries with major dental procedures, reporting savings of up to 70% compared to home prices. Popular destinations include Hungary, Thailand, Mexico, and Turkey.


A recent BBC interview captured the sentiment perfectly. One British patient said: "I have been waiting two years for an NHS implant. I flew to Budapest, had the implant done in two days, and toured the city for a week. I will definitely do it again."


His Budapest dentist confirmed: "Our standards are completely identical to the UK. We use the same German equipment and the same Swiss implants. The only difference is the price."

⚠️ The Risks Nobody Talks About

However, dentists in home countries warn strongly against the trend. A UK dental association spokesperson stated: "We see patients every week who are returning with severe infections. Many of these clinics are not accredited and operate under substandard conditions."


They added: "The liability issues are enormous. If something goes wrong six months after you return, your home dentist has to fix the problem, and it can cost three times more to repair foreign work than to have done it correctly here in the first place."


Despite the warnings, patient satisfaction surveys show that 78% of dental tourists report being either satisfied or very satisfied with their experience. The debate continues, but one thing is clear: the industry is only growing.

Task 8: Grammar Theory

Reported Speech — Statements

When we report what someone said earlier, we don't just change the speaker — we shift the tense backwards in time (called backshifting), and we often change pronouns and time expressions.

The "Backshift" Mechanism

Direct Speech
"I work here now."
⏪
Reported Speech
She said she worked there.

The tense moves one step into the past. Time expressions also change.

Direct Speech (What was said) Reported Speech (Backshifted)
Present Simple: "I work in Budapest." Past Simple: She said she worked in Budapest.
Present Continuous: "I am preparing the tray." Past Continuous: He said he was preparing the tray.
Present Perfect: "I have done this procedure before." Past Perfect: She said she had done it before.
Past Simple: "The patient arrived late." Past Perfect: He said the patient had arrived late.
Will: "I will call you tomorrow." Would: She said she would call me the next day.
Can: "I can treat that." Could: He said he could treat that.

Time & Place Expression Changes

Direct Speech → Reported Speech
now → then / at that time
today → that day
tomorrow → the next day / the following day
yesterday → the day before / the previous day
last week → the week before / the previous week
here → there
this (clinic) → that (clinic)
🎯 C1 Rule — When NOT to Backshift: If the reported statement is still true at the time of reporting, you do not have to backshift.
e.g., "Water boils at 100°C." → She said water boils at 100°C. (permanent fact — no backshift needed)
e.g., "I love travelling." → She said she loves / loved travelling. (general truth — both are acceptable)

Task 13: Pronunciation Focus

Reporting Verbs in Fast Speech

Based on Teaching American English Pronunciation (Avery & Ehrlich), reporting verbs like said, told, warned are often reduced and linked to the following word in fast speech. Let's practise.

Linking: "said that"

Written: She said that she worked there.
Spoken: She "sed-thə-t" she worked there.

The "th" in "that" weakens to /ðə/ and links directly to the verb.

Reduction: "told me"

Written: He told me he would call.
Spoken: He "told-mi" he'd call.

"Me" reduces to /mɪ/ and links to the preceding consonant.

Reduction: "would"

Written: She said she would go.
Spoken: She said she'd (/ʃiːd/) go.

"Would" contracts to 'd in natural, fast reported speech.

2. "She said that she would call the patient the following morning."
