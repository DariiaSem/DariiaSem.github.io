Task 2

The "Elevator Pitch" 🗣️

You will meet dozens of people on your first day. You need a smooth, confident 30-second introduction.

Draft your introduction: Include: Your name, where you trained, your current level/role, and expressing enthusiasm.

Introduction #1: for Doctors 

Example: "Hi everyone, I'm Alicja. I’m an OBGYN resident from Poland, and I’m thrilled to be joining the team here in London for my internship. Back home, I’ve been focusing particularly on fetal development. I’m really looking forward to learning how you manage 1st/2nd screening ultrasounds here and contributing to the department. Please feel free to give me any feedback as I get settled into the NHS system!



* I'm really excited to be here and learn/observe from you. I'm sure it will be a huge contribution to my clinical knowledge. 

Say it out loud 3 times until it feels natural.


Introduction #1: for Patients

Good morning, my name is Alicja. I’m an OBGYN resident from Poland visiting here for an internship. I work with Dr. Smith here. Would you mind if I stayed to observe your Ultrasound/Procedure for my clinical training? Please, don't feel pressured – it's completely up to you. 


Some polite phrases: 

- Would you mind if I ....

- Would you be comfortable with me doing ....

- May I have your permission to stay and observe...



Task 3

First Day Logistics 🏥

Before you touch a patient, you need to navigate HR and IT. Match the UK hospital term to its meaning.

Term Meaning
1. Trust ID Badge / Swipe Card Your security pass to open doors and access wards (department)
2. Bleep / Pager The device used to contact you urgently on call.
3. Scrubs Machine / SmartCard The automated machine where you collect clean surgical clothes.
4. Smartcard (Spine IT) The chip-card needed to log into NHS computer systems.
5. Roster / Rota Your work schedule (day shifts, nights, on-calls).
Tasks 4, 5 & 6

Grammar: Extreme Politeness 🇬🇧

In the NHS, speaking too directly is often interpreted as aggressive or rude. We use Indirect Questions and Softening Phrases to maintain good relationships with nurses and admin staff.

  • Direct (Rude): "Where is the locker room?"
  • Indirect (Polite): "Could you tell me where the locker room is?" (Notice the verb goes to the end!)
  • Direct (Bossy): "I need my login details."
  • Softened (Professional): "I was wondering if I could get my login details?"

Phrases: 
Could you tell/advise
I was wondering if
Would you mind telling me

Change these direct orders into polite requests.
"Give me the patient's notes." →
Suggested: "Could you pass me the patient's notes, please?"
"Help me with this ultrasound machine." →
Suggested: "Would you mind helping me with this machine?"
"I want a coffee." (To a colleague going to the kitchen) →
Suggested: "If you're making coffee, could I possibly have one too?"

Task 6: Indirect Questions

Change these direct questions into indirect ones. Remember the word order!

"What time does the morning handover start?"
→ Do you know. what time the morning handover starts .. what time the morning handover starts?
"Where is the consultant's office?"
→ Could you tell me..where the consultant's office is?  where the consultant's office is?
"How do I use the dictation software?"
→ I was wondering if you could show me... how to use the dictation software.

Task 9

Dealing with Accents & Misunderstandings 🗣️

London hospitals have staff from all over the world. You will misunderstand people. How do you handle it professionally without saying "What?"

Instead of "What?" or "Repeat that":
  • "I'm so sorry, I didn't quite catch that."
  • "Could you just run that past me one more time?" - repeat 

  • Discussion:

How comfortable are you asking a senior consultant to repeat themselves? Practice saying: "Sorry to interrupt, Mr. Smith, but could you clarify the dose for me?"

Task 10

British Slang Survival Guide 🇬🇧

Match the common UK slang/colloquialism to its meaning.

British Phrase Meaning
1. "I'm absolutely knackered." Exhausted / Very tired.
2. "I'll put the kettle on." I will boil water to make tea (usually to solve a crisis).
3. "He's just taking the piss/mickey." Making fun of someone / joking around.
4. "I was chuffed to bits." Very pleased or happy about something.
5. "I just need to nip to the loo (WC)." Go to the toilet/bathroom.
6. "It's all gone pear-shaped." It has all gone wrong / failed terribly.
Task 11

Pub Culture & Socializing 🍻

After a tough week, the team often goes to the pub. Understanding the rules is key to team bonding.

  • "Rounds": One person buys drinks for the whole group. Then the next person buys the next round. You must buy a round if someone bought you a drink!
  • "Going Dutch": Splitting the bill exactly in half (usually at restaurants, not pubs).

Discussion: Are you a pub person? How will you handle socializing with the team if you are tired after a 12-hour shift?
