Task 1 — Opening Question

The Question That Opens Everything

No warm-up small talk today. We go straight to the deep end. Take 30 seconds to think, then speak.

Today's Big Question
"Are you the same person you were at 18 — or has something fundamentally shifted?"
Don't answer quickly. Sit with it. What makes you say yes? What makes you hesitate?
Follow-up 1
If you met your 18-year-old self at a dinner party — would you like her? Would she like you?
Take your time. There's no right answer — only your honest one.
Follow-up 2
Think of one habit, belief, or fear you had at 18. Is it still there, hiding somewhere — or is it genuinely gone?
Can habits truly die, or do they just go underground?
Follow-up 3
Do you think people change because they want to — or because life forces them to?
Think of someone you know who changed dramatically. What caused it?

Task 3 — Reading Discovery

Read & Notice

Read this personal essay. Don't think about grammar yet. Just read. What do you notice? What do you feel? We'll look at the language together afterwards.

Personal Essay · Identity

I am sitting in a café I used to go to every Sunday at eighteen. The same wooden tables, the same smell of old coffee and radiator heat. But something is different today — and it is not the café.

At eighteen, I believed that personality was fixed, like bone structure. Some people are warm; others are cold. You simply are what you are, and the world arranges itself around that fact. I had a list — an actual written list — of things I knew to be true about myself: I am impatient. I don't cry at films. I hate small talk. I never ask for help.

Twenty years later, I am crying at a coffee shop, which is embarrassing. I am asking my waiter if he is all right, which surprises me. I am thinking about my mother, who is getting older, which I never used to let myself do.

"The habits are still there, of course — I still talk too fast, still forget to reply to messages, still want every room I enter to be quiet. But wanting things to be quiet is not the same as being afraid of noise. Something has shifted. I just cannot yet name what it is."

Psychologists argue — and the evidence increasingly supports this — that personality traits are not fixed. We are all, slowly and mostly without noticing, in the process of becoming someone slightly different. The question is not whether we change. It is whether we have the courage to notice when we have.

Task 4 — Vocabulary Discovery

Words From the Text — No Dictionary Yet

These 8 words appear in today's lesson. Before looking anything up, talk through what you think each one means based on how it feels and where you've heard it. Then we'll confirm together.

disposition
noun
Your natural tendency to think or behave in a certain way — the part of personality that feels inborn. "She has a cheerful disposition."
introspective
adjective
Tending to examine your own feelings and thoughts carefully. "She became more introspective after the loss."
reinvent
verb
To deliberately change your image, habits, or identity — often after a major life event.
trait
noun
A particular quality or characteristic — usually one that is consistent over time. "Empathy is her most striking trait."
evolve
verb
To change gradually over time — often used when there is no single dramatic turning point.
static
adjective
Unchanging, fixed, not developing. The opposite of dynamic. "Her view of herself was completely static."
perception
noun
The way something is understood, seen, or interpreted — by yourself or by others.
habit
noun
A behaviour that has become automatic through repetition — not always easy to separate from personality.

Task 6 — Grammar: The Rule You Just Discovered

Present Simple vs. Present Continuous

Here is the table. Does it match what you worked out in Task 5? What did you get right? What surprised you?

Present Simple vs. Present Continuous B2 Revision
StructureWhen to use itExample from the essay
Present Simple
I think / She wants / They argue		 Permanent truths, habits, facts, states that don't change moment to moment. Also for stative verbs — feelings, senses, possession, mental states. "Psychologists argue that personality is not fixed."
"She knows herself well." (stative)
Present Continuous
I am thinking / She is getting / They are becoming		 Actions happening right now or around this period. Temporary situations. Changing or developing situations. Also: planned future arrangements. "I am sitting in a café." (right now)
"My mother is getting older." (gradual change)
⚠️ Stative Verbs
know, want, believe, love, hate, see, hear, belong, own, mean, prefer		 Almost never used in continuous form. These verbs describe states, not actions. You cannot "be doing" a state — you simply "have" it. ✅ "I want the room to be quiet."
❌ "I am wanting the room to be quiet."
(Except: "I'm loving this" in informal use — but we learn the rule first)
The Tricky Cases 🤔
think, see, taste, have, feel		 Some verbs can be stative OR active — depending on meaning. The form changes the meaning entirely. "I think she's right." (opinion — stative)
"I am thinking about leaving." (active mental process)
"She has blue eyes." (possession)
"She is having a breakdown." (experience)
Task 7 — Grammar Practice

Fix the Patient — Error Correction ✏️

Something is wrong with each sentence below. Find the error, fix it, and explain why it was wrong. There is only one correct answer — but there may be more than one way to explain it.

  • I am knowing exactly who I am. → Correction: I know — "know" is stative; it describes a mental state, not an action in progress.
  • She always is complaining about her job. → Correction: She always complains — adverbs like "always" signal habit → Present Simple.
  • Right now, he thinks about changing careers. → Correction: he is thinking — "right now" signals an active, temporary mental process → Continuous.
  • This coffee is tasting wonderful. → Correction: tastes — when "taste" describes a quality, it is stative.
  • Water is boiling at 100°C. → Correction: boils — scientific fact → Present Simple. (Unless you mean: "Look! The water is boiling right now!")
  • I am having two sisters and a brother. → Correction: I have — family possession is stative.

Task 9 — Deep Discussion

The Change We Don't Choose 💬

These questions are harder than they look. Take at least 2 minutes on each before moving on.

Question A
Medicine changes people — but in which direction? Has becoming a doctor made you more empathetic, or more protected? More curious, or more certain?
Question B
Some people say: "I am exactly who I've always been." Others say: "I barely recognise myself from 10 years ago." Which feels more true — and which do you think is healthier?
Question C
Is there a version of yourself that you deliberately stopped being — not because it wasn't real, but because it wasn't safe, or useful, or acceptable?
Question D — Philosophy Corner
"The Ship of Theseus": if every plank is gradually replaced, is it still the same ship? Apply this to yourself. If every belief, habit, and relationship has changed since you were born — are you still you?

Task 13 — Pronunciation

Weak Forms & Contractions in Natural Speech 🎙️

In natural spoken English, "am," "is," and "are" almost disappear. Polish speakers often pronounce them too strongly — which sounds overly formal. Practice the weak forms below.

I am changing
→ "I'm changing" /aɪm ˈtʃeɪndʒɪŋ/
In fast speech: "am" becomes /əm/ and merges into the contraction. Never stress "am" unless you mean emphasis: "I am changing, I promise!"
She is thinking
→ "She's thinking" /ʃiːz ˈθɪŋkɪŋ/
The /z/ sound is almost silent in connected speech. Practice: "She's thinking about it" as one smooth phrase, not 4 separate words.
What are you doing?
→ "Whatcha doing?" /ˈwɒtʃə ˈduːɪŋ/
Very informal spoken form. "Are you" blends to /əjə/. You don't need to say this — but you need to understand it when you hear it.
Things are changing
/ˈθɪŋz ə ˈtʃeɪndʒɪŋ/
"Are" weakens to /ə/ — the schwa. This is the most common sound in English and the hardest for Polish speakers to trust. It feels "wrong" because it seems incomplete.
I don't know
→ "I dunno" /aɪ ˈdʌnəʊ/
In natural speech, "don't know" fuses entirely. You will hear this constantly. Say it with confidence — it doesn't mean you're being careless.
She does want to change
/ʃiː dʌz wɒnt/ (emphatic)
Emphatic "does" is fully stressed. This is how English creates emphasis where Polish might use intonation or a particle. Practice the contrast: "She wants" vs. "She DOES want."
🎯
Practice drill Read these aloud at natural speed: "I'm thinking about whether I'm changing." / "She's not sure what she's becoming." / "Things are getting more complicated, aren't they?" / "I don't know, I just don't know." Record yourself if possible — play it back. Does "am/is/are" disappear into the rhythm?
