Lesson 25

The Body Under Pressure:
Stress & Endocrinology

Explore the physiological cascade of prolonged stress. Master Cause & Effect language and Cohesion devices to articulate complex medical scenarios, differentiating between burnout and endocrine dysfunction.

01

The Provocation

"We frequently treat physicians for 'burnout' with mindfulness apps and mandatory wellness modules. However, the physical reality is often a dysregulated HPA axis masquerading as a purely psychological crisis."
💬 Discussion: In your clinical experience, how often are physical symptoms (like extreme fatigue or brain fog) misdiagnosed as purely psychological stress or depression? Where is the line drawn clinically?

Reading: The Case Study

The Resident Who Couldn't Stop
Burnout, or Something Else?

Dr. Ewa, a 38-year-old surgical resident, presented to occupational health with complaints of profound fatigue, unexplained weight gain (6 kg over 3 months), and severe brain fog. She attributed these somatic symptoms entirely to burnout, noting that she was working 70-hour weeks and suffering from constant rumination regarding patient outcomes.

While the psychological burden of her residency was undeniable, her physician suspected a deeper physiological issue. The constant stress of her job had maintained elevated cortisol levels for over two years, creating a significant allostatic load. This prolonged HPA axis dysregulation can disrupt the thyroid's function. Consequently, blood panels were ordered.

The results revealed elevated TSH and low Free T4, confirming hypothyroidism. Additionally, her HbA1c indicated early-stage insulin resistance. Her fatigue did not stem solely from a lack of rest; it was an endocrine cascade triggered by chronic stress. Treating the psychological burnout without addressing the thyroid would have yielded minimal clinical improvement.

Grammar I: Cause & Effect Language

To explain physiological cascades (how one physical event triggers another), B2/C1 speakers use specific verbs and phrases rather than just "because" or "so".

Focusing on the RESULT (A causes B) Focusing on the CAUSE (B is caused by A)
Results in / Leads to
"Elevated cortisol results in sleep disruption."

Triggers / Induces
"Chronic stress triggers an endocrine cascade."

Consequently, / Therefore,
"Her TSH was high; consequently, she felt fatigued." 		Results from / Stems from
"Her fatigue stems from hypothyroidism."

Is attributed to / Is due to
"The weight gain is attributed to insulin resistance."

Is brought on by
"The somatic symptoms were brought on by burnout."

Reading: Academic Excerpt

Cortisol & The Menopause Overlap
Extract from Journal of Occupational Endocrinology

When a female physician reaches her late forties, perimenopause often coincides with peak career demands. During this transitional phase, oestrogen and progesterone fluctuate wildly. These hormonal shifts frequently mimic the exact somatic symptoms of clinical burnout: disrupted sleep, mood swings, and cognitive fatigue.

If a patient presents with these symptoms, a clinician must differentiate between occupational stress and endocrine failure. The former requires rest and psychological intervention, whereas the latter may be effectively managed with HRT.

Furthermore, prolonged cortisol elevation can exacerbate perimenopausal symptoms. Addressing the burnout without treating the hormonal deficit is inefficient; doing so leaves the underlying physiological dysregulation intact. Patients must be counseled that HRT is not a failure of resilience, but a vital medical tool.

Grammar II: Cohesion Devices

Notice the underlined phrases in the text above. In C1 academic and clinical writing, we avoid repeating words by using **cohesion devices** (reference and substitution).

The Former / The Latter

Used to refer back to two specific things mentioned previously in the same order.

"She suffers from burnout and hypothyroidism. The former (burnout) needs rest, while the latter (hypothyroidism) needs medication."

Doing So / To do so

Substitutes an entire action or verb phrase previously mentioned to avoid repetition.

"We must treat the hormonal deficit. Doing so (treating the deficit) will improve her energy."

Such / This / These + Noun

Summarizes a complex idea from the previous sentence into a neat category.

"Oestrogen and progesterone fluctuate wildly. These hormonal shifts mimic burnout."

The Occupational Health Consult

Apply today's vocabulary and grammar in a clinical roleplay differentiating psychological burnout from physical endocrine issues.

Role A: The Occupational Health Doctor

Your colleague (Role B) has come to you claiming they are severely burnt out and need antidepressants. Based on their blood work and age, you suspect their symptoms stem from perimenopause and an allostatic load affecting their thyroid. You need to counsel them on the benefits of HRT and rest.

Grammar Target: Use Cause & Effect language ("results in", "is attributed to") and Cohesion ("doing so").

⬇

Role B: The "Burnt-Out" Colleague

You are convinced your fatigue and rumination are purely psychological due to 80-hour work weeks. You are dismissive of the idea that this is hormonal (perimenopause or thyroid) and resistant to taking HRT, seeing it as a sign of weakness.

Vocabulary Target: Use words like somatic, dysregulation, insulin resistance.

⬇

The Goal

Reach a consensus on a treatment plan that addresses both the psychological stress and the physical endocrine dysfunction.
