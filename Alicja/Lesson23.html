03

Reading: The AI Shift

Algorithms & Autonomy
Will Data Replace Doctors?

It is widely acknowledged that the integration of artificial intelligence into clinical diagnostics is inevitable. Recent studies have demonstrated that machine learning models can detect certain anomalies significantly earlier than human radiologists. This unprecedented level of accuracy is set to revolutionize patient outcomes.

However, this shift raises complex questions regarding legal and ethical liability. If a diagnostic algorithm misses a critical marker, who is to blame—the software developer, the hospital, or the physician who relied on the data? To mitigate these risks, ethical frameworks are currently being developed. It is expected that doctors will transition from primary data analysts to "empathy interpreters," translating algorithmic data into human-centric care plans.

While some speculate that the traditional role of a general practitioner will become obsolete, others argue that AI will simply augment human capabilities, allowing doctors more time to focus on patient autonomy and complex decision-making.

Advanced Passives (Reporting & Impersonal)

In academic, technical, or formal medical contexts (like explaining statistics during a consultation), we avoid using "we" or "they" to sound more objective. Master these advanced passive structures.

Structure Usage & Examples
It + Passive Verb + That
(Impersonal Reporting)		 Used to report general beliefs, facts, or expectations without a specific subject.
"It is believed that AI will augment human skills."
"It has been shown that recovery times are faster with this method."
Subject + Passive Verb + To infinitive
(Personal Reporting)		 Used to focus directly on the subject being reported on.
"The algorithm is expected to reduce errors."
"The new system is said to be unprecedented."
To be + Past Participle
(Passive Infinitive)		 Used after modal verbs or adjectives when the subject receives the action.
"The risks need to be mitigated immediately."
"He doesn't want to be treated by a robot."

07

Reading: Explaining Risk

The Pre-Op Consultation
Informed Consent in Plain English

When gaining informed consent for an upcoming procedure, a doctor must translate complex statistical data into transparent, accessible English. The goal is to provide the patient with true autonomy without causing unnecessary panic.

Using impersonal structures helps soften difficult information. Instead of saying, "You have a 5% chance of dying," a skilled communicator might say, "It has been documented that in a small percentage of cases, complications can arise." They must mitigate anxiety while remaining legally sound regarding liability.

Furthermore, as we look to the future, explaining risk will involve speculating about hybrid procedures. Doctors will have to explain that while they will be overseeing the surgery, a robotic system is bound to handle the precise incisions. Navigating this blend of human empathy and mechanical precision is the new frontier of medical communication.

08

Speculating about the Future

To sound fully fluent at a C1 level, you must move beyond simple "will" and "going to". Use these advanced forms to discuss technological changes and future scenarios.

Future Continuous

will be + -ing
Used for actions that will be in progress at a specific time in the future, or expected trends.

"By 2030, robots will be performing most routine surgeries."
"I will be monitoring your vitals constantly during the procedure."

Future Perfect

will have + past participle
Used to declare that an action will be completed BEFORE a certain point in the future.

"By the time you wake up, we will have removed the tissue."
"In ten years, AI will have replaced many administrative tasks."

Advanced Speculation Phrases

Used to express degrees of certainty about the future without using 'will'.

  • is bound to: highly certain. (It is bound to happen.)
  • is set to: scheduled/ready. (The software is set to launch.)
  • is likely to: probable. (It is likely to cause some pain.)
  • could well: possible. (This could well be obsolete soon.)

The Consultation

This is the ultimate test of today's vocabulary and grammar. You are gaining informed consent for an innovative, AI-assisted procedure.

Role A: The Medical Professional

You need to explain a complex, AI-assisted procedure to a nervous patient. You must be transparent about the risks, explaining that while complications are rare, they are possible. You must explain how the technology will mitigate human error, while assuring them you retain autonomy over the final decisions.

Grammar Target: Use impersonal passives ("It has been shown that...") and the Future Continuous ("We will be monitoring...").

⬇

Role B: The Anxious Patient

You are terrified of the idea of a "robot" or "algorithm" operating on you. You want to know who holds the liability if something goes wrong. You are worried that traditional human care has become obsolete.

Prompting Question: "What exactly is bound to happen when I go to sleep?"

⬇

The Goal

Gain the patient's informed consent without using heavy medical jargon, ensuring they feel secure about the unprecedented technology.
