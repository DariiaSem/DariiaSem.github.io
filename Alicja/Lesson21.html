02 // Grammar Input

Advanced Cohesion (Revision + Extension)

To write and speak at a C1 level, your sentences must link together seamlessly across paragraphs. Let's look at three powerful cohesive devices.

1. Pronoun Reference

Using pronouns (this, these, those, it, they) to refer back to a complex idea in the previous sentence.

"Many tourists experience severe re-entry shock. This psychological dislocation can last for months."

2. Substitution

Using words like one, ones, do, so to avoid repeating the exact same noun or verb phrase.

"I usually avoid the crowded resort beaches. I much prefer the isolated ones."

3. Lexical Chains

Connecting paragraphs by using a progression of related vocabulary (synonyms, hyponyms, associations) rather than repeating the same word.

Paragraph 1 ends with: "...the long journey."
Paragraph 2 begins with: "Such an exhausting trek..."

03 // Cohesion Analysis

The Gap Year Fantasy

Read the travel essay below. Notice how the highlighted words create links across sentences and paragraphs. Try to deduce the meaning of the bolded vocabulary.

Editorial • Travel & Psychology

The modern "gap year" is often marketed as a profound pilgrimage of self-discovery. Young adults pack their bags, fueled by intense wanderlust, expecting a transformative sojourn across continents. However, the reality of this itinerant lifestyle is frequently less glamorous than the filtered images suggest.

This disparity between expectation and reality stems from a misunderstanding of travel. True cultural immersion requires facing significant discomfort—navigating foreign bureaucracies, sleeping on uncomfortable buses, and dealing with provincial attitudes. Resort tourism protects you from these challenges; authentic travel guarantees them.

Furthermore, the hardest part of the voyage is often the return. Many backpackers report feeling a profound sense of psychological dislocation when they arrive home. Such re-entry shock can be harder to navigate than any foreign city one has visited.

Having attitudes or opinions supposedly typical of people outside the capital; unsophisticated or narrow-minded. "They found the villagers' views quite provincial."

07 // Clinical Simulation

The Colleague's Misunderstanding

Use your persuasive skills and new vocabulary to navigate a difficult conversation with a colleague.

Context: You have just returned from a difficult, exhausting medical volunteering trip (or a very rugged travel experience) in a developing country. You suffered a lot of discomfort. You return to your clinic in Poland, still feeling a sense of dislocation.

Your colleague (the teacher), who has never left Europe and prefers luxury resort tourism, says to you in the breakroom: "Wow, you look terrible for someone who just had a 4-week holiday. I told you going there was a bad idea. Why didn't you just go to an all-inclusive in Spain like normal people?"

Your Task:
  • Explain the difference between a sojourn/pilgrimage and "resort tourism".
  • Defend the value of cultural immersion and discomfort.
  • Use hedging so you don't sound arrogant (e.g., "One could argue that...").
