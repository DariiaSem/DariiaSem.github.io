The Relationship Audit:
What We Expect From Others

Explore the psychology of connection, attachment styles, and why some friendships expire. Master advanced grammar by fully exploring Passive Voice and Reported Speech to de-escalate conflict.

01

The Audit

"We are the average of the five people we spend the most time with. Yet, we rarely audit those relationships to see if they are rooted in genuine connection, or merely shared history and obligation."

The Hot Seat

  • Mentally map your 5 closest relationships (friends, family, colleagues). How many of them are maintained out of habit rather than active, mutual growth?
  • What is the difference between a "low maintenance" friendship and a "neglected" one?
  • Have you ever realized that you might be the toxic friend in a specific dynamic?

03

The Pop-Psych Debate

Are "Love Languages" Ruining Us?
The weaponization of therapy speak

In recent years, it has been widely claimed that understanding "The 5 Love Languages" (Acts of Service, Words of Affirmation, Quality Time, Gifts, Physical Touch) is the key to relationship survival. However, many clinical psychologists are now pushing back.

Therapists report that this framework is increasingly being weaponized. Rather than fostering vulnerability, patients use their "love language" as a rigid demand. For example, a partner might say, "You aren't buying me gifts, and since that is my love language, you are failing me." It is argued that this creates an environment of entitlement rather than reciprocity.

Similarly, the concept of attachment styles (anxious, avoidant, secure) is frequently misused. Individuals diagnose their friends and partners, using these labels to excuse their own codependency or to justify ending a relationship prematurely due to minor disenchantment. Are we over-analyzing our connections to the point of attrition?

Debate Room

  • Do you agree that therapy concepts (like love languages and attachment styles) are being "weaponized" in everyday arguments?
  • Is it helpful to label a friend as "avoidant", or does it just put them in a box?

Grammar Masterclass: Passive Voice

When we discuss relationship conflicts, using the Active Voice can sound highly accusatory ("You ruined the friendship"). Using the Passive Voice allows us to distance ourselves, speak objectively, and de-escalate tension.

Concept & Usage Rules & Examples
1. Avoiding Blame (Distancing)
Removing the 'Agent'		 Instead of attacking the person, focus on the action that occurred.

Aggressive (Active): "You broke my trust."
Diplomatic (Passive): "My trust was broken."

Aggressive: "You made a mistake."
Diplomatic: "Mistakes were made."
2. Impersonal Passives
Reporting general beliefs		 Used to introduce theories or rumors without taking personal ownership of the statement. Uses verbs like say, believe, claim, expect.

Format A (It): It is widely claimed that long-distance friendships fail.
Format B (Subject): Long-distance friendships are expected to suffer from attrition.
3. Verbs with Two Objects
(give, tell, offer, promise)		 When a verb has a person and a thing as objects, English prefers making the person the subject of the passive sentence.

Active: They told me lies.
Passive: I was told lies. (Much more natural than "Lies were told to me").
Passive: She was given an ultimatum.
05

Grammar Masterclass: Reported Speech

When recounting past arguments or analyzing why a friendship expired, we use Reported Speech. Because the speech happened in the past, we must "backshift" the tenses.

Concept The Rules of Backshifting
Tense Backshifting Move the tense one step back into the past when the reporting verb is in the past.

Present Simple ➔ Past Simple: "I need space."
➔ She said she needed space.

Present Perfect ➔ Past Perfect: "We have drifted apart."
➔ He noted they had drifted apart.

Past Simple ➔ Past Perfect: "I broke the boundary."
➔ He admitted he had broken the boundary.
Pronouns & Time Markers You must adjust the perspective based on when and where you are speaking.

"I can't do this tomorrow."
➔ She said she couldn't do that the next day.
(Here ➔ there, now ➔ then, yesterday ➔ the day before).
Advanced Reporting Verbs Instead of just using "said", B2/C1 English uses specific verbs that capture the tone.

Verb + (that): He admitted that he was codependent. (complain, claim, deny)
Verb + Object + to-inf: She advised me to end it. (warn, encourage, persuade)
Verb + Gerund (-ing): He suggested taking a break. (deny, recommend, admit)

Reported: He admitted that he felt suffocated by the enmeshment, and advised her to give him space the following day.

07

Therapy Room: Case Studies

Read the following micro-cases of friendships experiencing attrition. Discuss who is at fault, and formulate a sentence using Passive Voice or Reported Speech to summarize the problem.

Case 1: The One-Sided Vent

Mark calls David every week to complain about his terrible job and his dating life. David listens for hours. However, when David tries to talk about his own recent promotion, Mark changes the subject back to himself within two minutes.

Target: Discuss "Reciprocity". Formulate a passive sentence about David.

Case 2: The Resentful Roommate

Sarah and Lisa have lived together for three years. Sarah always buys the groceries, cleans the apartment, and pays the bills, silently hoping Lisa will notice and say "thank you" (Words of Affirmation). Lisa never notices. Sarah is furious.

Target: Discuss "Love Languages as weapons". Formulate reported speech for Sarah.
08

The Friendship Breakup

Apply today's vocabulary and grammar in a heavy, emotionally charged roleplay. You must navigate a friendship breakup using diplomacy.

Role A: The Initiator

You have decided to end a 10-year platonic friendship. The relationship has become a source of toxic enmeshment and lacks reciprocity. You feel drained. You want to explain your feelings kindly but firmly.

Grammar Target: Use Reported Speech to address past issues calmly (e.g., "When you told me that..., I felt..."). Use Passive Voice to distance blame ("Boundaries were crossed").

⬇

Role B: The Surprised Friend

You are shocked by this conversation. You thought your bond was incredibly strong, though you admit you have been relying on them heavily lately. You feel a sense of intense disenchantment that they are giving up so easily.

Grammar Target: Use Impersonal Passives to express your surprise (e.g., "It was always believed that we were best friends... I was told that...").

⬇

The Goal

Have an honest, respectful "friendship breakup" conversation without resorting to active voice anger ("You did this!"). Seek closure.
