What We Expect From Others

03

Reading: Friendships That Expire

The Expiration Date of Platonic Love
Why Some Ties Are Not Meant to Last

It is widely believed that romantic relationships are fragile, yet platonic friendships are often expected to endure a lifetime. However, therapists argue that many friendships suffer from attrition—they simply wear out over time as our core values shift.

When patients are asked why they hold onto toxic friendships, they often cite a shared history. However, longevity does not equal health. Many of these bonds are characterized by a severe lack of reciprocity, or worse, toxic enmeshment, where one person acts as the eternal savior and the other as the perpetual victim. This dynamic inevitably breeds disenchantment.

In her latest seminar, a leading psychologist claimed that our attachment styles dictate who we attract platonically. She suggested that individuals with anxious attachment often seek out friendships that border on codependency. Breaking up with a friend requires immense vulnerability, as there is no societal script for it. Yet, auditing our social circles is a crucial component of psychological hygiene.

Grammar Revision: Passive Voice

The Passive Voice is used when the action is more important than who did it, or when the agent is obvious, unknown, or irrelevant. In B2/C1 English, we use advanced passive structures frequently in formal and psychological writing.

Rule / Structure Explanation & Examples
Basic Form
Subject + BE + Past Participle (V3)		 The object of the active sentence becomes the subject of the passive sentence.
Active: Toxic dynamics breed disenchantment.
Passive: Disenchantment is bred by toxic dynamics.
Verbs with Two Objects
(give, offer, ask, tell)		 When a verb has two objects (e.g., "someone gave her advice"), it's more natural in English to make the person the subject.
Active: The therapist gave him an ultimatum.
Passive: He was given an ultimatum. (More common than "An ultimatum was given to him").
Impersonal Passives
(believe, say, expect, claim)		 Used to express general opinions or facts without stating who specifically believes them.
Pattern 1 (It): It is widely believed that platonic bonds endure.
Pattern 2 (Subject): Platonic bonds are expected to endure.
Pattern 3 (Past action): She is claimed to have suffered from codependency.
Passive with Modals
Modal + be/have been + V3		 Present/Future: Friendships can be audited.
Past: The friendship should have been ended years ago.

Grammar Revision: Reported Speech

When we recount what someone else said, we use Reported Speech. Because the speech happened in the past, we typically "backshift" the tenses and change pronouns and time markers.

Concept Rules & Examples
Tense Backshifting Move the tense one step back into the past when the reporting verb (said, told, claimed) is in the past.
Present Simple ➔ Past Simple: "I need space." ➔ He said he needed space.
Present Perfect ➔ Past Perfect: "We have drifted apart." ➔ She noted they had drifted apart.
Past Simple ➔ Past Perfect: "I broke the boundary." ➔ He admitted he had broken the boundary.
Pronouns & Time Markers Adjust based on perspective and time.
"I can't do this tomorrow." ➔ She said she couldn't do that the next day.
(Here ➔ there, now ➔ then, yesterday ➔ the day before).
Advanced Reporting Verbs Instead of just using "said", C1 English uses specific verbs that capture the tone. They follow different structural rules:
Verb + (that): claim, admit, insist, deny, complain.
He admitted that he was codependent.
Verb + Object + to-infinitive: advise, warn, encourage, persuade.
The therapist advised her to audit her friendships.
Verb + Gerund (-ing): suggest, deny, recommend, admit.
She suggested setting boundaries.

10

The Breakup Roleplay

Breaking up with a friend requires immense vulnerability. Practice navigating this difficult conversation using today's grammar and vocabulary.

Role A: The Initiator

You have decided to end a 10-year friendship. The relationship has become a source of codependency and lacks reciprocity. You feel drained. You want to explain your feelings kindly but firmly.

Grammar Target: Use Reported Speech to address past issues calmly (e.g., "When you told me that..., I felt...").

⬇

Role B: The Surprised Friend

You are shocked by this conversation. You thought your bond was incredibly strong, though you admit you have been relying on them heavily lately. You feel a sense of disenchantment that they are giving up so easily.

Grammar Target: Use Passive Voice to express your surprise (e.g., "I was completely caught off guard by this...").

⬇

The Goal

Have an honest, respectful "friendship breakup" conversation without resorting to anger. Seek closure.
