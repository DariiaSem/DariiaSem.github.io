Task 2 — Quick Revision: Lesson 1 Grammar

Still With Us? Present Simple & Continuous ♻️

Before we go further — a fast check. No pressure. Spot the error in each sentence and say why it's wrong. Then correct it.

  • "I am knowing this song — it's from the 90s." → Fix: I know — "know" is stative.
  • "She works on a project about nostalgia right now." → Fix: is working — temporary, ongoing action in this period.
  • "The photo is belonging to my grandmother." → Fix: belongs — possession is stative.
  • "I think about changing my travel plans." (right now, actively) → Fix: am thinking — active mental process in this moment.
✅

Task 3 — Reading

The Essay: What a Photo Cannot Hold

Read this essay once for pleasure. No grammar hunting yet. Just read and feel. What does it make you think of?

Essay · Memory & Photography

I have been to Lisbon twice. The first time, I was twenty-four and had never heard the word "nostalgia" applied to a place I had never known before. The second time, I was thirty-seven, standing in the same square, trying to photograph what I remembered — and failing completely.

The thing about photographs is that they have always lied, just very beautifully. They cut out the smell of the café, the argument you had that morning, the way the light changed as you stood there for ten minutes trying to get the shot right. The photo keeps the light. It discards the ten minutes.

"I have never managed to photograph what I actually felt. I have only managed to photograph what I was standing in front of."

Scientists who study memory — and this has surprised even them — now believe that every time we remember something, we change it slightly. We have been rewriting our own histories since the day we were born. The photograph, paradoxically, might be the only stable version of the story. The problem is that it was never the full story to begin with.

My grandmother died in 2014. I have not stopped thinking about her since. But the memories I have of her — I cannot tell you now which ones I have actually lived, and which ones I have constructed from photographs.

Think while you read
The writer says photos "have always lied." Do you agree — or is a photo just a different kind of truth? What do photos choose to tell?
Notice the bolded verbs. Some feel "finished." Some feel "still connected to now." Can you feel the difference before we talk about it?
The last paragraph is very personal. Does it remind you of anything in your own life? Which memories are yours, and which came from photographs?

What's the real difference? Past Simple says: it happened. It's in a box, in the past. Present Perfect says: it happened, and it still echoes. The finished-ness is not the point — the relevance to now is the point. That's why "I have been to Lisbon" and "I went to Lisbon" can both be true, but mean something different in a conversation.
Task 5 — Grammar: The Rule You Just Felt

Past Simple vs. Present Perfect

Here it is, laid out. Check it against what you discovered. What did you already know intuitively? What is genuinely new?

Past Simple vs. Present Perfect B2 Revision — The Echo Test
Structure
When to use it
Example — memory & travel
Past Simple
I went / She took / They visited
A finished action at a specific time. The time is mentioned, implied, or understood. The past feels closed. No echo into the present.
"I went to Lisbon in 2019."
"She took that photo on a Tuesday."
"He died in 2014."⚠️ Clues: yesterday, last year, in 2010, ago, when I was young, at that moment
Present Perfect
I have gone / She has taken / They have visited
A past action connected to the present. No specific time given. What matters is the result, experience, or relevance now. The echo is still audible.
"I have been to Lisbon twice." (life experience — still part of who I am)
"I have not stopped thinking about her." (started in past, still true now)
"Scientists have discovered something new." (recent, relevant today)⚠️ Clues: ever, never, just, already, yet, since, for, recently, it's the first time
Tricky: The British vs. American split
Did you just / Have you just
British English uses Present Perfect for recent events even without a time marker. American English often uses Past Simple instead. Both are "correct" — but know which register you're in.
🇬🇧 "I have just arrived."
🇺🇸 "I just arrived."

In medical and professional UK English — always use the British form.
The "for / since" Test
Both live in Present Perfect
For = a duration. How long something has been true.
Since = a starting point. When it began.
"I have lived in Warsaw for six years."
"I have lived in Warsaw since 2018."
"I haven't slept properly since medical school."

"I have visited Kraków many times."
No time given. Connected to now. ✓
Since — a starting point

Started in 2000 → still true now.
The action/state began at a point in the past — and has continued, unbroken, all the way to this moment.

Task 8 — Vocabulary Discovery

Words That Live in Memory

These 8 words all appeared in or around today's reading. Before looking at definitions — go around the room and say what you think each word means. Then check. How close were you?

nostalgia
noun
A sentimental longing for a past that may never have existed exactly as we remember it. From Greek: "nostos" (return) + "algos" (pain). Literally: the ache of homecoming.
ephemeral
adjective
Lasting for only a very short time. From Greek "ephemeros" — "lasting only a day." A sunset is ephemeral. A photograph pretends it doesn't have to be.
vivid
adjective
Producing powerful, lifelike mental images. A vivid memory feels more real than reality. A vivid dream leaves you confused about what happened when you wake up.
reminisce
verb
To indulge in enjoyable recollections of past events — usually with warmth, sometimes with selective editing of the difficult parts.
sentimental
adjective
Excessively emotional about the past — sometimes used dismissively, but it can also mean: willing to feel things fully. Not the same as "nostalgic," which is specifically about time.
curate
verb
Originally: to care for a museum collection, choosing what matters and how to present it. Now also: to select, organise and present your life — on Instagram, in conversation, in memory.
fleeting
adjective
Passing swiftly; lasting only a moment. A fleeting glance. A fleeting feeling of happiness. The word itself feels quick — it almost disappears as you say it.
document
verb
To record in detail — for evidence, memory, or posterity. When a doctor documents, it is clinical and precise. When a photographer documents, it is selective and interpretive. Same word, very different relationship to truth.

Task 12 — Deep Discussion

The Questions That Have No Easy Answers 💬

Take at least 3 minutes on each. Don't rush to a conclusion. The best discussions end with more questions than they started with.

Question A
Have you ever remembered something from your childhood — vividly, clearly — and then discovered it didn't happen the way you remembered it? What changed?
Use Present Perfect: "I have always believed…" / "I have never been sure whether…"
Question B
Travel, love, grief — which of these creates the most vivid memories? Why do we remember painful things so clearly, and happy days as a general blur?
Question C — Philosophy Corner
If a memory has been unconsciously edited every time you have recalled it — is it still your memory? Or has it become a story you tell about yourself?
This connects to L1's Ship of Theseus. Can we apply the same logic to memories?
Question D — Medical angle
Doctors rely on patient history — what the patient remembers. But if memory is reconstructive, what does that mean for the stories patients tell you? Have you ever caught a clear discrepancy?

Task 15 — Pronunciation & Writing

The Sound of the Present Perfect 🎙️

The auxiliary "have" almost disappears in spoken English. Polish speakers often over-pronounce it — which sounds formal and slightly unnatural in conversation. Listen and repeat.

I have been there
→ "I've been there" /aɪv bɪn ðeə/
"Have" collapses into 've. Never say "I HAVE been there" unless you are correcting a false assumption someone has made.
Have you ever visited?
→ "Have you ever…" /həv juː ˈevə/
In questions, "have" stays slightly more audible — but it's still reduced. The stress lands on "ever", not "have."
She has just arrived
→ "She's just arrived" /ʃiːz dʒʌst əˈraɪvd/
"Has" contracts to 's and merges. In speech, "just" carries the stress — the auxiliary is almost invisible.
I haven't decided yet
→ /aɪ ˈhævnt dɪˈsaɪdɪd jɛt/
The negative "haven't" is always fully pronounced — no reduction here. Stress falls on "haven't." Practice: slow → fast → natural.
We've never spoken
→ /wiːv ˈnevə ˈspəʊkən/
"We have" → "We've." Practice the contraction until it feels natural, not like a mistake. "Never" carries the stress — it's the emotional weight of the sentence.
How long have you lived here?
→ /haʊ lɒŋ həv juː lɪvd hɪə/
"Have" reduces to /həv/. The stress lands on "long" and "lived." Practice as one smooth phrase, not 7 separate words.
