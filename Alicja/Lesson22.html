03 // Editorial Reading

The Sabbatical Fantasy

Read the travel essay below. Notice how the highlighted words create links across sentences and paragraphs.

The modern "medical sabbatical" is often marketed as a profound pilgrimagepielgrzymka of self-discovery. Burned-out doctors pack their bags, fueled by intense wanderlust, expecting a transformative sojourn across continents. However, the reality of this itinerant lifestyle is frequently less glamorous than the filtered Instagram images suggest.

This disparity between expectation and reality stems from a misunderstanding of travel. True cultural immersion requires facing significant discomfort—navigating foreign bureaucracies, sleeping on uncomfortable buses, and dealing with provincial healthcare systems. Resort tourism protects you from these challenges; authentic travel guarantees them.

Furthermore, the hardest part of the voyage is often the return. Many professionals report feeling a profound sense of psychological dislocation when they arrive back at their sterile European hospitals. Such re-entry shock can be harder to navigate than any foreign city one has visited.

05 // Grammar Theory

Cohesion: Easy Mode 🧩

To write and speak at a C1 level, your sentences must link together logically. Here are the three simple rules to avoid sounding like a robot.

👈

Rule 1: The "Pointer" Words (Pronoun Reference)

Instead of repeating a whole idea, point back to it using This / These / Such + Summary Word.

"Patients often wait for hours in the ER. This delay causes anger."
🔄

Rule 2: The "Replacers" (Substitution)

Don't repeat nouns or verbs. Replace them with One / Ones / Do so.

"I prefer tropical climates to cold ones. If I can travel south, I will do so."
🔗

Rule 3: The "Vocabulary Chain" (Lexical Chains)

Don't use the same word in every sentence. Move from a specific word to a synonym or category.

"The surgeon arrived. The specialist looked tired. The doctor sighed."

09 // Grammar Revision

Hedging in Argumentation 🛡️

Before our debate, remember to soften your statements to sound objective and academic, just like we practiced in previous lessons.

  • ✔️ Instead of: "Travel is a privilege."
  • ➔ Use: "It could be argued that travel is inherently a privilege."

  • ✔️ Instead of: "Resorts are bad for the local culture."
  • ➔ Use: "Evidence suggests that resort tourism tends to negatively impact local culture."

11 // Clinical Simulation

The Colleague's Misunderstanding

Use your persuasive skills and new vocabulary to navigate a difficult conversation with a colleague.

Context: You have just returned from a difficult, exhausting medical volunteering trip in a developing country. You suffered a lot of discomfort. You return to your clinic in Poland, still feeling a sense of dislocation.

Your colleague (the teacher), who has never left Europe and prefers luxury resort tourism, says to you in the breakroom: "Wow, you look terrible for someone who just had a 4-week holiday. I told you going there was a bad idea. Why didn't you just go to an all-inclusive in Spain like normal people?"

Your Task:
  • Explain the difference between a sojourn/pilgrimage and "resort tourism".
  • Defend the value of cultural immersion and discomfort.
  • Use hedging so you don't sound arrogant (e.g., "One could argue that...").
