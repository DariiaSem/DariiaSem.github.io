Task 2 — Grammar Revision

Modals of Deduction — Rapid Revision 🔁

From Lesson 12. This grammar is essential in emergency medicine — deducing what is happening before test results arrive.

ModalCertaintyEmergency Example
must be90–100% certain (positive logic)"The rhythm on the monitor must be VF — there's no palpable pulse."
can't be90–100% certain (negative logic)"It can't be a simple faint — he's been down for over 3 minutes."
might / could be30–60% (genuine uncertainty)"The cause could be a PE — she was post-operative."
must have beenCertain — about the PAST"He must have been in VF for at least 5 minutes before we arrived."

Task 3 — Clinical Framework

The Chain of Survival 🔗

Every 10-second delay in defibrillation reduces survival by ~10%. The Chain of Survival is the international framework for maximising out-of-hospital cardiac arrest (OHCA) outcomes. Study each link.

1
Early Recognition
Recognise cardiac arrest. Call 999/112. Don't wait for a pulse.
→
2
Early CPR
30:2 ratio. 100–120 compressions/min. Hard, fast, full recoil.
→
3
Early Defibrillation
AED shock within 3–5 min doubles or triples survival.
→
4
Post-Resuscitation Care
Targeted temperature management, PCI, ICU admission.

Task 6 — Case Study

The Warsaw Supermarket Collapse 🏪

Outcome

At 11:17, Mr. Kowalski, a 61-year-old retired electrician with a known history of hypertension and smoking, collapsed without warning in the dairy aisle. A witness reported he made a brief gurgling noise — agonal breathing — and then became completely unresponsive. A shop assistant called 112. Crucially, a 34-year-old woman who had completed a basic CPR course two years prior began chest compressions within 90 seconds of collapse.

When the ambulance crew arrived 7 minutes later, the patient remained in ventricular fibrillation (VF). An AED shock was delivered. After a further 2 minutes of CPR, a Return of Spontaneous Circulation (ROSC) was achieved. Mr. Kowalski was transported to the cath lab and was found to have a 95% occlusion of the LAD artery. He underwent successful PCI and was discharged neurologically intact 6 days later.

Q1. What is "agonal breathing" and why is it clinically dangerous?

Answer: Slow, gasping irregular breaths caused by a dying brainstem. It is dangerous because bystanders often mistake it for normal breathing and delay CPR. Remember: if in doubt, compress.

Q2. What does ROSC stand for and how is it confirmed clinically?

Answer: Return of Spontaneous Circulation — confirmed by a palpable central pulse (carotid/femoral), a narrow-complex rhythm on the monitor, and ideally a rising end-tidal CO₂ reading.

Q3. The bystander's CPR quality was imperfect — but she still helped. Discuss.

Answer / Discussion point: Some CPR, even imperfect, dramatically increases survival over none. Studies show bystander CPR doubles/triples survival. The "Good Samaritan" principle applies in most countries. Discuss Polish law regarding bystander duty to act.

Q4. What is the significance of VF as the presenting rhythm (vs. asystole)?

Answer: VF is a shockable rhythm — it responds to defibrillation. Asystole and PEA are non-shockable. Patients presenting in VF have a significantly better prognosis if defibrillated promptly, which is why early AED access is critical.

The 4Hs and 4Ts — Reversible Causes

Every arrest must prompt a search for reversible causes. Can you name all 8? 4Hs: Hypoxia, Hypovolaemia, Hypo/Hyperkalaemia, Hypothermia. 4Ts: Tension pneumothorax, Tamponade (cardiac), Toxins, Thrombosis (PE/coronary). Discuss how you would identify and treat each one in a real arrest scenario.

Task 8 — Grammar Theory

Commanding a Crash Scene 🗣️

Emergency communication has its own grammar. Speed and clarity save lives. There are two key structures you must master.

StructureHow it worksCrash Team Example
Imperative
Direct command		 Use the base verb. No subject needed. Fastest, most direct. Appropriate when speed is critical. "Compress. Stop. Stand clear. Charge. Shock."
Imperative Ellipsis
Telegraphic speech		 Only the critical nouns survive. Verbs, articles, and connectives are all dropped. Used for rapid narration or instrument calls in theatre. "Airway clear. Two breaths. Pulse check. No output. Adrenaline now. Rhythm check in 2."
Passive Voice (Clinical)
For reporting & handover		 Used when documenting or handing over a resuscitation. Depersonalises actions. Essential for legal records. "CPR was initiated at 14:32. Adrenaline 1mg IV was administered. ROSC was achieved at 14:49."
💡
The Register Shift

Notice how the same event is described in three different registers: 1) During the arrest (imperatives, ellipsis — fast, direct), 2) Immediately after (questions, assessment language), 3) In the notes / handover (passive, past tense, full sentences). Practise shifting between these three registers fluidly.

Task 13 — Pronunciation Focus

Medical Word Stress — Fast Speech in Emergencies 🎙️

Based on Avery & Ehrlich's Teaching American English Pronunciation. In emergencies, word stress errors cause dangerous miscommunication. Focus on these patterns today.

📐
The Rule: Noun vs. Verb Stress Shift

In English, many medical words shift their stress depending on whether they function as a noun or verb. This creates confusion in fast speech. Listen to how your teacher says each pair — and practise the difference deliberately.

RE-cord (n)
→ re-CORD (v)
"Check the RE-cord" vs. "re-CORD this arrest"
COM-press (n)
→ com-PRESS (v)
"Apply the COM-press" vs. "com-PRESS the chest"
DE-fib (n, informal)
→ de-FIB-ril-late (v)
Focus: "de-FIB-ril-late" — 4 syllables. Not "de-fib-ri-LATE"
RE-sus (informal noun)
→ re-SUS-ci-tate (v)
British informal: "He's in RE-sus." Full form: re-SUS-ci-tate
VEN-tric-u-lar
/ven-TRIK-yoo-ler/
Common error: "ven-tri-CU-lar". Stress is on syllable 2.
fi-BRIL-la-tion
/faɪ-BRIL-ə-ʃən/
Common error: "fi-bril-LAY-tion". Stress on syllable 2.
🎯
Fast Speech Reductions — The Crash Call

In real crash calls, "ventricular fibrillation" becomes "V-fib" /ˈviːfɪb/; "defibrillation" becomes "defib"; "adrenaline" becomes "epi" in some US-trained teams but remains "adrenaline" in the NHS. Practise reading this aloud at speed: "V-fib on the monitor. Defib charged to 200. Epi drawn up. Stand clear — shocking now."
