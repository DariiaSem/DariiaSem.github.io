02. Clinical Narrative

The Next Ten Minutes

Read the following clinical scenario. Focus on the advanced general English vocabulary used to describe the psychological impact of the consultation.

Case Study: Gynecological Oncology Presentation

Anna, a 40-year-old mother of two, presented with vague abdominal bloating. An ultrasound revealed a complex adnexal mass. Today, she is sitting in your clinic to receive her biopsy results. You tell her: "I'm afraid the results show that the mass is malignant."

Hearing that word for the first time is a profoundly daunting experience. In the immediate ten minutes following this revelation, Anna will likely suffer from "auditory exclusion"—the shock renders her unable to process complex medical terminology.

This is a critical moment for the physician. You cannot simply gloss over the emotional fallout and immediately begin discussing survival rates or the multidisciplinary team. The silence in the room might feel insurmountable, but the physician's role is to endure it. By managing the emotional shock first, you anchor the patient in reality, offering them a clear, manageable next step to rebuild their resilience.

05. Grammatical Theory I

Hedging Language for Uncertainty 🛡️

In oncology, we often cannot give 100% guarantees. We use 'Hedging' to soften statements and avoid making absolute claims that could be legally or medically inaccurate.

Softening the Diagnosis & Prognosis

Type of Hedging Structures Example in Practice
Introductory Phrases It seems that...
It appears that...
The results suggest...		 "The results suggest that the cells are abnormal."
(Softer than: "You have abnormal cells.")
Modal Adverbs Likely
Possibly
Unlikely		 "It is highly likely that we will need to operate."
Modal Verbs Could / Might
May / Would		 "It would appear that the tumor has responded to chemo."
06. Grammatical Application I

Soften the Blow 🪶

Rewrite these harsh, direct statements into professional, hedged medical language using the prompts.

  • Direct: "This is ovarian cancer."
    (Use: The pathology report / suggest)
    → The pathology report suggests that this is ovarian cancer.
  • Direct: "You need a hysterectomy."
    (Use: It is highly likely)
    → It is highly likely that you will need a hysterectomy.
  • Direct: "The cancer has spread to the lymph nodes."
    (Use: It would appear)
    → It would appear that the cancer has spread to the lymph nodes.
  • Direct: "This treatment will cure you."
    (Use: There is a strong possibility)
    → There is a strong possibility that this treatment will cure you.
07. Grammatical Theory II

Advanced Passive Voice (Revision) 📑

In MDT (Multidisciplinary Team) meetings and medical reports, we use the impersonal passive to sound objective and distance the author from the action.

Structure Active (Subjective) Passive (Objective / MDT)
It is + past participle + that... "We decided that surgery is best." "It was decided that surgery is best."
Subject + is thought/believed + to + infinitive "We believe the tumor originated in the cervix." "The tumor is believed to have originated in the cervix."
08. Grammatical Application II

The MDT Report 📋

Transform these subjective sentences into Advanced Passives for the official oncology report.

  • Active: We recommend that the patient starts chemotherapy.
    → Passive: the patient starts chemotherapy. It is recommended that
  • Active: We think the metastasis reached the liver.
    → Passive: The metastasis the liver. is thought to have reached
  • Active: We agreed that we should perform a biopsy.
    → Passive: we should perform a biopsy. It was agreed that
  • Active: We know that this mutation causes rapid growth.
    → Passive: This mutation rapid growth. is known to cause

11. Communication Framework

The SPIKES Protocol 📋

The internationally recognized 6-step framework for delivering difficult diagnoses.

Step Goal Example Phrase
S - Setting Privacy, sit down, avoid interruptions. "Let's go into the quiet room. Is there anyone you'd like to be with you?"
P - Perception Find out what the patient already knows. "What is your understanding of why we did the biopsy?"
I - Invitation Ask how much detail they want to hear. "Are you the type of person who wants all the details, or just the big picture?"
K - Knowledge Fire a "Warning Shot", then state the facts clearly. "I'm afraid I have some difficult news... The results show it is cancer."
E - Empathy Acknowledge their emotion. Be silent. "I can see this is a huge shock. Take your time." (Embrace silence).
S - Strategy/Summary Provide a clear, immediate next step. "Our next step is an MRI to see exactly what we are dealing with."

13. Tone & Register

Curative vs. Palliative Intent ⚖️

It is vital to communicate the "Intent" of a treatment clearly. Is it to cure, or to prolong life/reduce pain?

Curative Intent Language

  • "Our primary goal here is eradication of the tumor."
  • "We aim to achieve complete remission."
  • "This is a radical procedure, but it offers the highest chance of a cure."

Palliative Intent Language

  • "Our focus now shifts to managing your symptoms and keeping you comfortable."
  • "This chemotherapy is to slow the progression, not to cure."
  • "We want to maximize your quality of life."
