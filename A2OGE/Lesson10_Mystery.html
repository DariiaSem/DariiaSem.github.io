Case No. 11 — The Overnight Train

Location: Night Express 214 — Lyon to Paris · Date: March 14 · Reported by: Conductor R. Mercier

At 2:30 AM, conductor Robert Mercier discovered that Compartment 7 was unlocked and the passenger inside, a well-known art dealer named Victor Blanc, was missing. His luggage remained on the seat, including his laptop, phone, and a briefcase reportedly containing €80,000 in cash. The money was gone.

There were three other passengers in nearby compartments that night. The first was Anna Sorel, a nurse travelling to Paris for a conference. She said she heard nothing unusual. The second was Marcus Djinn, a freelance photographer — his compartment showed signs of a rushed exit: an empty coffee cup was still warm, and his camera bag was missing one lens. The third passenger, Elise Vance, said she saw a figure in a dark jacket in the corridor around 2:00 AM, but could not identify the face.

The train made no stops between Lyon and Paris that night. Victor Blanc's phone was found switched off under the seat in Compartment 9 — the compartment that had been reserved under a false name. No body was ever found.

Anna Sorel
Nurse · Seat 6A

Claims she was asleep. No record of her attending any conference in Paris. Has a medical background — knows sedatives.

Marcus Djinn
Photographer · Seat 8B

Warm coffee cup suggests he was awake at 2 AM. The missing camera lens was found near Compartment 7. He has a debt of €40,000.

Elise Vance
Unknown profession · Seat 9C

Her compartment was booked under a false name. She spoke to Blanc at dinner. Found near the bathroom when conductor checked.

Victor Blanc
Art Dealer · MISSING

Left luggage and phone behind. No body found. Had enemies in the art world. Insurance policy on the cash: €150,000.

📐 Task 7 · Grammar Theory

Future Simple vs. Going To

Detectives predict what will happen next. But they use different futures for different reasons! Let's investigate.

The Future on a Timeline

Past
The crime happened
NOW
The detective is working
Near Future
"He's going to arrest someone." (plan/evidence)
Distant Future
"The case will be solved." (prediction/promise)
Future Form When to use it Example
will + verb Spontaneous decisions made right now "Someone's at the door!" — "I will get it."
will + verb Predictions (no evidence — just your opinion) I think the detective will find the thief soon.
will + verb Promises and offers I will help you investigate, I promise.
going to + verb Plans decided BEFORE the moment of speaking We are going to interview all three suspects tomorrow.
going to + verb Predictions based on current EVIDENCE or signs Look at the fingerprints! The case is going to be solved fast.
❌ Common Mistake: "I will go to the police tomorrow." ← This sounds spontaneous!
✅ Correct: "I am going to go to the police tomorrow." ← You already decided this.

Deduction Modals: must / might / can't

Detectives don't always know the truth. They deduce — they make logical conclusions from evidence. We use modals for this:

Modal Meaning Example (from the train case)
must be / must have Almost certainly true (strong deduction) His coffee was warm. He must have been awake at 2 AM.
might be / might have Possibly true (not certain) She might have seen something but is afraid to say.
can't be / can't have Almost certainly NOT true (strong negative deduction) She can't have left the train — there were no stops!
🔑 Structure for the Past: modal + have + past participle
must have stolen · might have hidden · can't have known

🎙️ Task 11 · Pronunciation

Fast Speech: Gonna, Wanna & Hafta

In fast, natural English speech, going to, want to, and have to are almost never said in their full form. Listen to how native speakers reduce them — and practise!

going to → "gonna"

Used in informal, fast speech. Only in spoken English — never write "gonna" in formal writing.

Written: "I am going to call the detective."

Spoken: /aɪm ˈɡʌnə kɔːl ðə dɪˈtɛktɪv/

→ Sounds like: "I'm gonna call the detective."

want to → "wanna"

The vowels in "want to" merge together very quickly in natural speech.

Written: "Do you want to know who did it?"

Spoken: /duː jə ˈwɒnə noʊ huː dɪd ɪt/

→ Sounds like: "Do you wanna know who did it?"

have to → "hafta"

The "ve" in "have" disappears and "to" becomes a schwa sound /ə/.

Written: "We have to find the truth."

Spoken: /wiː ˈhæftə faɪnd ðə truːθ/

→ Sounds like: "We hafta find the truth."

Weak form: "will" → 'll

In natural speech, "will" is almost always contracted and unstressed after a pronoun.

Written: "She will find the evidence."

Spoken: /ʃɪl faɪnd ðɪ ˈɛvɪdəns/

→ Sounds like: "She'll find the evidence."

🎯 Practice drill: Say these out loud three times each, getting faster each time:
"I'm gonna interview the suspect." · "Do you wanna see the evidence?" · "We hafta solve this tonight." · "She'll find the answer."

🔮 Will vs. Going To

Spontaneous decisions and general predictions → will. Planned intentions and evidence-based predictions → going to.

must have · might have · can't have — for logical conclusions based on evidence, past or present.
