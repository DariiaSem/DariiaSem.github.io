02 Level 3 Format

L3 LISTENING: What's Different

You practised Level 2 listening in Lesson 2 — short clips, basic facts, simple conversations. Level 3 is structurally different. Study this comparison before listening to anything.

Feature Level 2 (Lesson 2) Level 3 (Today)
Clip length15–40 seconds — short dialogues2–7 minutes — lectures, news segments, expert interviews, military briefings
Speaker countUsually 1–2 speakersMay include 1 speaker (monologue), 2–3 with overlapping views, or a structured panel
Speech speedClear, slightly slower than naturalNatural speed — including reductions, hedging, and qualified statements
What is testedWho, what, when, where — direct factsMain argument, speaker's attitude, implied conclusions, specific complex details
Question challengeAnswer is usually stated directly in audioAnswer often requires understanding the whole text, not just one sentence
Key trapWords from wrong options appear in audioPartially correct summaries — one detail is changed to make an option wrong
Note-takingNot usually necessaryEssential — impossible to hold all details in memory for 4–7 minutes

The Note-Taking Imperative: At Level 3, you MUST take notes during the first play of a long audio. Without notes, you will lose track of the argument's structure and the specific details that appear in the questions. 03 Vocabulary: Audio Language

Vocabulary: Spoken Report Language

Level 3 audio uses the language of news broadcasting, academic lectures, and official briefings. These 8 phrases and words appear constantly — and they are easy to miss if you don't know them cold.

unprecedentedadj

Never having happened or existed before; extraordinarily unusual in scale or nature.

"The scale of the humanitarian crisis was described as unprecedented in the region's modern history."

rhetoricnoun

Persuasive language — often used critically to mean impressive-sounding words that lack real substance or action.

"Analysts warned that strong rhetoric from both sides could mask the absence of genuine diplomatic progress."

ratifyverb

To officially approve or sign a treaty or agreement, giving it legal force.

"All member states must ratify the new defence protocol before it enters into force."

sanctions regimenoun phrase

A complete system of economic penalties imposed on a country — not just individual sanctions but the whole structure.

"The effectiveness of the existing sanctions regime has been widely questioned by independent observers."

multilateraladj

Involving more than two countries or parties — contrasted with bilateral (two parties) or unilateral (one party acting alone).

"A multilateral response was deemed more effective than any single nation acting unilaterally."

mandatenoun/verb

An official authority or instruction to carry out a policy — also used as a verb meaning to officially require something.

"The UN Security Council extended the peacekeeping force's mandate for a further twelve months."

oversightnoun

The process of supervising or monitoring an organisation or activity to ensure accountability.

"Critics argued that there was insufficient parliamentary oversight of the intelligence agencies."

in the wake ofphrase

Following as a consequence or result of — used to connect a cause with subsequent events or responses.

"In the wake of the incident, all border crossings were placed under heightened security protocols."

05 The STANAG Note-Taking System

Note-Taking: The T-Frame Method

You cannot memorise a 5-minute audio. You must write strategically. The T-Frame gives you a simple structure that captures what Level 3 questions actually test — without slowing down your listening.

1

Before the Audio Starts

Read ALL questions first. Underline the key word in each question. This tells you exactly what information to hunt for — you're not taking general notes, you're sniping specific facts.

2

The T-Frame Structure

Draw a T-shape on paper. Left column: the TOPIC of each paragraph or section. Right column: the KEY DETAIL or number mentioned. Never write full sentences — symbols, abbreviations, arrows.

3

Signal Words to Write Down

Always note: numbers, percentages, dates, names, "however / but / despite" (= contrast = likely question), "therefore / as a result" (= cause-effect = likely question).

4

First Play vs. Second Play

First play: take notes, mark likely answers. Second play: verify your chosen answers ONLY — don't re-listen to everything. Focused verification beats passive re-listening every time.

5

Attitude Signals

Note tone words: "unfortunately," "alarmingly," "encouragingly," "I would argue," "it is clear that." These signal the speaker's opinion — critical for attitude questions.

6

If You Miss Something

Do NOT panic and stop listening. Mark a "?" next to that question and keep going. The second play is for catching what you missed — missing one moment is not missing the question.

T-FRAME EXAMPLE — HOW TO STRUCTURE YOUR NOTES

Audio: 4-minute policy discussion
TOPIC (left column)
Para 1: Background / intro → NATO E. flank
Para 2: Problem → interop. failures → "critical"
Para 3: HOWEVER → some progress → std. programs
Para 4: Conclusion → "not optional" → political will
Tone: Concerned → cautiously optimistic
KEY DETAILS (right column)
2022 → redeployment → Poland / Baltics
equip. + comms + cmd = 3 gap areas
Joint ex. ↑ 40% since 2022 — BUT still not enough
"existential" → not strategic preference

Audio 1: Defence Policy Briefing

Teacher reads this aloud at natural speed — no pauses, no repetition during the first read. Use the T-Frame to take notes. Answer the MCQ after both plays. Time: approximately 3.5 minutes reading time.

Instructions: Read the questions FIRST (below). Underline one key word per question. Then close this section (scroll down to the questions). Teacher reads the script at natural speed. Take T-Frame notes. Then answer MCQ. Second play for verification only.
TRACK 01 — DEFENCE POLICY BRIEFING (Teacher reads aloud) MONOLOGUE ~3.5 min
[ Analyst — Policy Briefing ] Good morning. Today I want to address the evolving security landscape in Eastern Europe and what it means for NATO's collective defence posture. In the wake of Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine, the alliance has been forced to confront an uncomfortable reality: the forward defence model that NATO relied on for three decades was built on assumptions that no longer hold. The idea that a small, lightly armed tripwire force could deter aggression by signalling alliance commitment has been tested — and found wanting. What we are seeing now is a fundamental shift from deterrence-by-punishment — the threat of retaliation after an attack — to deterrence-by-denial: the positioning of sufficient force along the eastern flank to make an initial assault militarily prohibitive. This is not just rhetoric. Since 2022, eight new multinational battle groups have been activated. Defence spending across European NATO members has increased by unprecedented levels — with twelve member states now meeting or exceeding the 2% GDP target for the first time. However, I want to be frank about what remains unresolved. Interoperability — the ability of different national forces to operate seamlessly together — remains the central challenge. It is not sufficient to have troops deployed if those troops cannot effectively communicate, share targeting data, or rely on each other's logistics chains. These are not abstract concerns. In several multinational exercises conducted over the past two years, critical failures in command and communication were documented — not at the political level, but at the operational and tactical levels where lives are on the line. The good news is that standardisation programmes are accelerating. Joint exercises have increased by approximately forty percent since 2022. But the pace of change in doctrine and procurement must accelerate further if we are to avoid the scenario where our collective defence posture exists on paper but fails in practice. The ultimate question is one of political will. The mandate to change exists. The financial resources, while stretched, are available. What is needed is a willingness among member states to accept the short-term costs of deep integration — including, in some cases, the subordination of national procurement preferences to alliance-wide standards. Without that commitment, interoperability will remain an aspiration rather than a reality.

07 Level 3 Audio — Simulated Track 2

Audio 2: Hybrid Warfare Report

A two-speaker discussion — more complex than a monologue as you must track two distinct positions. Teacher plays both roles. Differentiate the speakers' views in your T-Frame notes. Time: approximately 4 minutes.

TRACK 02 — HYBRID WARFARE DISCUSSION (Teacher reads both roles) DIALOGUE ~4 min
[ HOST — Radio Programme ] We're joined today by two analysts to discuss the growing phenomenon of hybrid warfare. To begin — what exactly do we mean by the term, and why has it become so central to security debates? [ ANALYST A — Defence Institute ] Hybrid warfare is, at its core, a deliberate blurring of the boundaries between conventional military operations, cyber attacks, information operations, and economic coercion. The goal is to achieve strategic objectives — destabilising a government, influencing an election, undermining public trust in institutions — without crossing the threshold that would trigger a formal military response from an adversary or an alliance. Russia has arguably been the most sophisticated practitioner of this approach in Europe. [ ANALYST B — Strategic Communications ] I agree with that broad definition, but I think we sometimes overstate the novelty of the concept. States have always used non-military means to advance their interests — what's changed is the speed and scale enabled by digital technology. A disinformation campaign that once took months to propagate through a society can now reach millions of people in hours. And critically, it is often impossible to attribute reliably — meaning the targeted state faces a serious dilemma: respond and risk escalation, or absorb the attack in silence and signal weakness. [ ANALYST A ] That attribution problem is, I think, the central challenge. And it's not just about cyber — the same logic applies to the so-called "little green men" tactic we saw in Crimea in 2014. Forces in unmarked uniforms conducting operations, with plausible deniability built in from the start. The target state, NATO, and the international community all faced the same dilemma: how do you respond proportionately to aggression when the aggressor has deliberately made attribution almost impossible? [ ANALYST B ] The answer — and this is where I think progress has been made — is not primarily military. It is about building societal and institutional resilience. Countries like Finland and Estonia have invested heavily in what you might call a "whole of society" approach: media literacy programmes, robust oversight of social media platforms, civil defence exercises, strong election security infrastructure. The evidence suggests these investments are far more effective at countering hybrid threats than any increase in conventional military capability. [ HOST ] Is there a lesson there for other NATO members? [ ANALYST A ] Absolutely. The uncomfortable truth is that for many NATO members, the most significant vulnerabilities are not military at all — they are social and informational. A population that has been systematically misinformed, or that has lost trust in its own democratic institutions, is far more susceptible to hybrid influence than any gap in its conventional defence posture. Investing in education, press freedom, and institutional transparency may, in the long run, prove to be the most effective form of defence spending.

10 Grammar Module

Grammar: Concession & Contrast

Level 3 audio frequently signals the most important information with contrast and concession structures. If you miss "However," "Although," or "Despite," you often miss the answer. Master these in both listening AND writing.

Structure Usage Example in Military/Policy Context Listening Signal
However,Introduces a contrasting or qualifying point at sentence/paragraph level"Funding has increased significantly. However, structural reforms have lagged behind."⚠ High probability of MCQ answer immediately after
Although / Even thoughAcknowledges a fact while introducing a contrasting one in same sentence"Although the ceasefire was signed, violations continued to be reported."The SECOND clause (after the comma) is usually more important
Despite / In spite ofFollowed by a noun phrase — shows something happened contrary to expectation"Despite years of diplomatic negotiations, the conflict escalated."Note: "despite" + noun, not "despite" + verb
While / WhereasCompares two contrasting facts simultaneously"While some states increased spending, others fell short of targets."Two contrasting positions — likely a "what is the difference?" question
Nevertheless / NonethelessConcedes a point but maintains the main argument — more formal than "however""The challenges are significant. Nevertheless, progress is being made."Signals the speaker's overall positive or negative stance
admittedly / grantedConcedes a point before reasserting the main argument — used in academic speech"Admittedly, the approach is costly. But the alternative — inaction — is far more dangerous."Signals: the speaker believes the SECOND point, not the concession
🎯
The Contrast-Question Pattern: In both STANAG listening and reading, approximately 40% of Level 3 questions are triggered by contrast or concession language. The question will often ask: "What does the speaker say DESPITE/ALTHOUGH [some positive fact]?" — Training your ear to flag "however," "but," "although," "despite" as automatic alarm signals will dramatically improve your score.

  • the significant increase in defence spending, interoperability challenges persist across several member states. (despite / in spite of)
  • The joint exercises were broadly successful. , critical failures in command communication were documented at the tactical level. (however / nevertheless)
  • hybrid tactics are not entirely new, digital technology has dramatically accelerated their impact. (although / while)
  • attribution is technically possible in some cyber attacks, doing so publicly risks escalating the conflict. (even though / admittedly)

Discuss register: "Nevertheless" is more formal than "However"; "Admittedly" is more formal than "granted."
