02 Speaking Vocabulary

Vocabulary: Advanced Military & Policy Terms

These 8 terms are the vocabulary of high-level international security discussions — exactly what STANAG Level 3 speaking questions are built around. Know them by ear AND by mouth.

geopoliticaladj

Relating to politics influenced by geographical factors — territory, resources, strategic locations between nations.

"The geopolitical implications of Ukraine's membership aspirations extend far beyond the bilateral relationship."

reconciliationnoun

The process of restoring friendly relations between parties after conflict, division, or disagreement.

"Post-conflict reconciliation requires addressing not only political grievances but also the deeper social wounds of war."

accountabilitynoun

The obligation to accept responsibility for one's actions and to be answerable to those affected by them.

"International accountability mechanisms have been strengthened in response to documented war crimes."

institutionaladj

Relating to established organisations, systems, or bodies — particularly governmental, military, or international ones.

"Institutional reform is widely regarded as a prerequisite for sustained democratic consolidation."

compelverb

To force or oblige someone to do something; or to be so convincing that it demands a response.

"The evidence compels us to reconsider our assumptions about the effectiveness of conventional deterrence."

implicationnoun

The likely consequence or effect of something — what follows from an action, decision, or situation.

"The implications of this policy shift for burden-sharing within the alliance are likely to be far-reaching."

incumbent uponphrase

Obligatory for someone; a duty or responsibility that rests with a particular person or group.

"It is incumbent upon alliance members to honour their collective defence commitments consistently."

narrativenoun

The story or interpretation that shapes how an event, policy, or conflict is understood by the public or internationally.

"Controlling the information narrative has become as strategically important as controlling physical territory."

04 Level 3 Speaking Strategy

The 6 Level 3 Speaking Rules

These are the specific behaviours that separate a Level 3 speaker from a Level 2 speaker in the STANAG exam. Each one is observable by the examiner within the first 2 minutes.

1

Never Give a One-Sentence Answer

Level 3 requires elaboration. Every answer must have: a position → a reason → an example or evidence. A single sentence cannot achieve Level 3 even if it is grammatically perfect.

2

Use the 3-Part Answer Structure

Open → Develop → Conclude. Even for a 90-second answer. "That's a thought-provoking question. I would argue that... [reason]. This is particularly evident when... [example]. Overall, I believe that... [conclusion]."

3

Acknowledge Both Sides Before Concluding

Level 3 is not about winning an argument — it is about demonstrating the ability to consider multiple perspectives. Use: "While one could argue that... I would nevertheless contend that..."

4

Connect to Your Military Experience

Use "From my experience in the military..." at least once per major answer. This phrase simultaneously demonstrates vocabulary range, L3 register, and authentic personal engagement.

5

Self-Correct Confidently

If you make an error, correct yourself: "...that is to say... / what I mean is... / perhaps more accurately..." This signals language awareness, which is a Level 3 marker. Silence is worse than a smooth correction.

6

Use Discourse Markers as Anchors

Start paragraphs with: "Firstly..." / "Furthermore..." / "Conversely..." / "To sum up..." — these signal structure to the examiner, showing you planned your answer even when speaking spontaneously.

05 Fluency Building

Fluency Drills: Train Your Mouth

Fluency is a physical skill as much as a linguistic one. These drills target the specific speaking weaknesses that separate Level 2 from Level 3 — hesitation, repetition, and falling pitch at the end of arguments.

Drill 1: The Bridge

When you feel yourself hesitating, use a "bridge phrase" instead of silence or "eeehh". Practise saying each of these SMOOTHLY, at natural speed, as if they were a natural thought:

"What I find particularly interesting about this is..."
"From a military perspective, this raises the question of..."
"The implications of this are, I think, rather significant..."
"It is perhaps worth considering that..."

→ Your turn: Say each bridge phrase 3× until it feels natural

Drill 2: The Expand

Take any short answer and double it by adding "which means that..." or "and what this implies is..." Practise adding these extensions automatically:

"NATO needs better interoperability... which means that standardisation programmes must be accelerated across all three branches of the armed forces."

→ Your turn: Take a 1-sentence answer about military spending and expand it twice

Drill 3: The Pivot

Practise pivoting from one side to another using formal contrast language. Teacher reads Side A — you respond with the counter using one of these:

"While this is certainly true in some contexts, one must also consider..."
"Notwithstanding this point, I would contend that..."
"This view, though compelling, overlooks the fact that..."

→ Your turn: 3 rounds — teacher states a position, you pivot using each phrase

Drill 4: The Strong Finish

Many candidates give excellent answers but trail off weakly at the end. Practice ending EVERY answer with a strong, falling-intonation conclusion phrase:

"To sum up my position: I am of the firm conviction that..."
"Ultimately, the evidence compels us to conclude that..."
"It is, I believe, incumbent upon us to recognise that..."

→ Your turn: End your next 3 answers with a different strong finish each time

11 Grammar Module

Grammar: Hypothetical & Conditional Structures

Level 3 speaking questions often ask you to speculate, recommend, or describe hypothetical scenarios. These structures are essential for answering those questions without sounding simplistic.

Structure When Used in Speaking Military / Policy Example
If + Simple Past → would
(Type 2 — hypothetical)		Imagining a different reality or making a recommendation about what SHOULD happen"If the alliance invested more heavily in societal resilience, it would significantly reduce its vulnerability to hybrid threats."
If + Past Perfect → would have
(Type 3 — counterfactual)		Discussing what could have happened differently — useful for analysing past events"If earlier diplomatic channels had been more effectively utilised, the situation might never have escalated to this point."
were to + infinitiveMore formal hypothetical — used in policy discussion and formal recommendations"Were the government to reduce social spending significantly, the long-term consequences for national cohesion could be severe."
should + infinitive
(formal recommendation)		Strong formal recommendation — "should" in speaking signals urgency without arrogance"Alliance members should recognise that the accountability for collective defence cannot rest with one or two nations alone."
It would be + adjective + to...Evaluative statement about a hypothetical — avoids direct criticism while being clear"It would be misguided to assume that conventional military superiority alone is sufficient to address 21st-century threats."
🎙️
Speaking Application: STANAG Level 3 questions often ask "What could be done?", "What should happen?", or "If you were responsible for this, how would you approach it?" — These all demand conditional and hypothetical structures. Practise using "were X to happen...", "if governments had..." and "it would be advisable to..." until they come out naturally under pressure.
12 Grammar Practice

Complete the Conditional

Fill in each blank with the correct form of the verb in brackets to complete the conditional structure. Then say the complete sentence aloud at natural speaking speed.

  • If NATO members (invest) more consistently in interoperability, the alliance (be) better positioned to respond to hybrid threats.
  • Were governments (recognise) the institutional value of veteran reintegration, they (develop) more comprehensive support structures long ago.
  • If diplomatic channels (exhaust — passive) earlier, the conflict might never (escalate) to this level of intensity.
  • It (be) misguided (assume) that propaganda alone is responsible for radicalisation — the role of socioeconomic frustration cannot be overlooked.
  • Alliance members (should) acknowledge that accountability for collective defence is incumbent upon all partner nations, not merely the largest contributors.
✔
Answer Key: 1. invested / would be  |  2. to recognise / would have developed  |  3. had been exhausted / have escalated  |  4. would be / to assume  |  5. should — this one is direct; the sentence is a recommendation, not a conditional.
