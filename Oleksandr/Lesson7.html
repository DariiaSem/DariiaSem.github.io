03 Writing Vocabulary

Vocabulary: Essay Power Phrases

These 8 phrases are the engine of Level 3 essay writing. They directly raise your Coherence and Lexical Resource scores. Memorise them and deploy them deliberately in today's essays.

It is widely acknowledged thatINTRO

Opens your introduction with a shared truth — positions your essay as engaging with the existing debate.

"It is widely acknowledged that the proliferation of asymmetric threats has fundamentally altered the security landscape."

A compelling case can be made forBODY 1

Introduces your first main argument without stating your personal opinion — keeps the tone objective.

"A compelling case can be made for increasing defence expenditure, particularly in nations facing active threats."

Conversely / By contrastBODY 2

Introduces the counter-argument with a stronger contrast signal than "however" — signals deliberate structural shift.

"Conversely, critics argue that investment in education and social infrastructure yields more durable long-term security."

The evidence suggests thatEVIDENCE

Introduces supporting evidence without overstating certainty — appropriately hedged for academic register.

"The evidence suggests that nations with strong civil institutions are more resistant to hybrid influence operations."

This view fails to account forREFUTATION

Politely but firmly challenges an opposing argument — signals sophisticated critical thinking.

"This view, however, fails to account for the immediate security demands faced by nations under active military threat."

far-reaching implicationsIMPACT

A powerful noun phrase — emphasises that the issue has significant and wide-ranging consequences.

"The decision has far-reaching implications not only for defence policy but for the broader social contract."

Striking a balance betweenCONCLUSION

Introduces a nuanced conclusion — avoids the simplistic "one side wins" ending that examiners penalise.

"Striking a balance between security expenditure and social investment is, I would argue, the most defensible position."

I am of the firm conviction thatOPINION

States your conclusion with confidence — stronger than "I think" or "I believe," signals a Level 3 register.

"I am of the firm conviction that a multilateral approach, while imperfect, remains the most viable long-term solution."

05 The Blueprint

Level 3 Essay Architecture

Every Level 3 essay follows the same 4-paragraph blueprint. Deviating from it in an exam costs marks. Internalise this structure until it becomes automatic.

¶1 — Introduction

~40 words · NO personal opinion yet

Set the scene. State the issue. Acknowledge both sides exist. End with a clear thesis — what your essay will argue.

It is widely acknowledged that... The question of whether... is one that has attracted significant debate. In recent years, growing attention has been paid to... Few issues in contemporary [security/social/economic] discourse are as contested as...
  • ✗ Do NOT give your opinion in paragraph 1
  • ✗ Do NOT start with "In my opinion" or "I think"
  • ✗ Do NOT define terms unless the prompt specifically requires it

¶2 — First Argument (For / Side A)

~65 words · Strongest argument + evidence/example

Present the argument supporting ONE side. State it clearly, give one reason why it is valid, then add a concrete example or evidence.

A compelling case can be made for... Proponents of this view argue that... On one hand, there is considerable evidence to suggest that... Those in favour of... contend that... For instance,...

💡 End this paragraph with: "This is particularly evident in the case of [country/event/data]..."

¶3 — Counter-Argument (Against / Side B)

~65 words · Present THEN optionally refute

Acknowledge the opposing view fairly. You may then gently challenge it — this shows critical thinking and is rewarded at Level 3.

Conversely, critics argue that... On the other hand, opponents contend that... Nevertheless, this view fails to account for... While this perspective has merit, it overlooks the fact that...

💡 Optional refutation: "This view, however, fails to account for [weakness or counterexample]..."

¶4 — Conclusion

~40 words · YOUR opinion — clearly stated here for the FIRST time

Summarise the tension. State your opinion firmly. End with a forward-looking or qualifying statement.

Taking all factors into consideration, I am of the firm conviction that... Striking a balance between... I believe that... In conclusion, while both perspectives have merit, I would argue that... Ultimately, the evidence points towards... though this must be balanced against...
  • ✗ Do NOT introduce new arguments here
  • ✗ Do NOT repeat paragraph 2 or 3 word for word
  • ✗ Do NOT end with "In my personal opinion" — just "In my opinion"
06 Grammar Module

Grammar: Hedging & Academic Qualification

Level 3 essays use hedging language to avoid overstating claims. Saying "X is true" is simplistic — saying "X appears to be the case in many contexts, though exceptions exist" is sophisticated. This distinction separates L2 from L3.

Hedging Tool Function Military / Policy Example Register
It appears / seems thatSoft factual claim — signals based on evidence but not certain"It appears that hybrid tactics have become the preferred method of aggression below the Article 5 threshold."FORMAL
may / might / couldModal hedging — possibility, not certainty"Increased defence spending may deter aggression in the short term, but its long-term effects remain unclear."FORMAL
tends to / is likely toPattern-based generalisation — more hedged than "always""Nations facing existential threats tend to prioritise conventional military capability over social investment."FORMAL
to a considerable extentDegrees of agreement — avoids absolute statements"To a considerable extent, the effectiveness of deterrence depends on the credibility of the threat being communicated."FORMAL
in many / most casesQualified generalisation — acknowledges exceptions"In most cases, diplomatic channels are exhausted before military options are formally considered."FORMAL
one might argue thatPresents a position without fully committing — perfect for opposing views"One might argue that the primary obstacle is not financial but political in nature."FORMAL
🎯
The Level 3 Hedging Rule: Use hedging in every paragraph — at least once in the introduction and once in each body paragraph. Reserve your strong, unhedged opinion for the conclusion only: "I am of the firm conviction that..." The contrast between careful hedging throughout and a confident conclusion at the end is the hallmark of a Level 3 essay.
07 Grammar Practice

Upgrade to Level 3 Hedging

Rewrite each sentence by adding appropriate hedging language. Some sentences also need to be upgraded to more formal vocabulary. There is often more than one correct answer — discuss the options.

  • Overconfident: "More defence spending always makes a country safer." → Hedged L3: ", increased defence expenditure contribute to national security, though the relationship is rarely linear."
  • Overconfident: "Diplomacy doesn't work against determined aggressors." → Hedged L3: " diplomatic channels are effective against a determined adversary committed to achieving its objectives through force."
  • Informal + Overconfident: "Everyone knows that social media causes radicalisation." → Formal + Hedged: " that exposure to certain forms of online content play a role in the radicalisation process."
  • Overconfident: "NATO will certainly survive the next decade." → Hedged: "NATO maintain its relevance over the next decade, provided that member states continue to demonstrate genuine commitment to collective defence."
  • Informal + Blunt: "Rich countries should just give more money to defence." → Formal + Hedged: " that wealthier member states consider increasing their financial contributions to collective security arrangements."
✔
Possible Answers: 1. In many cases / may  |  2. It is unlikely / one might argue that  |  3. It appears / may  |  4. is likely to / could  |  5. One might argue / should / could — Multiple valid answers; discuss register and precision differences.
08 Model Text

Annotated Model: Salary Inequality

This is a full model answer for a real STANAG Level 3 prompt from your exam sample. Every annotation shows which rubric criterion is being addressed. Study this before writing your own essay.

"In some countries, a few people earn extremely high salaries. Some think this is good for a country, while others believe governments should limit the amount people can earn. Discuss both views and give your opinion."

Model Answer · ~270 words · STANAG Level 3

It is widely acknowledged that income inequality is one of the defining economic debates of the 21st century. While both sides of this argument have considerable merit, the question of whether extreme salary gaps benefit or harm society remains deeply contested. This essay will examine both perspectives before offering a considered conclusion.

A compelling case can be made for allowing individuals to earn unrestricted high salaries. Proponents argue that the prospect of significant financial reward drives entrepreneurship, innovation, and risk-taking — activities which, it tends to be argued, generate broader economic growth and employment. The evidence suggests that countries with dynamic private sectors frequently exhibit higher levels of innovation than those with heavily regulated wage structures. This is particularly evident in the technology and defence industries, where private investment has accelerated capabilities that benefit entire societies.

Conversely, critics contend that extreme salary disparities may undermine social cohesion, erode public trust in institutions, and reduce the incentive for individuals to contribute to public services. This view fails to account for, however, the significant tax revenue generated by high earners — revenue which, when effectively redistributed, can fund the very social programmes that critics argue are being neglected.

Striking a balance between rewarding individual achievement and ensuring equitable social outcomes, I am of the firm conviction that governments should not impose rigid salary caps but should instead ensure progressive taxation systems that redistribute wealth effectively. Ultimately, the goal should be an economy that incentivises excellence without abandoning those who are most vulnerable.

📐 Word count: ~270 words ✓ 🟢 Teal = Introduction phrases 🔴 Red = Argument phrases 🟡 Gold = Evidence phrases 🟣 Purple = Conclusion phrases
📌
What makes this Level 3: (1) Complex noun phrases ("income inequality," "extreme salary disparities"). (2) Consistent hedging ("tends to be argued," "may undermine," "can fund"). (3) Opinion ONLY in paragraph 4 — clearly signalled. (4) Partial refutation of Side B in ¶3 — demonstrates critical thinking. (5) Passive voice in formal context ("it tends to be argued," "when effectively redistributed"). (6) Conclusion is balanced but clear.
