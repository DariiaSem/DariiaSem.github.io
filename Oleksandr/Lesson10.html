02 New Vocabulary: Defence Economics

Vocabulary: Economics & Inequality

Today's reading, listening audio, and mock writing prompt all share this vocabulary set. Mastering these 8 terms before the lesson means you encounter them as friends, not strangers, during the mock.

fiscaladj

Relating to government finances, taxation, and public expenditure — the money management dimension of state policy.

"Fiscal constraints made a significant increase in defence spending politically difficult without cuts elsewhere."

disparitynoun

A great difference or inequality — especially in income, opportunity, or resources between groups or nations.

"The growing disparity in defence investment levels across NATO members has become a strategic liability."

expenditurenoun

The total amount of money a government spends — specifically on a programme, ministry, or policy area.

"Defence expenditure as a share of GDP has risen sharply across Eastern European NATO members since 2022."

inequalitynoun

An unfair situation where some people have far more wealth, opportunities, or rights than others in the same society.

"Rising income inequality has been identified as a driver of social unrest and political radicalisation."

procurementnoun

The formal process by which a government or military organisation obtains equipment, goods, or services through contracts.

"Defence procurement cycles spanning a decade make rapid adaptation to new threats structurally difficult."

redistributeverb

To reallocate wealth, income, or resources more equally — typically through progressive taxation and public spending programmes.

"Progressive tax systems are designed to redistribute wealth from higher earners toward essential public services."

austeritynoun

A government economic policy of reducing public spending to cut deficits — typically at the cost of social services and welfare.

"Years of defence austerity had degraded military readiness before the urgent need for rearmament arose."

GDPabbr · noun

Gross Domestic Product — the total monetary value of all goods and services produced within a country in a given period; the main measure of economic output.

"NATO's 2% GDP commitment requires each member to allocate that proportion of national output to defence."

04 Level 3 Reading: Defence Economics

Reading: Guns, Butter & Growth

Read all questions FIRST. Skim for paragraph structure. Scan for keywords. Time: 8 minutes. This text directly prepares you for the mock writing prompt — read strategically.

Economic Policy Analysis ~360 words · Level 3 · Defence Economics

AThe relationship between defence expenditure and economic growth remains one of the most contested questions in security economics. Classical economic theory posits a direct trade-off: resources devoted to military capability are resources unavailable for productive investment, education, or healthcare — the so-called "guns versus butter" dilemma. Yet the empirical evidence presents a more nuanced picture. In post-Cold War Europe, prolonged periods of defence austerity — driven by the assumption that major conflict had become impossible — coincided with infrastructure degradation, reduced military readiness, and, paradoxically, increased strategic vulnerability.

BThe rupture in European security in 2022 forced a fundamental reassessment. Nations that had allowed their defence industrial bases to atrophy over three decades faced acute procurement challenges: insufficient manufacturing capacity, fractured supply chains, and a workforce lacking the specialised skills required for rapid rearmament. The fiscal implications proved substantial. Rebuilding defence capacity is not merely a matter of increasing a budget line; it requires sustained, predictable investment over years — investment that competes directly with social welfare commitments already under pressure from ageing populations and post-pandemic debt levels.

CThis creates a genuine dilemma for democratic governments. Increasing defence expenditure at the required scale may necessitate difficult choices: cuts to social programmes, higher taxation, or increased borrowing. Each carries political costs. Critics argue that diverting resources from education, healthcare, and poverty reduction risks deepening the very inequalities and social vulnerabilities that fuel extremism and undermine democratic cohesion. Proponents counter that without physical security, no social programme can be reliably delivered.

DThere is, however, an emerging consensus that the "guns versus butter" framing is increasingly inadequate. Modern security analysts argue for a "resilience economy" model — one in which defence capability, social investment, and technological innovation are understood as complementary rather than competing priorities. In this view, the most capable society is not the one with the largest defence budget, but the one with the most cohesive, educated, and economically secure population.

RADIO DISCUSSION — SALARY INEQUALITY, DEMOCRACY & SOCIAL COHESION 2 RESEARCHERS ~3.5 min
[ HOST ] Today we are discussing the growing debate around salary inequality — whether extreme income gaps between the highest and lowest earners are good or bad for society and democracy. I'm joined by two researchers with distinct views. [ RESEARCHER A — Social Policy Institute ] My view is that the evidence is now quite clear: extreme salary inequality is corrosive to democratic institutions and social cohesion. When a small number of individuals accumulate enormous wealth, they inevitably gain disproportionate influence over political processes — through campaign finance, media ownership, and lobbying. The political system begins to serve the interests of the very wealthy rather than the electorate as a whole. The result is a growing sense of disenfranchisement among ordinary citizens, which in turn fuels populist movements and political polarisation. There is also a health dimension — societies with extreme inequality consistently show worse health outcomes across almost every indicator. [ RESEARCHER B — Economic Freedom Institute ] I would push back on that characterisation. Economic inequality is not inherently problematic — what matters is economic mobility: whether individuals can improve their position through effort, talent, and enterprise. Many of the most innovative economies in the world exhibit significant inequality. The danger of over-regulating or capping salaries is that it reduces the incentives that drive investment, risk-taking, and growth. A more equal society that is economically stagnant provides far fewer real opportunities for its citizens than a dynamic, growing economy with some degree of inequality. [ RESEARCHER A ] That is a compelling argument in theory, but the empirical data on mobility is increasingly concerning. In many high-inequality countries, intergenerational mobility has actually declined — meaning that your economic position at birth is becoming more predictive of your life outcomes, not less. That is not a meritocracy. And once people believe the system is fundamentally rigged against them, institutional trust collapses. When institutional trust collapses, democratic governance becomes extremely fragile. We have seen this pattern clearly in several European and American contexts over the past decade. [ RESEARCHER B ] I concur that intergenerational mobility is a genuine concern. However, I would argue the solution is not broad redistributive taxation or salary caps — it is targeted investment in education, skills, and the removal of structural barriers to participation. A highly skilled workforce is simultaneously a more equal and a more productive one. That is a conclusion both sides of this debate can, I think, agree on — even if we differ significantly on the mechanisms for achieving it.

07 Grammar: Complex Sentences for Level 3 Writing

Grammar: Subordinate Clauses

Level 3 essays require complex, multi-clause sentences. This single grammar feature most clearly separates Level 2 from Level 3 writing in the STANAG scoring rubric. Master these five types before the mock.

Clause TypeConnectorFunctionEssay Example (Economics topic)
Relative clause which / that / who / whose Add detail about a noun without a new sentence "Austerity policies, which reduced military readiness over three decades, proved costly when security demands re-emerged."
Concessive clause although / even though / while / despite the fact that Acknowledge a contrasting point within one sentence "Although increased defence expenditure creates short-term fiscal pressure, its long-term strategic value may outweigh the costs."
Causal clause since / given that / owing to the fact that / as Explain the reason or cause within the same sentence "Given that defence industrial capacity takes years to rebuild, the case for sustained investment is compelling."
Result clause to such an extent that / such that / with the result that Show the consequence or outcome of an action "Inequality has risen to such an extent that intergenerational mobility has declined across multiple European economies."
Formal conditional provided that / on condition that / were + subject State conditions more formally than "if" "Provided that social investment is maintained, increased defence expenditure need not deepen inequality."
✒️
Mock exam writing goal: Aim for at least one of each clause type across your four essay paragraphs. A range of subordinating connectors is one of the clearest markers of Level 3 grammatical control. Examiners are trained to identify and reward grammatical variety — and to notice its absence.
08 Grammar Practice

Combine into One Complex Sentence

Merge each pair of simple sentences into one complex sentence using the connector type indicated. Say each sentence aloud — it must sound formal and fluent at natural speaking speed.

  • A: "Defence industrial capacity declined over 30 years." B: "It could not be rapidly rebuilt when needed." → RELATIVE: "Defence industrial capacity, had declined over three decades, be rapidly rebuilt when urgent security demands arose."
  • A: "Increased defence spending creates fiscal pressure." B: "Its strategic benefits may outweigh the costs." → CONCESSIVE: " increased defence spending creates significant fiscal pressure, its long-term strategic benefits outweigh the short-term costs."
  • A: "Inequality has grown significantly." B: "Intergenerational social mobility has now declined." → RESULT: "Inequality has grown that intergenerational mobility declined across several high-income economies."
  • A: "Social investment is maintained." B: "Democratic governance remains achievable." → FORMAL CONDITIONAL: " social investment is maintained at adequate levels, effective democratic governance remain achievable even under fiscal pressure."
  • A: "The peace dividend reduced military investment." B: "Strategic vulnerability increased substantially." → CAUSAL: " the so-called 'peace dividend' significantly reduced military investment, strategic vulnerability increased substantially by the early 2020s."
✔
Answer Guide: 1. which / could not  |  2. Although / may  |  3. to such an extent / has  |  4. Provided that / can  |  5. Given that / had — Multiple valid forms exist. Discuss which sounds most formal and most natural at speaking speed.
