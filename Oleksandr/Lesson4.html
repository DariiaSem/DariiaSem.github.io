Know the Terrain

Speaking Test: 30-Minute Breakdown

The STANAG speaking exam has three phases. Each phase tests something different. Knowing exactly when each phase begins — and what is expected — eliminates surprise and reduces nerves.

Warm-Up
~3 min

Personal intro — name, rank, role, background. Light conversation. Relaxed. The examiner is not scoring heavily yet — but your first impression matters.

Structured Interview
~25 min

This is where your score is decided. Level 2 & Level 3 questions on military topics, current events, and controversial social issues. You must speak continuously, structure arguments, and use formal vocabulary.

Wind-Down
~2 min

A closing casual question: "What are you going to do after the test?" Brief, friendly — end on a positive, confident note. Do not trail off or say "I don't know."

Speaking Arsenal

Vocabulary: Spoken Discourse Markers

These phrases do three things: buy you thinking time, structure your argument, and signal sophistication to the examiner. They are the difference between a Level 2 and a Level 3 speaker.

That's a thought-provoking questionOPENER

Buys 3–4 seconds thinking time while sounding engaged and sophisticated. Never say "I don't know."

"That's a thought-provoking question. From a military perspective..."

To put it another wayCLARIFY

Use when you want to rephrase your point more clearly or when you sense the examiner didn't follow.

"To put it another way, without interoperability, NATO cannot function effectively."

From my experience in the militaryEVIDENCE

Powerful phrase — connects abstract discussion to your real professional background. Very impressive at Level 3.

"From my experience in the military, language barriers can significantly delay decision-making."

It is worth noting thatEMPHASIS

Signals that the next point is particularly important. More formal than "also" or "and."

"It is worth noting that this tendency has accelerated since 2020."

One could argue thatHEDGING

Presents a viewpoint without fully committing — useful for presenting the "other side" in a discussion.

"One could argue that defence spending should take priority in times of active threat."

The implications of this are significantEXPAND

Forces you to elaborate beyond the basic answer — prevents short, underdeveloped answers.

"The implications of this for NATO partners are significant, particularly in terms of..."

I would contend thatOPINION

A strong, formal way to state your own opinion — more sophisticated than "I think."

"I would contend that a multilateral approach is the only viable long-term solution."

To sum up my positionCONCLUDE

Signals clearly that you are wrapping up. Examiners appreciate structured, deliberate closings.

"To sum up my position, I believe both security and welfare investment are mutually dependent."

Pronunciation Module

Pronunciation: Stress & Intonation

STANAG examiners are trained to assess pronunciation. Two things cost marks: incorrect word stress on key military vocabulary, and falling intonation on questions (which sounds rude). Master both today.

PART A — WORD STRESS: Military & Formal Vocabulary

Red syllable = primary stress. Say each word aloud, then say it in a sentence. The stress pattern is what matters — not a "perfect" accent.

re-CON-nais-sance re-CON-nuh-sance — NOT re-con-NAIS-sance. 4 syllables total.
de-PLOY-ment de-PLOY-ment — stress on second syllable.
op-er-A-tion-al op-er-A-tion-al — stress on third syllable. Very common STANAG word.
pre-LIM-i-nar-y pre-LIM-in-ary — stress on second syllable. NOT pre-lim-in-AR-y.
MIL-i-tar-y MIL-it-ree (3 syllables in natural speech) — NOT mil-I-tar-y. First syllable stressed.
in-TER-op-er-a-bil-i-ty in-TER-op-er-a-BIL-ity — secondary stress on 6th syllable too. Key NATO vocabulary.

PART B — INTONATION PATTERNS

"Are you serving in active duty?"
YES/NO Q → RISING ↗
"What is your current rank?"
WH-Q → FALLING ↘
"I visited Poland, Germany, and the UK."
LIST → RISE, RISE, FALL ↗↗↘
"From my experience in the military, I believe that..."
CLAUSE + OPINION → LEVEL THEN FALL ↘
🎙️
Pronunciation Drill: Say each word from Part A five times — slowly first, then at natural speed. Then put it in the sentence: "The reconnaissance mission was preliminary in nature — the full operational assessment will follow." Say it three times until the stress and rhythm feel natural.

Grammar Module

Grammar: Modal Verbs for Speaking

Modals are the engine of spoken English — especially for opinion, obligation, and possibility. In the speaking test, using the right modal instantly signals fluency and sophistication.

Modal Function in Speaking STANAG Speaking Example Register
mustStrong obligation or logical deduction"Governments must ensure that returning soldiers receive psychological support."Formal ✓
shouldRecommendation or moral obligation"In my view, NATO should increase its investment in language training programmes."Formal ✓
couldPossibility or suggestion — softer than "can""One could argue that propaganda is the most dangerous factor in radicalisation."Formal ✓
wouldHypothetical or polite opinion"I would contend that a balanced approach is more effective than either extreme."Formal ✓
may / mightHedged possibility — useful for discussing uncertain outcomes"The implications may be more complex than they initially appear."Formal ✓
need notAbsence of necessity — more formal than "don't need to""A country need not sacrifice social welfare in order to maintain effective defence."Very Formal ✓✓

Grammar Practice

Fill the Gap: Choose the Modal

Complete each speaking-style sentence with the most appropriate modal verb. Then say the complete sentence aloud — the goal is to use it naturally when speaking, not just to write it correctly.

  • Governments invest in psychological rehabilitation programmes for veterans — it is a moral obligation, not an option. (strong obligation)
  • In my view, the international community approach this issue with greater caution. (recommendation)
  • One argue that the root cause of radicalisation is, in fact, economic marginalisation. (soft possibility / hedged opinion)
  • The situation deteriorate further if diplomatic channels are not used immediately. (uncertain future possibility)
  • I contend that interoperability is the single most critical factor in modern coalition warfare. (formal hypothetical opinion)
  • A country sacrifice civilian welfare entirely in order to fund its military — there is a balance to be found. (absence of necessity)
✔
Answer Guide: 1. must  |  2. should  |  3. could  |  4. may/might  |  5. would  |  6. need not

Wind-Down: Exit with Confidence

Many candidates lose points in the wind-down by giving one-word answers or trailing off. These 2 final minutes are easy marks — if you know what to do.

WIND-DOWN SIMULATION — PHASE 3 ~2 MINUTES
🎓
Examiner"We're coming to the end of our session today. What are you going to do after the test?"
💡 This is your final impression. Answer in 3–4 sentences — casual but still correct. Show personality. Use: "I'm planning to..." / "I intend to..." / "After the test, I'll be..." — Avoid: "I don't know" / "Sleep" / single-word answers.
🪖
Oleksandr — Model"After the test, I'm planning to meet with a colleague to debrief on today's experience. I'm also intending to review the areas I found most challenging — particularly the Level 3 discussion questions. 💡 Respond warmly and professionally: "Thank you very much. It was a pleasure speaking with you. I look forward to receiving the results." — Smile, make eye contact, and do NOT ask "Did I pass?" (Very common mistake!)

Writing Consolidation

Writing Check: Timed Essay Paragraph

Write ONE body paragraph (60–70 words) for this Level 3 essay question in 10 minutes. Use a modal verb, a discourse marker, and the passive voice. This consolidates all four lessons.

✏️
Task: "Some argue that military service should be mandatory for all citizens. Discuss both views." — Write ONLY the "On one hand..." first body paragraph, arguing FOR mandatory service. Include: modal verb + passive voice + one formal linker. 60–70 words, 10 minutes.

Start: "On one hand, proponents of mandatory military service argue that..."

  • After writing: Did you use a modal verb? Which one, and was it appropriate?
