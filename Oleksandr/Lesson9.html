02 Today's Vocabulary

Vocabulary: International Order & Diplomacy

These 8 terms appear throughout today's reading and listening tasks. Mastering them before encountering them in context dramatically accelerates comprehension speed — critical under exam time pressure.

consensusnoun

General agreement among all members of a group — especially within an alliance or multilateral body.

"Achieving consensus among 32 member states on a joint response proved considerably more difficult than expected."

posturenoun

A country's or alliance's overall stance or position — military, diplomatic, or strategic.

"NATO's deterrence posture has been fundamentally reassessed since the 2022 invasion."

leveragenoun/verb

Power or advantage used to influence a situation; to use that power strategically.

"Economic sanctions were intended to give the alliance additional leverage in diplomatic negotiations."

normativeadj

Relating to establishing or enforcing norms — standards of expected or accepted international behaviour.

"The international community has a normative interest in upholding the principle of territorial sovereignty."

fragmentationnoun

The breaking apart of something unified into smaller, disconnected pieces — used for political orders, alliances, or institutions.

"Analysts warned that the fragmentation of the Western alliance could undermine collective deterrence."

projectionnoun

The extension of power, influence, or military capability beyond a country's borders.

"The capacity for force projection has historically defined great power status in the international system."

interlocutornoun

A person who takes part in a dialogue or negotiation, especially as a representative; the interviewer in the STANAG speaking test.

"Finding a credible interlocutor on the other side proved essential to beginning substantive talks."

regimenoun

A system of rules, norms, or governance — not always negative; used in international relations for established frameworks.

"The non-proliferation regime has faced increasing pressure from states seeking to develop independent nuclear capabilities."

04 Level 3 Reading — Text 1

Reading: The Post-Cold War Order Under Pressure

Apply the 4-step reading strategy from Lesson 5: read the questions first, underline key terms, skim for structure, then answer. Time: 8 minutes.

International Relations Analysis ~280 words · Geopolitics / Global Order

AThe international order that emerged from the Cold War's end was premised on a set of assumptions that now appear increasingly fragile. Chief among these was the belief that the expansion of liberal democracy, market economies, and multilateral institutions would progressively reduce the incentives for great power conflict. This optimistic vision has been subjected to sustained pressure from multiple directions simultaneously — from authoritarian consolidation within several major states, from the fragmentation of Western unity, and from the outright rejection of core normative principles by revisionist powers.

BRussia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022 represented the most direct challenge to this order in decades. The invasion did not simply violate Ukrainian sovereignty — it challenged the foundational premise that territorial borders in Europe were inviolable, and that the threat or use of force to resolve interstate disputes had been permanently delegitimised. The international community's response — unprecedented sanctions, military assistance, and coordinated diplomatic isolation — demonstrated that consensus on the normative framework could, under extreme circumstances, be rapidly mobilised.

CYet the durability of this consensus remains uncertain. Fractures have emerged between member states regarding the scale and pace of support. Some governments, facing domestic political pressures, have proved reluctant to commit to open-ended obligations. Meanwhile, global actors outside the Western sphere — including China, India, and much of the Global South — have declined to join the sanctions regime, choosing instead to maintain economic leverage over both parties. Their calculated neutrality reflects not indifference, but a considered assessment that alignment with either bloc carries greater risks than strategic ambiguity.

DWhat emerges from this landscape is not a return to Cold War bipolarity, but something potentially more unstable: a multipolar order in which the normative frameworks that once constrained great power behaviour are contested rather than accepted, and in which the projection of power is increasingly unencumbered by the institutional architecture that previous generations built with such effort.

06 Level 3 Reading — Text 2

Reading: NATO Burden-Sharing & The 2% Question

A text that directly relates to Oleksandr's professional context. Read with the questions first. Time: 8 minutes.

Alliance Defence Policy ~260 words · Military / Finance

ANATO's commitment to member states spending at least two percent of their gross domestic product on defence has transformed from a guideline into a political fault line. For years, the target was honoured more in rhetoric than in reality; in 2014, only five of the then-28 member states met it. The invasion of Ukraine fundamentally altered the political calculus, and by 2024, a record number of members — over twenty — were meeting or exceeding the threshold, with several committing to significantly higher targets.

BThe debate over burden-sharing, however, runs deeper than raw percentages. Critics argue that the two percent metric is a crude instrument that measures input rather than output — the amount spent tells us little about the capability, readiness, or strategic coherence of the forces purchased. A nation spending two percent on obsolete equipment and bloated administrative structures contributes less to alliance capability than one spending one point eight percent on highly trained, rapidly deployable units equipped with interoperable systems.

CA more fundamental tension underlies these debates: the divergence between members who perceive an existential threat from Russia and those who do not. For the Baltic states, Poland, and most recently Finland, the threat is immediate and viscerally felt. For some Western European members, particularly those further from the eastern flank, the threat feels more abstract and the domestic political cost of large defence budgets remains high. Bridging this perceptual gap — not merely the financial one — is arguably the alliance's most enduring governance challenge.

[ HOST ] We're joined today by two analysts to examine the concept of a rules-based international order — what it means, whether it still exists, and what the implications are for countries like Ukraine seeking security guarantees within that framework. [ ANALYST A — International Law ] The rules-based order is, at its core, a normative claim — a proposition that international relations should be governed by agreed rules and institutions rather than by the unilateral projection of power. It's easy to be cynical about this, because great powers have always bent those rules when it suited them. But the alternative isn't a world of no rules — it's a world where the only rule is power. And for smaller and medium-sized states without great power leverage, that's an infinitely worse outcome. [ ANALYST B — Strategic Studies ] I broadly agree, though I think we need to be honest about the fragmentation we're seeing. The rules-based order was never universally accepted — it reflected a particular Western-led consensus that emerged after 1945 and was consolidated after 1991. China has consistently argued that the order is a vehicle for Western hegemony dressed up in normative language. And frankly, they have a point — the United States has frequently invoked the rules-based order selectively, applying it vigorously where it served American interests and quietly setting it aside where it didn't. [ ANALYST A ] That's true, but selective application doesn't delegitimise the framework entirely. There's a difference between a state that periodically violates norms it otherwise upholds and a state that rejects the normative framework altogether. Russia's position isn't that it was treated unfairly within the rules-based order — it's that territorial conquest and sphere-of-influence politics are legitimate instruments of state policy. That's a much more fundamental challenge. [ ANALYST B ] Agreed. And that's precisely why Ukraine's situation is so pivotal. If Russia's invasion is ultimately rewarded — even partially — it sends a signal to every other revisionist actor that the normative framework can be broken with manageable consequences. The interlocutor nations currently sitting on the fence are watching closely. Their assessment of whether the rules-based order still has teeth will shape their choices for the next decade. [ ANALYST A ] Which is why the level of support provided to Ukraine matters beyond the bilateral relationship. Every javelin missile and every round of sanctions is also a statement about the posture of the international community towards norms violations. Whether that statement is credible — whether it actually deters future challengers — is the central question for the next decade of international order.

08 Grammar Module

Grammar: Advanced Passive Constructions

You know basic passive voice. Level 3 essays and formal reports use advanced passive constructions — double passives, passive with modals, and reporting passives — that are hallmarks of academic and diplomatic register.

Construction Structure Level 3 Example Effect
Reporting passive It is widely + reported/believed/argued/acknowledged + that... "It is widely argued that the fragmentation of Western unity represents an existential risk." Presents a view without naming a source — distanced, academic tone
Impersonal it-passive It was + pp + that / It has been + pp + that "It has been noted that the perceptual gap between eastern and western members remains significant." Removes the subject entirely — highly formal, no attribution
Modal passive Modal + be + past participle "Alliance coherence can only be maintained if the perceptual gap is bridged." Combines necessity/possibility with formality
Perfect passive has/have/had + been + past participle "The normative frameworks that previous generations built with such effort are being steadily eroded." Links past construction to present consequence — shows process
Passive + agent past participle + by + agent "The invasion was described by analysts as a direct challenge to foundational principles." Keeps formal passive while specifying who made the claim
✒️
Why Today's Texts Use Advanced Passive: Look back at Text 1 — count how many passive constructions appear. You will find at least 8. This is deliberate: academic and journalistic writing about international relations uses passive to create analytical distance and to focus on events rather than actors. Your Level 3 essays should replicate this register consciously.
09 Grammar Practice

Transform to Academic Passive

Rewrite each sentence using the most appropriate advanced passive construction. Then check: does the new version sound more like Text 1 or Text 2 from today's reading? It should.

  • "Many analysts think that burden-sharing tensions will persist." → Reporting passive: " that burden-sharing tensions will persist."
  • "Someone noted that Russia's challenge differs fundamentally from previous norm violations." → Impersonal it-passive: " that Russia's challenge differs fundamentally from previous norm violations."
  • "We cannot resolve the perceptual gap through financial measures alone." → Modal passive: "The perceptual gap through financial measures alone."
  • "Previous generations built the institutional architecture that now constrains great power behaviour." → Perfect passive: "The institutional architecture that now constrains great power behaviour by previous generations."
  • "Scholars describe the current moment as a pivotal juncture in the evolution of international order." → Passive + agent: "The current moment by scholars in the evolution of international order."
✔
Answer Key: 1. It is widely argued  |  2. It has been noted  |  3. cannot be resolved  |  4. was built (or has been built)  |  5. is described / as a pivotal juncture
