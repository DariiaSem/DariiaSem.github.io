🗂️ Task 5 · Reading

Case Study: The Apex Scandal

Read the inspector's notes regarding the corporate scandal. Pay close attention to the rules and obligations mentioned.

04

Audit File: Apex Clothing Corporation

Sector: Fast Fashion · Investigated by: Inspector L. Chen

Initial Findings: Apex Clothing's marketing team launched the "Earth First" line, claiming all materials were ethically sourced. However, international trade law dictates that a company must prove the origin of its cotton. Apex has failed to do so. Our investigators found that they buy cheap, chemically-treated cotton.

Factory Regulations: According to local laws in the production sector, factories have to treat toxic wastewater before releasing it into the river. Furthermore, factories mustn't dump chemical dyes during the rainy season. Apex ignored both rules to save $2 million. The CEO argued internally, "We don't have to follow European laws if we operate in unregulated zones."

Recommendations: The board of directors should immediately fire the executives involved. To avoid bankruptcy from fines, they ought to hire an independent environmental consultant. Consumers must be warned about the false advertising immediately.

📐 Task 7 · Grammar Theory

Modals of Obligation & Advice

When dealing with environmental laws and corporate ethics, we must be very careful about the difference between a "good idea" and a "strict law."

The Scale of Obligation

NO OBLIGATION
don't have to / needn't
ADVICE (Good Idea)
should / ought to
STRONG RULE (Internal)
must
EXTERNAL LAW
have to
Modal Nuance & Usage Corporate Example
Must vs. Have to Must: Personal obligation, internal rules, or written signs.
Have to: External obligation, forced by law or someone else.		 "I must stop printing so much paper!" (My choice)
"We have to pay the pollution tax." (The law)
Should / Ought to Advice, recommendations. Doing this is a good idea, but you won't go to jail if you don't. "You should use biodegradable packaging."
Mustn't PROHIBITION. It is strictly forbidden. Do not do it. "You mustn't dump chemicals in the river."
Don't have to LACK of obligation. It is optional. You can do it if you want, but it's not required. "You don't have to be vegan to help the planet."
⚠️ Crucial Difference: "Must" and "Have to" are similar in the positive. But in the negative, they are completely different!
You mustn't enter (Forbidden) vs. You don't have to enter (Optional).
🖊️ Task 8 · Grammar Practice

The Inspector's Checklist

Complete the environmental inspector's notes using must, mustn't, have to, don't have to, or should. Think carefully about the context!

  • "According to the 2024 Energy Act, all corporate buildings switch to LED lighting by December." (Law)
  • "I personally believe that we ban all plastic bags immediately. It's the only way." (Strong personal opinion)
  • "The company lie on their emissions report! It is a federal crime." (Forbidden)
  • "Employees drive an electric car to work, but the company offers a bonus if they do." (Optional)
  • "If they want to fix their public image, they apologize on national television." (Strong advice)
  • "Sorry, I can't stay late. I attend the city council meeting about the new wind farm." (External obligation)

🎙️ Task 11 · Pronunciation

Fast Speech: Obligation Reductions

In fast, natural English speech, modals of obligation are heavily reduced. Practice these to sound more fluent during debates.

have to → "hafta"

The 'v' becomes an 'f' sound, and 'to' becomes a schwa.

Written: "We have to change."

Spoken: /wi ˈhæftə tʃeɪndʒ/

has to → "hasta"

The 'z' sound in 'has' becomes an 's' sound.

Written: "The company has to pay."

Spoken: /ðə ˈkʌmpəni ˈhæstə peɪ/

mustn't → "muss-ent"

The first 't' is completely silent! Do not say "must-nt".

Written: "You mustn't do that."

Spoken: /ju ˈmʌsənt du ðæt/

ought to → "oughta"

The 't' blends into a flap T or disappears.

Written: "They ought to know."

Spoken: /ðeɪ ˈɔtə noʊ/
