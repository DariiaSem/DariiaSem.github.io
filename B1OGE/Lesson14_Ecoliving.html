Task 6

Grammar Theory: Modals of Obligation & Advice

How do we express rules, necessities, and good ideas?

Modal Verb Usage / Meaning Example
Must Strong obligation (usually internal, from the speaker). Official written rules. "I must stop buying fast fashion." (My own rule)
Have to Strong obligation (external, forced by laws or situations). "We have to separate our recycling in this city." (The law)
Should / Ought to Advice, recommendation, or a good idea (not a strict rule). "You should bring your own bags to the supermarket."
Mustn't vs. Don't have to Mustn't: Prohibition (Do not do it!).
Don't have to: No obligation (It's optional).		 "You mustn't dump oil in the drain."
"You don't have to be perfectly zero-waste."

The Obligation Scale

Optional
(Don't have to)
Good Idea
(Should)
Mandatory
(Must / Have to)

Task 11

Pronunciation: Fast Speech Reductions

In native American English, modals are heavily reduced in fast speech. Practice saying these out loud.

Have to ➔ "Hafta" (I hafta go.)

Has to ➔ "Hasta" (He hasta recycle.)

Got to ➔ "Gotta" (We gotta change.)

Should have ➔ "Shoulda" (We shoulda acted sooner.)
