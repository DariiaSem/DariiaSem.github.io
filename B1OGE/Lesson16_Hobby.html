03. Editorial Reading

The Beauty of the Mundane

Read the following cultural profile. Do not consult a dictionary immediately. Rely on the context to deduce the meaning of the highlighted words. Pay close attention to how the author describes actions that started in the past and continue today.

Profile: Arthur Pendelton • The Collector's Chronicle

For twenty years, Arthur Pendelton has been accumulating objects that most reasonable people would throw away. From the outside, his apartment looks entirely ordinary. Inside, however, he has been meticulously curating one of the most eccentric collections in Europe: over 14,000 vintage matchboxes.

"I have been searching for a specific 1920s Parisian matchbox since 2015," Arthur explains, carefully adjusting his glasses. "I have been writing letters to dealers all over the world, but no luck yet."

To an outsider, this behavior borders on the obsessive. Why dedicate your life to something so mundane? While modern culture pushes us toward minimalism, collectors like Arthur find a deep sense of nostalgia in these tiny cardboard squares. They represent forgotten factories, closed restaurants, and quiet moments of history.

Arthur has been meticulously organizing his collection by year and country all morning. His hands are covered in dust, and he looks exhausted, but his eyes shine with pure joy. "People think I'm crazy," he laughs. "But I have been preserving history. What have they been doing?"

05. Grammatical Theory

The Present Perfect Continuous

Why does the author use "has been accumulating" instead of "has accumulated"? We use this complex tense to focus on the duration and the ongoing activity, rather than the finished result.

Structure: subject + have/has + been + V-ing

Rule 1: Unfinished Actions Focusing on Duration
We use this tense to emphasize how long an action has been happening. It started in the past and is still continuing right now. We often use for or since.
"I have been searching for that matchbox since 2015."

Rule 2: Recently Stopped Actions with Visible Results
We use it for an action that has just stopped, but we can clearly see, hear, or feel the physical results in the present moment.
"His hands are covered in dust because he has been organizing his collection all morning."

Pronunciation Note: The Weak "Been"
Native speakers rarely pronounce "been" with a long 'ee' sound (/biːn/). In the Present Perfect Continuous, it reduces to a short, weak /bɪn/.
"I have been working" ➔ "I've /bɪn/ working." Practice reading your answers with this fluent reduction.
