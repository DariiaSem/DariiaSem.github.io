Task 1: The Memory Check

The Conditional Audit

In previous lessons, we covered the 0, 1st, 2nd, and 3rd Conditionals. Let's see if you can match the rules to the examples!

Zero & 1st Conditional

0 (Fact): If + Present, Present.

"If you heat water to 100 degrees, it boils."

1st (Possible Future): If + Present, Will + V.

"If I miss the bus, I will take a taxi."

2nd & 3rd Conditional

2nd (Imaginary Now): If + Past, Would + V.

"If I had a million dollars, I would buy an island."

3rd (Past Regret): If + Past Perfect, Would have + V3.

"If I had woken up earlier, I wouldn't have missed the flight."

Task 5: Reading

The Missed Train 🚆

Read this true story about how one small annoyance changed a woman's life completely.

Ten years ago, Clara was running late for work. As she ran out the door, it started to rain. She ran back inside to grab her umbrella, which took exactly two minutes. Because of those two minutes, she missed the 8:05 AM train to London.

She was very angry. She sat in a café to wait for the next train. While she was drinking coffee, a man asked to sit at her table because the café was full. They started talking. That man was James. Today, Clara and James are happily married and run a business together.

Clara often says: "If I hadn't forgotten my umbrella that morning, I wouldn't be married to James today!"

Task 7: Grammar Theory

Mixed Conditionals (Type 1)

Sometimes, an action we did (or didn't do) in the PAST has a massive result on our life RIGHT NOW. We mix the 3rd and 2nd conditionals to express this.

Type 1: Past Action ➔ Present Result

If + Past Perfect
(Past Cause)
"If she hadn't missed the train..."
PAST
would (not) + bare verb
(Present Effect)
"...she wouldn't be married today."
NOW
Logic Check: She did miss the train in the past. Therefore, she is married today. We are imagining the opposite reality!

Task 11: Grammar Theory

I Wish / If Only

When we want to talk about regrets or things we want to change, we use "I wish" or "If only". The grammar rule is similar to conditionals.

Time of Regret Structure Example
Present Regret
(Wanting to change the present)		 I wish + Past Simple "I don't have a car." ➔
I wish I had a car.
Past Regret
(Wanting to change history)		 I wish + Past Perfect "I didn't study." ➔
I wish I had studied.
Note: "If only" means the exact same thing as "I wish", but it sounds stronger and more emotional!

Task 13: Pronunciation

The Sound of Emotion 🎭

When native speakers say "I wish" or "If only," they use intonation to show strong emotion or regret.

The voice goes UP on the word "wish", and then drops DOWN heavily on the verb that shows the regret.

Listen & Repeat:

"I wish I HADN'T done that."

"If only I KNEW the answer."
