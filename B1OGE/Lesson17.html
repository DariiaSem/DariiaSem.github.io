02. Journal Extract

Shattering Fitness Dogma

Read the following excerpt from a sports medicine journal. Deduce the mechanics of the highlighted vocabulary and observe the grammatical structures of ability and duration.

The Physiology of Grind • Vol. 14, Issue 2

For decades, amateur athletes have been operating under severe physiological misconceptions. The most persistent is the myth of localized fat loss. Countless individuals have been performing thousands of crunches in hopes of revealing abdominal muscles. However, you are not able to dictate where the body oxidizes lipids; systemic caloric deficit is the only absolute rule.

Secondly, the cultural obsession with "No Pain, No Gain" is inherently flawed. If a patient has been pushing their musculoskeletal system to the point of acute, sharp agony, they are inducing trauma, not hypertrophy. Biomechanically, if joint pain occurs during a lift, the athlete should not be allowed to continue that specific vector of movement.

Finally, consider neurological recovery. You cannot treat the human body like an RPG character that can instantly restore HP with a potion. If a patient has been neglecting their sleep architecture, they won't be able to trigger the parasympathetic response required for tissue repair. Sleep is not a luxury; it is the fundamental biological catalyst for all physical adaptation.

03. Structural Anatomy (A)

Modals: Ability & Permission

Analyze how we express physiological capability versus clinical or social rules.

I. The Matrix of Ability Physical or mental capability
Temporal State Primary Form Clinical Example
Present Can / Am able to "The patient can articulate symptoms clearly, but is unable to stand."
Past Could / Was able to "Before the trauma, he could lift 100kg without joint pain."
Future Will be able to "Following physical therapy, she will be able to resume marathon training."
II. Permission & Protocol Rules, guidelines, and authorization

We use allowed to or permitted to when discussing external rules (hospital protocols, gym rules, medical contraindications).

[Be] + allowed to + Base Verb
  • 🩺 "As a resident, you aren't allowed to discharge this patient without the attending's sign-off."
  • 🏋️ "Members are not permitted to drop heavy weights on the clinic floor."
04. Structural Anatomy (B)

Present Perfect Continuous

Used to emphasize the duration of an ongoing physiological process or action, rather than the finished result or quantity.

The Continuous Formula
Subject + have/has + been + V-ing

Diagnostic Focus 1: Duration

To express how long a symptom or action has been occurring up to the present moment. (Often with for or since).

"The patient has been experiencing tachycardia since 3:00 AM."

Diagnostic Focus 2: Visible Results

An action that recently stopped, but the physiological evidence is currently visible.

"His cortisol levels are heavily elevated. Has he been overtraining?"
