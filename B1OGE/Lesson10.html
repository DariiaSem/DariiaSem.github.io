With improvements in technology and people becoming more environmentally aware, what is the future of motorised transport? ✈️ Dealing with Jetlag

When you travel to the other side of the world, jetlag is a real problem. You find yourself awake in the middle of the night, and you feel like going to bed in the morning, just when everyone around you is getting up. For example, if you leave London at midday and fly to Los Angeles, your body clock takes a few days to change. You might arrive at 3 pm local time, but at midnight LA time, your body still thinks it's 8 am in London!

Task 5: Grammar Theory

Present Tenses & Stative Verbs

Review the rules for Present Simple, Present Continuous, and Stative verbs.

Tense / Concept Usage Example
Present Simple Habits, permanent situations, general truths, timetables. Toby walks to work. Poland is in the EU.
Present Continuous Actions happening now, temporary situations, changing trends. Mike is driving to work at the moment.
Stative Verbs Verbs of state (thinking, sensing, feeling, owning). Never take -ing! I see what you mean. (Not: I am seeing)
Watch out! Some verbs can be both stative and dynamic depending on meaning.
State (Possession): Do you have your passport?
Action (Activity): Are you having lunch?

Task 7: Grammar Theory

Past Tenses & Present Perfect

Review the narrative tenses used for storytelling and reporting past events.

Tense Key Usage Example
Past Simple Single completed actions; main events in a story. Tom and I played chess and he won.
Past Continuous Actions in progress at a past moment; background setting. At 5 o'clock, I was reading my book.
Present Perfect Simple Past actions with a result NOW; life experiences. Have you ever flown in a helicopter?
Past Perfect Simple Action completed before another past action. I had already bought the game when I saw a cheaper one.
Used to Past habits or states that are no longer true. My mother used to play squash.

Task 9: Grammar Theory

Quantifiers

Review how to express quantities using countable and uncountable nouns.

Quantifier Use With Meaning / Example
many / much Countable / Uncountable Usually in negatives/questions. (not many cars / not much money)
a lot of / lots of Both Positive statements. (a lot of awards / lots of luggage)
a few / a little Countable / Uncountable Means 'some' (positive nuance). (a few friends / a little milk)
few / little Countable / Uncountable Means 'not many/much' (negative nuance). (few interests / little info)

Task 11: Grammar Theory

Modals of Obligation & Necessity

What is the level of obligation?
↓
Strong Obligation
↓
must / have to / need to
No Obligation (Optional)
↓
don't have to / needn't
Past Obligation
↓
had to / didn't have to
Watch out! "Mustn't" means it is PROHIBITED (Don't do that!). "Don't have to" means it is OPTIONAL (You can if you want, but it's not required).
