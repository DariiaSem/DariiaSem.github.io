03. Editorial Reading

Busting the "No Pain, No Gain" Myth

Read the following fitness critique. Rely on the context to deduce the meaning of the highlighted words. Pay attention to how the author talks about rules and abilities.

Health & Wellness • By Dr. Elena Vance

For decades, the fitness industry has operated on a massive misconception: if you aren't completely destroyed after a workout, you haven't done enough. This "no pain, no gain" mentality is not only false; it is highly detrimental to your long-term health.

When I was a junior athlete, we weren't allowed to sit down during practice. We were told to push through the pain, ignoring our body’s warning signs. Today, sports science proves that muscle growth doesn't happen in the gym—it happens in bed. If your circadian rhythm is broken and you sleep poorly, your body simply cannot recuperate.

Unfortunately, modern society forces us into a sedentary lifestyle. We sit at desks for nine hours, then go to the gym and attempt extreme exercises we aren't able to perform safely.

To truly optimize your vitality, you don't need to run ultra-marathons. You need consistency, mobility, and above all, permission to rest without feeling guilty.

05. Syntactical Architecture

Modals: Ability & Permission

English is very strict about distinguishing between a general skill, a specific achievement in the past, and rules made by an authority.

Part 1: Expressing Ability

General Ability
(Present / Past)		 can / could "I can run 5km easily."
"When I was 20, I could sleep anywhere."
Specific Achievement
(Past - single event)		 was able to / managed to "Despite my knee pain, I was able to finish the race yesterday."
(Do NOT use 'could' for a specific past success)
Missing Tenses
(Future / Perfect)		 be able to "I haven't been able to sleep lately."
"Will you be able to train tomorrow?"

Part 2: Expressing Permission (Rules)

When we talk about rules made by someone else (the gym, the doctor, the law), we use be allowed to.

Present Permission can / am allowed to "We aren't allowed to wear outdoor shoes in the gym."
Past Permission could / was allowed to "When I was a kid, I wasn't allowed to eat sugar before bed."
💡 Summary Check: If you did something difficult one time yesterday, use managed to or was able to. If it's a rule from the boss, use allowed to.
